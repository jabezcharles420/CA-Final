00. Introduction to Ind AS, Schedule III & Conceptual Framework

Where it fits: This opening chapter sets the coverage boundary of the FR paper — which entities must apply Ind AS and when (roadmap), the presentation format they must follow once covered (Schedule III Division II), and the conceptual foundation (Conceptual Framework) that underlies every Ind AS you will study afterward.

1. Ind AS Applicability Roadmap — Corporate Roadmap (Non-NBFC, Non-Bank, Non-Insurance)

Key rule(s):

  • Phase I — mandatory from FY 2016-17 (i.e., accounting periods beginning on or after 1 April 2016):
  • All companies whose equity and/or debt securities are listed (or in the process of listing) on any stock exchange in India or outside India; and
  • Unlisted companies with net worth ≥ ₹500 crore.
  • Phase II — mandatory from FY 2017-18 (i.e., accounting periods beginning on or after 1 April 2017):
  • All other listed companies (other than those on the SME exchange), irrespective of net worth; and
  • Unlisted companies with net worth ≥ ₹250 crore but < ₹500 crore.
  • Net worth is checked as on 31 March 2014 and thereafter (i.e., 31.3.2014, 31.3.2015, 31.3.2016 …) for corporates; a company crossing the threshold at the end of any accounting year applies Ind AS from the immediately next accounting year, with comparatives.
  • A company not meeting the applicability criteria as on the cut-off date must reassess applicability at every subsequent balance sheet date.
  • Once Ind AS becomes applicable, it always applies — there is no option to revert to AS even if net worth later falls below the threshold.
  • If a company is covered, its Holding, Subsidiary, Joint Venture and Associate companies are also automatically covered under Ind AS, irrespective of their individual net worth/listing status.
  • Ind AS applies to both standalone and consolidated financial statements.
  • Companies listed only on the SME exchange are not required to apply Ind AS.
  • Comparatives for the transition year must also be presented under Ind AS.
  • Example: a company adopts Ind AS from 1 April 20X4 (FY 20X4-X5) → first Ind AS financial statements are for 20X4-X5 with comparative year 20X3-X4 restated under Ind AS, and the date of transition to Ind AS = 1 April 20X3.
  • Companies not covered by the above roadmap continue to apply the existing Accounting Standards (AS).

Concept: The roadmap tells you, given a company's listing status and net worth, from which financial year it must switch from AS to Ind AS — this is almost always tested as a one-mark or theory-based MCQ/short note at the start of an FR paper.

Pitfalls / exam tips:

  • Do not confuse the cut-off net-worth assessment date (31.3.2014 onward, checked every year) with the date from which Ind AS applies (the year following the year in which the threshold is first met).
  • "Once applicable, always applicable" is a favourite trap — students wrongly assume a company can exit Ind AS if net worth falls later.
  • Holding-Subsidiary-Associate-JV "contagion" rule applies regardless of the individual entity's own net worth.

2. Ind AS Applicability Roadmap — NBFCs, Banks & Insurance Companies

Key rule(s) — NBFCs:

  • Phase I — mandatory from FY 2018-19 (accounting periods beginning on or after 1 April 2018): NBFCs (whether listed or unlisted) with net worth ≥ ₹500 crore.
  • Phase II — mandatory from FY 2019-20 (accounting periods beginning on or after 1 April 2019): (a) listed NBFCs with net worth below ₹500 crore; and (b) unlisted NBFCs with net worth ≥ ₹250 crore but < ₹500 crore.
  • NBFCs with net worth below ₹250 crore are neither required, nor permitted, to apply Ind AS (no voluntary adoption).
  • Net worth for NBFCs is checked from 31 March 2016 and thereafter.
  • Voluntary adoption of Ind AS is not allowed for Banks, Insurance companies and NBFCs (unlike other corporates, where early/voluntary adoption is permitted).
  • Urban Co-operative Banks (UCBs) and Regional Rural Banks (RRBs) are not required to apply Ind AS.
  • Holding, Subsidiary, JV and Associate companies of the above Banks/Insurance/NBFCs (other than those already covered under the general corporate roadmap) also apply Ind AS from the same date.
  • Comparatives are presented as per Ind AS in the transition year, as with the corporate roadmap.

Key rule(s) — Banks & Insurance companies:

  • ⚠️ VERIFY — The OCR's roadmap image (with exact dates for Scheduled Commercial Banks/Insurance companies) did not survive extraction. Per RBI/IRDAI public notifications: RBI has deferred implementation of Ind AS by Scheduled Commercial Banks (excluding RRBs) till further notice (last deferral: March 2019, pending legislative amendments), and IRDAI has similarly deferred Ind AS implementation for the insurance sector (pending finalisation of Ind AS 117/IFRS 17). Students should confirm the latest deferral status from the ICAI Study Material / RBI-IRDAI circulars before the exam, as these dates are updated periodically.

Key rule(s) — Mutual Funds:

  • SEBI has mandated that financial statements of Mutual Fund schemes be prepared in accordance with Ind AS, along with prescribed formats for MF scheme financial statements; this requirement is applicable from 1 April 2023.

Concept: Regulated entities (Banks, NBFCs, Insurance) follow a separate roadmap from ordinary corporates because their regulator (RBI/IRDAI) has an overriding say, and — unlike ordinary companies — they cannot adopt Ind AS voluntarily ahead of their mandated date.

Pitfalls / exam tips:

  • Remember the asymmetry: corporates can voluntarily adopt Ind AS early; Banks/Insurance/NBFCs cannot.
  • UCBs and RRBs are explicitly carved out — a commonly tested exception.
  • Keep the NBFC net-worth check date (31.3.2016 onward) distinct from the general corporate check date (31.3.2014 onward).

3. Net Worth — Definition

Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Net Worth = Paid-up Share Capital + All Reserves created out of profits + Securities Premium - Accumulated Losses - Deferred Expenditure - Miscellaneous Expenditure not written off

  • Net worth excludes: reserves created out of revaluation of assets, write-back of depreciation, and the Amalgamation Reserve.
  • (Per Companies Act, 2013, Section 2(57): "Reserves created out of profits" includes the credit/debit balance of the Profit & Loss Account, and figures are taken as per the audited balance sheet.)

Concept: Net worth is the threshold metric used throughout the Ind AS applicability roadmap (corporates, NBFCs) — get this formula exactly right, as an error here cascades into a wrong applicability conclusion.

Pitfalls / exam tips:

  • A very common trap: students add revaluation reserve or amalgamation reserve into net worth — both are specifically excluded.
  • Deferred/miscellaneous expenditure not written off is deducted, not ignored.

4. Schedule III (Division II) — Balance Sheet Format for Ind AS-based Financial Statements

Key rule(s): Division II of Schedule III to the Companies Act, 2013 prescribes the format for companies applying Ind AS. Balance Sheet (Part I) structure:

  • ASSETS
  • (1) Non-current assets: Property, Plant & Equipment; Capital work-in-progress; Investment Property; Goodwill; Other Intangible assets; Intangible assets under development; Biological assets other than bearer plants; Financial Assets (Investments, Trade receivables, Loans, Others); Deferred tax assets (net); Other non-current assets.
  • (2) Current assets: Inventories; Financial Assets (Investments, Trade receivables, Cash and cash equivalents, Bank balances other than cash equivalents, Loans, Others); Current Tax Assets (Net); Other current assets.
  • EQUITY & LIABILITIES
  • Equity: Equity Share capital; Other Equity.
  • (1) Non-current liabilities: Financial Liabilities (Borrowings; Trade Payables — split into (A) dues to micro & small enterprises and (B) dues to other creditors; Other financial liabilities); Provisions; Deferred tax liabilities (Net); Other non-current liabilities.
  • (2) Current liabilities: Financial Liabilities (Borrowings; Trade payables — again split (A) micro/small enterprises and (B) others; Other financial liabilities); Other current liabilities; Provisions; Current Tax Liabilities (Net).
  • Followed by the Statement of Changes in Equity, comprising:
  • A. Equity Share Capital — Balance at the beginning of the reporting period + Changes during the year = Balance at the end of the reporting period.
  • B. Other Equity — reconciliation of each component (Reserves & Surplus, OCI items, etc.).

Concept: This is the mandatory vertical-format Balance Sheet layout for Ind AS companies — the classification is always Non-current vs Current, with Trade Payables mandatorily split by MSME status.

Pitfalls / exam tips:

  • The Trade Payables MSME split — "(A) total outstanding dues of micro enterprises and small enterprises" and "(B) total outstanding dues of creditors other than micro enterprises and small enterprises" — must appear under both non-current and current liabilities where applicable, and is frequently tested in disclosure-based questions.
  • Do not merge "Financial Assets/Liabilities" sub-items into a single line — Schedule III requires each sub-category (Investments, Trade receivables, Loans, etc.) shown separately.

5. Schedule III (Division II) — Statement of Profit and Loss & OCI Classification

Key rule(s): Part II — Statement of Profit and Loss, in sequence:

  • I. Revenue from operations; II. Other Income; III. Total Income (I + II).
  • IV. Expenses: Cost of materials consumed; Purchases of Stock-in-Trade; Changes in inventories of finished goods, Stock-in-Trade and WIP; Employee benefits expense; Finance costs; Depreciation and Amortization Expense; Other expenses; Total expenses.
  • V. Profit/(loss) before exceptional items and tax (III − IV); VI. Exceptional items; VII. Profit/(loss) before tax (V ± VI).
  • VIII. Tax expense: (1) Current tax (2) Deferred tax.
  • IX. Profit/(Loss) for the period from continuing operations (VII − VIII).
  • X. Profit/(Loss) from discontinued operations; XI. Tax expense of discontinued operations; XII. Profit/(loss) from discontinued operations (after tax) (X ± XI).
  • XIII. Profit/(loss) for the period (IX + XII).
  • XIV. Other Comprehensive Income (OCI) — split into (A) items that will not be reclassified to P&L and (B) items that will be reclassified to P&L, each net of related income tax.
  • XV. Total Comprehensive Income for the period = Profit/(loss) for the period + OCI for the period.
  • XVI–XVIII. Earnings per equity share (Basic & Diluted) — shown separately for continuing operations, discontinued operations, and continuing & discontinued operations combined.

OCI classification (mandatory memorised list):

A. Not reclassified to P&LB. Reclassified to P&L
Changes in revaluation surplusExchange differences on translating foreign operations
Re-measurements of defined benefit plansDebt instruments through OCI
Equity instruments through OCIEffective portion of gains/losses on cash flow hedging instruments
FV changes relating to own credit risk of financial liabilities designated at FVTPLShare of OCI of Associates/JVs, to the extent reclassifiable to P&L
Share of OCI of Associates/JVs, to the extent not reclassifiable to P&LOthers (specify nature)
Others (specify nature)

Concept: This is the mandatory line-by-line format for the Statement of P&L under Ind AS Schedule III, and the A/B split of OCI items ties directly into individual standards (Ind AS 16 revaluation, Ind AS 19 remeasurements, Ind AS 109 equity/debt instruments through OCI, Ind AS 21 translation, Ind AS 109 cash flow hedges).

Pitfalls / exam tips:

  • Memorise which OCI item goes to bucket A (never reclassified) vs bucket B (reclassified on derecognition/disposal) — this is a recurring theory/MCQ area, and mixing up "equity instruments through OCI" (bucket A, never recycled) with "debt instruments through OCI" (bucket B, recycled) is the single most common error.
  • EPS must be disclosed for three separate categories (continuing; discontinued; combined) — each with Basic and Diluted — six EPS figures in total when discontinued operations exist.

6. Rounding Off of Figures in Financial Statements

Key rule(s)/threshold:

TurnoverRounding off permitted
Less than ₹100 croreNearest hundreds, thousands, lakhs or millions, or decimals thereof
₹100 crore or moreNearest lakhs, millions or crores, or decimals thereof
  • Once a unit of measurement is selected, it must be used uniformly throughout the financial statements (no mixing units across line items or years).

Concept: Schedule III fixes the rounding-off precision a company may use based on its turnover, ensuring comparability between statements of similarly-sized entities.

Pitfalls / exam tips:

  • The ₹100 crore turnover threshold is on turnover, not net worth or total assets — do not confuse this with the Ind AS applicability net-worth thresholds (₹250/₹500 crore) covered above.
  • A company cannot round to "crores" if its turnover is below ₹100 crore.

7. Conceptual Framework for Financial Reporting under Ind AS

Key concept: The Conceptual Framework sets out the underlying concepts for general purpose financial reporting — objective of financial statements, qualitative characteristics (relevance, faithful representation, comparability, verifiability, timeliness, understandability), the elements of financial statements (asset, liability, equity, income, expense), recognition and derecognition criteria, and measurement bases (historical cost, fair value, value in use, current cost). It is not itself an Ind AS and does not override any specific standard in case of conflict.

Pitfalls / exam tips:

  • The source material for this chapter directs students to the ICAI Study Material for detailed coverage of the Conceptual Framework, as it is foundational/conceptual in nature rather than formula-driven — this guide accordingly keeps this section brief; revise the qualitative characteristics and the definitions of the five elements directly from the ICAI Study Material / Framework document before the exam.
  • Remember the hierarchy: if a specific Ind AS conflicts with the Conceptual Framework, the specific Ind AS prevails.

01. Ind AS 1 - Presentation of Financial Statements

Where it fits: Ind AS 1 is the "umbrella" standard prescribing the components, structure, minimum content, and general features (going concern, accrual, materiality, offsetting, consistency, comparatives) that every complete set of Ind AS financial statements must satisfy — the foundation on which every other Ind AS in this guide sits.

Scope & Interaction with Companies Act / Schedule III

  • Concept: Ind AS 1 applies to all general-purpose financial statements prepared in accordance with Ind AS. NPOs (e.g., Section 8 companies) applying this Standard may need to amend the descriptions used for particular line items in the FS.
  • Key rule (hierarchy of conflict):
  • Conflict between the Companies Act, 2013 and Ind AS → Companies Act prevails.
  • Conflict between Schedule III and Ind AS → Ind AS prevails.

Pitfalls / exam tips:

  • This is a favourite one-line MCQ/theory trap — students often reverse the two rules; remember "Act beats Ind AS, but Ind AS beats Schedule III."

Complete Set of Financial Statements

  • Key rule — a complete set of FS comprises:
  1. Balance Sheet as at the end of the period
  2. Statement of Profit and Loss for the period (in two sections: Profit or Loss, and Other Comprehensive Income)
  3. Statement of Changes in Equity (SOCIE) for the period
  4. Statement of Cash Flows for the period
  5. Notes, comprising significant accounting policies and other explanatory information
  6. Comparative information in respect of the preceding period
  7. A Balance Sheet as at the beginning of the preceding period — only when note (a) below applies

Concept: Every Ind AS financial statement set must contain all these components together (not a subset) for a period to be considered a complete presentation.

Pitfalls / exam tips:

  • The third Balance Sheet (opening BS of the preceding period) is triggered only when the entity: (i) applies an accounting policy retrospectively, or (ii) makes a retrospective restatement of items in its FS, or (iii) reclassifies items in its FS.
  • An entity is not required to present the related notes to this third (opening) Balance Sheet.

Balance Sheet — Current vs Non-Current Classification

  • Key rule — an asset is classified as Current if ANY ONE of the following is met:
  • Expected to be realised, or intended to be sold or consumed, in the normal operating cycle; OR
  • Expected to be realised within 12 months after the reporting period; OR
  • Held primarily for the purpose of trading; OR
  • It is Cash or Cash Equivalent (as per Ind AS 7) unless restricted from being exchanged or used to settle a liability for at least 12 months from the reporting date.
  • All other assets are classified as Non-Current.
  • Key rule — a liability is classified as Current if ANY ONE of the following is met:
  • Expected to be settled in the normal operating cycle; OR
  • Due to be settled within 12 months after the reporting period; OR
  • Held primarily for the purpose of trading; OR
  • The entity does not have an unconditional right to defer settlement of the liability for at least 12 months after the reporting period.
  • All other liabilities are classified as Non-Current.
  • Operating cycle:

Operating Cycle = Time from acquisition of assets for processing → their realisation in cash or cash equivalents

  • Where the normal operating cycle is not clearly identifiable, it is assumed to be 12 months.

Concept: Schedule III requires a classified Balance Sheet separating Current and Non-Current Assets/Liabilities; Ind AS 1 supplies the tests used to make that classification.

Pitfalls / exam tips:

  • All four tests are independent — meeting any one is sufficient; a common trap is assuming all conditions must be met simultaneously.
  • "Held for trading" assets/liabilities are always current, irrespective of the 12-month or operating-cycle tests.

Breach of Long-Term Loan Arrangement (Carve-out from IAS 1)

  • Key rule — where a breach of a material provision of a long-term loan arrangement occurs on or before the reporting date, giving the lender the right to demand repayment on demand:
  • Case A: If the lender's grace period to rectify the breach is less than 12 months from the end of the reporting period → classify the loan as Current Liability.
  • Case B: If the lender's grace period to rectify the breach is 12 months or more from the end of the reporting period → classify the loan as Non-Current Liability.

Concept: This is a specific Ind AS 1 carve-out from IAS 1 designed to give preparers relief where a breach is technical/temporary and a genuine grace period exists.

Pitfalls / exam tips:

  • If the compliance date is extended before the breach occurs, the loan retains its original (non-current) classification, even if the extended period is less than 12 months from the reporting date.
  • Under the Ind AS 1 carve-out (as applicable up to FY 2025-26, with disclosure), if the lender agrees after the reporting period but before approval of the FS not to demand payment as a consequence of the breach, the loan retains its original (non-current) classification — this is treated as an adjusting event under Ind AS, unlike IAS 1 where such a post-year-end waiver does not avoid current classification.
  • ⚠️ VERIFY — Recent amendment (topical for 2026 attempts): MCA's Companies (Indian Accounting Standards) Second Amendment Rules, 2025 (notified 19 Aug 2025) apply a temporary Indian carve-out for FY 2025-26 (post-balance-sheet waiver still avoids current classification, subject to disclosure), but remove this carve-out entirely from FY 2026-27 onwards, aligning fully with IAS 1 (only rights existing at the reporting date determine classification). Students should confirm with the latest ICAI Study Material/RTP for the exact attempt-period applicability before relying on this carve-out in an answer.
  • This carve-out area is discussed further under Ind AS 10 (Events after the Reporting Period) for the "rectification/waiver after Balance Sheet date" scenario.

Statement of Profit & Loss

  • Key rule — the Statement of P&L has two sections:
  • Profit or Loss for the period; and
  • Other Comprehensive Income (OCI) for the period.

Total Comprehensive Income = Profit or Loss for the period + Other Comprehensive Income for the period

  • Key rule — when an entity is required to prepare Consolidated FS (CFS), it shall separately present, attributable to Non-Controlling Interests (NCI) and Owners of the Parent:
  • (a) Profit or Loss for the period; and
  • (b) Total Comprehensive Income for the period.

Concept: Total Comprehensive Income captures both realised P&L items and unrealised OCI items (e.g., revaluation surplus, certain FVOCI gains/losses) in one aggregate figure.

Pitfalls / exam tips:

  • Do not forget the NCI vs. Owners-of-parent split is required for both P&L and Total Comprehensive Income when CFS is prepared — a common omission in consolidation answers.

Statement of Changes in Equity (SOCIE)

  • Key rule — SOCIE reconciles the opening and closing balance of each component of equity, separately disclosing changes from:
  • Profit or Loss;
  • Each item of Other Comprehensive Income;
  • Effects of retrospective application or retrospective restatement recognised per Ind AS 8;
  • Transactions with owners in their capacity as owners (contributions by and distributions to owners), shown separately; and
  • Any item recognised directly in equity (e.g., amount recognised directly in equity as capital reserve under Ind AS 103).

Concept: SOCIE is the bridge statement showing why each equity line item moved between two Balance Sheet dates.

Statement of Cash Flows & Notes

  • Key rule: The Statement of Cash Flows is prepared in accordance with Ind AS 7.
  • Key rule — Notes shall disclose:
  • A statement of compliance with Ind AS;
  • Information about the basis of preparation of FS and significant accounting policies applied;
  • Supporting information for items presented in the Balance Sheet, P&L, SOCIE and Cash Flows, in the order in which each statement and line item is presented; and
  • Other disclosures required by Ind AS.

General Features — True & Fair View and Compliance

  • Key rule: Compliance with applicable Ind AS is presumed, in virtually all circumstances, to result in FS that present a true and fair view.
  • Key rule: An entity whose FS comply with Ind AS shall make an explicit and unreserved statement of such compliance in the notes.
  • Key rule: Disclosure of an inappropriate accounting policy, or notes/explanatory material, does not rectify the resultant misstatement — i.e., disclosure is not a remedy for wrong accounting.

Concept: Even if the auditor disagrees with the applicability of an Ind AS and qualifies the audit report, the financial statements are still treated as being Ind AS compliant (compliance is an assertion by management, not conditional on auditor agreement).

Pitfalls / exam tips:

  • A classic trick question: "auditor qualified the report, so are FS still Ind AS compliant?" — Yes, FS compliance is unaffected by an auditor's disagreement/qualification.

Going Concern

  • Key rule: FS shall be prepared on a going concern basis unless management either intends to liquidate the entity or to cease trading, or has no realistic alternative but to do so.
  • Key rule: If FS are not prepared on a going concern basis, disclose: (i) the basis of preparation used, and (ii) the reason why the entity is not regarded as a going concern.
  • Key rule: If management is aware of material uncertainties regarding the entity's ability to continue as a going concern, those uncertainties must be disclosed.

Accrual Basis of Accounting

  • Key rule: FS shall be prepared using the accrual basis of accounting, except for cash flow information (which is inherently a cash-basis statement).

Materiality & Aggregation

  • Key rule (materiality definition): Information is material if omitting, misstating, or obscuring it could reasonably be expected to influence decisions that primary users of general-purpose FS make on the basis of those financial statements.
  • Key rule: Materiality depends on the nature or magnitude of information, or both, assessed individually or in combination with other information, in the context of the FS as a whole.
  • Key rule: Each material class of similar items must be presented separately; items of a dissimilar nature or function must be presented separately unless immaterial — except that even immaterial items must be presented separately where required by law.

Pitfalls / exam tips:

  • Materiality is a matter of professional judgment — no single quantitative threshold is prescribed by Ind AS 1 itself (unlike some Schedule III line-item disclosure thresholds elsewhere).

Offsetting

  • Key rule: An entity shall not offset Assets and Liabilities, or Income and Expenses, unless required or permitted by an Ind AS.
  • Key rule: Gains and losses arising from a group of similar transactions (e.g., foreign exchange gains/losses, gains/losses on financial instruments held for trading) are presented on a net basis; however, if such gains and losses are material, they are presented separately.

Pitfalls / exam tips:

  • Measuring assets net of valuation allowances (e.g., obsolescence allowance on inventories, doubtful debt allowance on receivables) is not offsetting — a frequently tested distinction.

Consistency

  • Key rule: An entity shall retain the presentation and classification of items in the FS from one period to the next unless:
  • Another presentation or classification would be more appropriate (following a significant change in the nature of operations or a review of FS); or
  • An Ind AS requires a change in presentation.
  • Key rule: When presentation/classification changes, the entity reclassifies its comparative amounts, unless reclassification is impracticable — in which case it discloses the reason for not reclassifying and the nature of the adjustments that would have been made.

Frequency of Reporting

  • Key rule: A complete set of FS (including comparative information) shall be presented at least annually.
  • Key rule: When the reporting period changes (longer/shorter than one year), the entity discloses: (i) the reason for using a longer or shorter period, and (ii) the fact that the amounts presented are not entirely comparable.

Comparative Information

  • Key rule (minimum comparative information): Comparative information for the preceding period is required for all amounts reported in the current period's FS; comparative narrative/descriptive information is included if relevant to understanding the current period's FS.
  • i.e., minimum 2 Balance Sheets, 2 P&L Statements, 2 Statements of Cash Flows, 2 SOCIEs, and related notes.
  • Key rule (retrospective application/restatement/reclassification): In such cases, the entity presents a minimum of 3 Balance Sheets — end of current period, end of previous period, and beginning of the earliest comparative period presented — plus 2 of each of the other statements, and related notes.
  • Key rule (additional comparative information): An entity may voluntarily present comparative information for more than one year, and may even present, say, 2 years of comparatives for the Statement of P&L while presenting only 1 year of comparatives for other statements (or vice versa), as long as it is consistent with the requirements above as a minimum.

Pitfalls / exam tips:

  • The "3 Balance Sheets, 2 of everything else" rule is one of the most frequently tested numerical/theory points in this chapter — do not confuse it with the "complete set of FS" 3rd-BS trigger condition (they are the same trigger, described from two angles).

02. Ind AS 2 & Ind AS 7 - Inventories and Statement of Cash Flows

Where it fits: Ind AS 2 governs how inventories are measured and expensed (a Balance Sheet/P&L standard); Ind AS 7 governs how the same period's transactions are re-presented as cash movements (operating/investing/financing) to reconcile opening and closing cash & cash equivalents — together they are a staple of both objective-type and practical questions in CA Final FR.

PART A: IND AS 2 — INVENTORIES

Definitions & Scope

  • Definitions:
TermMeaning
InventoriesAssets held for sale in the ordinary course of business (finished goods); in the process of production for such sale (WIP); or in the form of materials/supplies to be consumed in production or rendering services (raw material, stores)
Net Realisable Value (NRV)Estimated selling price in the ordinary course of business, less estimated costs of completion and estimated costs necessary to make the sale — an entity-specific value
Fair ValueThe price that would be received to sell an asset in an orderly transaction between market participants — a market-based measure
  • Concept: Ind AS 2 does NOT apply to: financial instruments; biological assets related to agricultural activity and agricultural produce at point of harvest (Ind AS 41, up to that point); and WIP of construction contracts including directly related service contracts (Ind AS 115).

Measurement — Lower of Cost and NRV

  • Key rule:

Inventory Value = min(Cost, Net Realisable Value)

  • The comparison of cost and NRV should be made item by item, or by group of similar/related items — never on an aggregate/total inventory basis.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • NRV ≠ Fair value less costs to sell. Example: entity can sell in the open market @ ₹10/unit (this is the market/fair-value benchmark) but has a firm order to sell @ ₹11/unit with incremental selling cost of ₹0.50/unit → Fair Value = ₹10, but NRV = ₹10.50 (order-linked, entity-specific).
  • Never write down inventory on a "total inventory" basis when items are dissimilar — this understates losses/overstates gains by netting off unrelated items.

Cost of Inventories — Components

  • Key rule (cost = sum of three elements):

Cost of Inventories = Cost of Purchase + Cost of Conversion + Other Costs to bring to present location & condition

  • Cost of purchase = purchase price + import duties/other non-recoverable taxes + freight, handling & other directly attributable costs, less trade discounts, rebates and similar items.
  • Cost of conversion = direct labour/costs directly related to units produced + a systematic allocation of fixed and variable production overheads incurred in converting materials into finished goods.
  • Fixed production overhead is allocated to units of production based on the normal capacity of production facilities; unallocated overhead (due to abnormally low production) is expensed in the period it is incurred, not deferred into inventory cost.
  • Concept: Only costs incurred in bringing inventories to their present location and condition are inventoriable; everything else is a period expense.
  • Definitions:
TermMeaning
Cost of purchasePurchase price + duties/taxes + directly attributable acquisition costs − discounts/rebates
Cost of conversionDirect costs + systematically allocated fixed & variable production overheads
Normal capacityExpected production over a number of periods/seasons under normal circumstances, factoring planned maintenance

Exclusions from Cost of Inventories

  • Key rule — the following are excluded and expensed as incurred, NOT capitalised into inventory:
  • Abnormal amounts of wasted material, labour, or other production costs.
  • Storage costs — unless storage is a necessary part of the production process before a further production stage (e.g., storage of wine, pickles during maturing), in which case it IS included in cost.
  • Administrative overheads (not related to bringing inventories to present location/condition).
  • Selling and distribution costs.
  • Interest/borrowing costs — unless the inventory is a qualifying asset under Ind AS 23 (e.g., inventory that necessarily takes a substantial period to get ready for sale).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: the storage-cost exception is a favourite trick — always ask "is this storage part of the production process itself?" before excluding it.

Allocation of Cost — Joint Products & By-Products

  • Key rule — Joint products: when conversion costs of each product are not separately identifiable, allocate on a rational and consistent basis, typically the relative sales value of each product — either at the point they become separately identifiable, or at completion of production.
  • Key rule — By-products: most by-products are immaterial, so they are measured at NRV, and this NRV is deducted from the cost of conversion of the main product(s); the net cost of conversion (cost of conversion − NRV of by-product) is then allocated among the main products.
  • Concept: joint products = simultaneous production of two+ significant products from one process; by-product = a minor product incidental to the main product.

Cost of Agricultural Produce Harvested from Biological Assets

  • Key rule:

Initial Cost of Harvested Produce (for Ind AS 2) = Fair Value - Costs to Sell (at point of harvest)

  • Concept: Ind AS 41 governs the biological asset and the produce up to the point of harvest; the FVLCTS determined under Ind AS 41 at that point of harvest becomes the "cost" figure that Ind AS 2 then carries forward (subsequently measured at lower of this cost and NRV, like any other inventory).

Cost Formulas

  • Key rule:
  • Items not ordinarily interchangeable, and goods/services produced/segregated for specific projects → Specific identification of individual costs.
  • Items that are ordinarily interchangeable → FIFO or Weighted Average Cost formula (LIFO is not permitted under Ind AS 2).

Weighted Average Cost = Cost of opening inventory + Cost of purchases/production during the periodUnits of opening inventory + Units purchased/produced during the period

  • An entity must use the same cost formula for all inventories of a similar nature and use; a different formula may be justified for inventories with a different nature or use.
  • Standard cost method: takes into account normal levels of material/supplies use, labour efficiency and capacity utilisation; must be regularly reviewed and revised. Acceptable only if results approximate actual cost.
  • Retail method (used where it is impracticable to use other costing methods, e.g. rapidly changing retail items with similar margins):

Cost of Inventory = Selling Price of Inventory - Appropriate % of Gross Margin

  • Pitfalls / exam tips: the average % gross margin used in the retail method must take account of markdowns to below original selling price; each retail department may need its own average percentage.

Net Realisable Value — Detailed Estimation

  • Key rule (Finished Goods):

NRVFG = Estimated Selling Price (ordinary course) - Estimated Costs to Make the Sale

  • Key rule (Work-in-Progress):

NRVWIP = Estimated Selling Price - Estimated Cost of Completion - Estimated Costs to Make the Sale

  • Key rule (Raw Materials to be used in Finished Goods):
  • Do NOT write raw materials down below cost if the finished products in which they will be incorporated are expected to be sold at or above cost.
  • If a decline in the price of raw materials indicates that the cost of the finished product will exceed its NRV, the raw material is written down to NRV — replacement cost is usually the best available measure of that NRV.
  • Concept — most reliable evidence / subsequent events: estimates of NRV must use the most reliable evidence available at the time of estimation and must factor in price/cost fluctuations occurring after the balance sheet date, to the extent those events confirm conditions existing at the period end.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: classic exam trap — if goods are actually sold after the balance sheet date (but before approval of financial statements) at a price different from the estimated NRV, use the actual post-year-end realised price as NRV for the lower-of-cost-or-NRV comparison (e.g., 100 units, estimated NRV ₹60, actually sold post-year-end at ₹50 → use ₹50 as NRV).

Writing Down to NRV & Reversal of Write-Downs

  • Key rule: when the circumstances that previously caused inventories to be written down below cost no longer exist (e.g., selling price has increased), or when there is clear evidence of an increase in NRV, the write-down is reversed — but the reversal is limited to the amount of the original write-down, so that the new carrying amount is the lower of cost and the revised NRV.
  • Concept: the reversed amount is recognised as a reduction in the amount of inventories recognised as an expense in the period the reversal occurs (not as "other income").

Derecognition of Inventory

  • Key rule: when inventories are sold, their carrying amount is recognised as an expense (cost of goods sold) in the period in which the related revenue is recognised.
  • Any write-down to NRV, and all losses of inventories, are recognised as an expense in the period the write-down or loss occurs.

PART B: IND AS 7 — STATEMENT OF CASH FLOWS

Meaning & Definitions

  • Concept: the Cash Flow Statement explains how cash & cash equivalents (CCE) moved during the period — opening CCE + cash generated − cash applied = closing CCE — classified under Operating, Investing and Financing activities.
  • Definitions:
TermMeaning
CashCash on hand and demand deposits
Cash Equivalents (CCE)Short-term, highly liquid investments readily convertible to known amounts of cash, subject to insignificant risk of changes in value — normally an investment qualifies only if it has a maturity of 3 months or less from the date of acquisition; equity investments are excluded (normally) from cash equivalents
Bank overdraft (as CCE)Bank borrowings are generally financing activities; but overdrafts repayable on demand that form an integral part of the entity's cash management are included as a component of CCE
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: on the Balance Sheet, a bank overdraft is still classified as a financial liability — its inclusion in CCE for the cash flow statement does NOT mean it should be netted off against the Balance Sheet cash balance.

Classification of Cash Flows

  • Key rule — three categories:
  • Operating activities: cash flows from the principal revenue-producing activities — the residual after investing and financing are identified; derived from transactions entering into determination of profit or loss.
  • Investing activities: acquisition and disposal of long-term assets and other investments not included in cash equivalents; only expenditure resulting in a recognised asset qualifies.
  • Financing activities: activities resulting in changes in the size and composition of the contributed equity and borrowings of the entity (raising and repaying funds; cost of capital).
  • Concept — classification examples:
ItemClassification
Sale of goods/services, receipts from royalties/fees/commissionOperating
Payments to suppliers/employees; income tax paid (unless specifically identifiable with investing/financing)Operating
Purchase/sale of dealing or trading securities; loans made by financial institutionsOperating (their core business)
Purchase/sale of PPE, intangibles, other long-term assetsInvesting
Purchase/sale of equity/debt instruments (not CCE, not held for trading)Investing
Loans/advances given and their repayment (other than by financial institutions)Investing
Proceeds from issuing shares/equity instruments, debentures, bonds, borrowingsFinancing
Repayments of borrowings; payments to redeem shares; lessee's repayment of finance lease liabilityFinancing
Profit/loss on sale of an asset (P&L item)The related cash flow itself is classified as Investing (not adjusted as operating merely because it hit P&L)
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: properties built specifically to let out and later sold — both the construction payments and the subsequent rental receipts/sale proceeds are operating, not investing, because letting is the entity's principal revenue-producing activity in that fact pattern.

Reporting of Cash Flows from Operating Activities

  • Key rule — Direct Method: discloses major classes of gross cash receipts and gross cash payments (top-down: starts from cash revenue/receipts, deducts cash expenses, ignoring non-cash items).
  • Key rule — Indirect Method: starts from profit/loss after tax (bottom-up) and adjusts for: non-cash items (depreciation, provisions), gains/losses on investing/financing items, and working-capital movements (changes in receivables, payables, inventory).
  • Concept: the two methods must arrive at the same figure for net cash flow from operating activities — only the presentation differs. Ind AS 7 encourages (does not mandate) the direct method.

Reporting on Gross vs Net Basis

  • Key rule: Ind AS 7 requires investing and financing cash flows to be reported gross (receipts and payments shown separately) — as the default rule, unless permitted to be netted.
  • Exceptions permitting net reporting (quick, high-turnover, short-maturity, or pass-through items):
  • Acceptance and repayment of demand deposits of a bank
  • Funds held for customers by an investment entity
  • Rents collected on behalf of, and paid over to, property owners
  • Items where turnover is quick, amounts are large, and maturities are short
  • Principal amounts of credit-card customer receivables
  • Purchase and sale of investments
  • Receipts/payments for deposits with a fixed maturity date
  • Cash advances/loans made to customers, and their repayment
  • Concept: presentation of investing and financing cash flows is identical whether the operating section uses the direct or indirect method.

Foreign Currency Cash Flows

  • Key rule: cash flows from foreign-currency transactions are recorded in the functional currency by applying the exchange rate at the date of the cash flow; cash flows of a foreign subsidiary are translated at the exchange rates at the dates of the cash flows.
  • Key rule: unrealised gains/losses from exchange rate changes are not cash flows; however, the effect of exchange rate changes on CCE held or due in foreign currency is reported separately in the statement (as a reconciling item between opening and closing CCE), not within operating/investing/financing.
  • Concept — indirect method mechanics: exchange differences arising on translation, at balance sheet date, of monetary items forming part of operating activities require no separate adjustment in the profit-to-operating-cash-flow reconciliation, because the movement is already captured through the corresponding change in the underlying asset/liability (e.g., inventory/payables) in working-capital adjustments.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: worked example — goods imported on credit, recorded at ₹7,00,000 (EUR 10,000 @ ₹70), rate moves to ₹65 by year-end giving an unrealised gain of ₹50,000; if inventory is still unsold, the reconciliation nets to zero net operating cash flow for this item: Profit +₹50,000, less increase in inventory (₹7,00,000), add increase in payables ₹6,50,000 = Nil.

Interest, Dividends & Taxes

  • Key rule — Interest & dividends (each disclosed separately, consistently period to period):
ItemFinancing companyOther (non-financing) company
Interest paidOperating (core business)Financing
Interest & dividends receivedOperating (core business)Investing
Dividends paidFinancingFinancing
  • Key rule — Taxes on income: cash flows from income tax are classified as operating, unless they can be specifically identified with financing or investing activities, in which case classify as investing/financing accordingly.

Investments in Subsidiaries, Associates & Joint Ventures

  • Key rule: where an investee (subsidiary/associate/JV) is accounted for using the equity or cost method, the investor restricts its cash flow statement to cash flows between itself and the investee only (e.g., dividends received, advances) — not the investee's own underlying cash flows.

Changes in Ownership Interest in Subsidiaries

  • Key rule — obtaining/losing control: aggregate cash flows from obtaining or losing control of subsidiaries/businesses are presented separately, classified as Investing activities, with disclosure of: total consideration paid/received; portion of consideration in CCE; CCE of the subsidiary over which control is obtained/lost; and non-CCE assets/liabilities of the subsidiary, by major category.
  • Key rule — no loss of control: cash flows from changes in ownership interest in a subsidiary that do not result in a loss of control are classified as Financing activities — unless the subsidiary is held by an investment entity and required to be measured at FVTPL.

Non-Cash Transactions

  • Key rule: investing and financing transactions that do not require the use of cash/CCE are excluded from the cash flow statement (but disclosed elsewhere in the financial statements so users can understand their effect).
  • Examples: acquisition of assets by assuming directly related liabilities, or by finance lease; acquisition of an entity by an equity issue; conversion of debt to equity.

Disclosures

  • Key rule: an entity shall disclose the components of CCE and present a reconciliation of the amounts in its cash flow statement with the equivalent items reported in the Balance Sheet.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: remember — bank overdraft sits within CCE for Ind AS 7 purposes (if repayable on demand and part of cash management) but remains a financial liability on the face of the Balance Sheet; the reconciliation note must bridge this difference, not eliminate it.

03. Ind AS 8 & Ind AS 10 - Accounting Policies, Estimates, Errors and Events After the Reporting Period

Where it fits: Ind AS 8 tells you how to select/change accounting policies, treat changes in estimates, and correct prior period errors; Ind AS 10 tells you how to treat events occurring between the balance sheet date and the date the FS are approved for issue. Together they govern the "clean-up" adjustments examiners love to test through numerical journal/restatement problems.

1. Accounting Policies — Meaning, Selection & Hierarchy

  • Concept: Accounting policies are the specific principles, bases, conventions, rules and practices applied by an entity in preparing and presenting financial statements (e.g., cash vs accrual basis, cost vs revaluation model for PPE, FIFO vs weighted average for inventory).
  • Rule — hierarchy for developing a policy when no Ind AS specifically applies (management must use judgement to give information that is Relevant to decision-making AND Reliable), applying the following sources in descending order:
  1. Requirements/guidance in any other Ind AS dealing with similar and related issues;
  2. Definitions, recognition and measurement concepts in the Framework for financial reporting under Ind AS;
  3. Most recent pronouncements of the IASB;
  4. Pronouncements of other standard-setting bodies with a similar conceptual framework;
  5. Accepted accounting literature and industry practices — provided these do not conflict with (1)–(4).
  • Concept — consistency: An entity applies its policies consistently for similar transactions across categories, unless an Ind AS specifically requires/permits categorisation — then a distinct (but internally consistent) policy may be used per category (e.g., Revaluation model only for land, Cost model for other PPE classes; or different cost formulas — FIFO/Weighted average — for different classes of inventory, never within the same class).

Pitfalls / exam tips

  • Memorise the hierarchy strictly in the given descending order — a common trap is to reverse it (literature/industry practice is the last resort, not the first).
  • "Different accounting policy for a different category of the same class" is wrong — consistency is required within a category/class.

Definitions

TermMeaning
Accounting PoliciesSpecific principles, bases, conventions, rules and practices applied in preparing and presenting FS
FrameworkThe Ind AS conceptual framework providing general recognition/measurement principles

2. Change in Accounting Policy

  • Rule — a policy can be changed only if:
  1. Required by an Ind AS; OR
  2. The change results in the FS providing more relevant and reliable information about the effects of transactions/events on the entity's financial position, performance or cash flows.
  • NOT a change in accounting policy:
  • Applying a policy to transactions that differ in substance from those occurring previously; and
  • Applying a new policy to transactions that did not occur previously or were immaterial (e.g., moving a PPE into Investment Property under Ind AS 40, which mandates cost model only, is not a policy change — it's a reclassification consequence).
  • Early adoption of a new/amended Ind AS is not a voluntary change in accounting policy.
  • Rule: Where, in absence of a specific Ind AS, a policy was based on IFRS/other pronouncements, and that source is later amended and the entity follows the amendment voluntarily, it is treated as a voluntary change and must be applied retrospectively.

Concept: A genuine change in policy (voluntary or mandated) is generally applied retrospectively — as if the new policy had always applied — subject to specific transitional provisions in the Ind AS requiring the change, or impracticability.

Retrospective application mechanics

  • Comparative information for all prior periods presented is restated for the effect of the change.
  • The cumulative adjustment relating to periods before those presented is made to the opening balance of each affected component of equity of the earliest period presented.
  • Per Ind AS 1, whenever retrospective application/restatement is required, the entity must present a third balance sheet — i.e., the Balance Sheet as at the beginning of the preceding (previous) period, in addition to the current and previous year balance sheets.
  • Restating prior-year figures does not mean the already-approved/issued FS of those years are withdrawn or reopened — only the comparatives shown in the current FS are restated.

Rule — Impracticability limitation: If retrospective application is required but it is impracticable to determine the period-specific or cumulative effect for a prior period, the entity applies the new policy prospectively from the earliest date for which retrospective application is practicable.

  • Impracticable = the entity cannot apply the requirement after making every reasonable effort, e.g.:

a) the retrospective effects are not determinable; or b) it would require assumptions about what management's intent/estimates would have been in that past period, which cannot be reconstructed objectively from evidence available at that time.

Disclosures on change in accounting policy

  • Nature of the change;
  • Reasons for the change;
  • Effect of the change on the current and prior periods presented;
  • If retrospective application is impracticable, the reason;
  • For an issued-but-not-yet-effective new Ind AS not yet applied: disclose that fact plus known/reasonably estimable information relevant to assessing the possible impact of its future application.

Pitfalls / exam tips

  • "Third balance sheet" is a very common numerical/theory MCQ — it is required specifically because of retrospective restatement (also applies for reclassification/restatement of errors), per Ind AS 1.
  • Distinguish "change in policy" (retrospective, prior period comparatives restated) from "change in estimate" (prospective, see below) — this distinction is the examiner's favourite trap.

3. Accounting Estimates

  • Concept: An accounting estimate is a monetary amount in the FS that is subject to measurement uncertainty, requiring judgement based on the latest available and reliable information (not the correction of an error). Common examples: allowance for bad/doubtful debts, inventory obsolescence, fair value of financial assets/liabilities, useful life/residual value/depreciation method of PPE, warranty obligations.
  • Reasons for change in estimate: new information, more experience, new developments.
  • Rule — accounting treatment: A change in accounting estimate is recognised prospectively — i.e., applied to transactions from the date of change onward; it is not applied by restating prior periods.
  • The change may affect only the current period's profit or loss, or both current and future periods' P&L (e.g., a change in useful life of an asset changes depreciation charge for current and remaining future years, not the past).
  • If a change affects both current and future periods, the effect on future periods is recognised in those future periods as they occur (not upfront).

Disclosures on change in accounting estimate

  • Nature of the change;
  • Amount of the change (or a statement that determining the amount is impracticable);
  • Effect of the change on current and future periods;
  • If the effect on future periods cannot be estimated, that fact must be disclosed.

Pitfalls / exam tips

  • Change in method of depreciation (e.g., SLM to WDV) is treated as a change in accounting estimate under Ind AS (unlike the older AS 6 position which is now replaced) — apply prospectively.
  • If it is difficult to distinguish a change in accounting policy from a change in accounting estimate, treat it as a change in accounting estimate (Ind AS 8 default rule).

4. Errors (Prior Period Errors)

  • Concept: Prior period errors are omissions from, and misstatements in, the entity's FS for one or more prior periods arising from a failure to use, or misuse of, reliable information that was available when those FS were approved for issue and could reasonably be expected to have been obtained and taken into account. Includes mathematical mistakes, mistakes in applying accounting policies, oversights/misinterpretations of facts, and fraud.
  • Materiality note: Errors need not be individually material — several individually immaterial errors may, in aggregate, materially misstate the FS (classic ICAI example: depreciation understated by 0.30% of sales, warranty provision understated by 0.15% of sales, bad debts allowance understated by 0.25% of sales — individually immaterial, collectively a material prior period error).

Rule — treatment of a material prior period error

  • Error relates to the comparative prior period presented: Unless impracticable, correct it retrospectively by restating the comparative amounts for that prior period.
  • Error relates to a period before the earliest comparative period presented: Unless impracticable, correct it retrospectively by restating the opening balances of assets, liabilities and equity for the earliest prior period presented.
  • Per Ind AS 1, whenever retrospective correction is required, the entity must present the opening Balance Sheet of the previous year (third balance sheet), same as for policy changes.
  • If retrospective restatement is impracticable for a period, apply the same impracticability approach as for changes in accounting policy (restate prospectively from the earliest practicable date).
  • Use the term "Restated" against the Balance Sheet and Statement of P&L line items after correcting prior period errors.

Disclosures on correction of errors

  • Nature of the error;
  • Amount of the correction and its effect (for each prior period presented, to the extent practicable);
  • If retrospective restatement is impracticable for a particular period, the circumstances and a description of how/from when the error has been corrected.

Pitfalls / exam tips

  • Errors are corrected retrospectively; estimates are revised prospectively — do not confuse the two directions.
  • A change from an unacceptable/non-Ind-AS-compliant accounting basis to an Ind AS-compliant basis is correction of an error, not a change in accounting policy.

5. Ind AS 10 — Events After the Reporting Period: Basics

Definitions

TermMeaning

  • Rule: Even if some financial information is publicly announced (e.g., preliminary profit figures) before the FS are formally approved, all events up to the date of approval must still be considered as "events after the reporting period."

Pitfalls / exam tips

  • The trigger date is BOD approval, not AGM/shareholder approval, not the date of signing the audit report by itself, and not the date of issue to the public — this is a frequently tested nuance.

6. Adjusting vs Non-Adjusting Events — Examples & Special Cases

Rule — Adjusting events (recognise/adjust amounts in the FS):

  1. Settlement of a court case after the balance sheet date that confirms a present obligation existing at year-end.
  2. Receipt of information after the reporting period indicating an asset was impaired at year-end, or that a previously recognised impairment loss needs adjustment.
  3. Sale of inventories after year-end (but before FS approval) providing evidence of NRV at year-end.
  4. Determination, after year-end, of the actual purchase/sale price of an asset transacted before year-end (the amount recognised at year-end is adjusted to the amount finally determined).
  5. Discovery of fraud or error after the reporting period which relates to that reporting period.

Rule — Non-adjusting events (disclose only, no adjustment to recognised amounts):

  1. Decline in fair value of investments between year-end and date of approval, where the decline does not relate to conditions existing at year-end.
  2. A major business combination where both the agreement and completion occur after year-end (non-adjusting). If the agreement (not mere negotiations/meetings) was entered into before year-end but completed after, it becomes an adjusting event.
  3. Destruction of a major production plant by fire after the reporting period.
  4. Changes in tax rates after the reporting period.
  5. Commencement of major litigation arising solely from events occurring after the reporting period.
  6. Abnormally large changes in asset prices or foreign exchange rates after the reporting period.

Special Case A — Dividends: Dividends declared after the end of the reporting period but before FS approval are not recognised as a liability at year-end (no obligation existed at that date) — they are a non-adjusting event, disclosed in the notes to accounts.

Special Case B — Going Concern: If, after year-end but before FS approval, management determines an intention/necessity to liquidate or cease trading, or a major deterioration of operating results/financial position indicates going concern is no longer appropriate, the FS for that reporting period must not be prepared on the going concern basis — a different basis (e.g., liquidation/realisable-value basis) must be used, and this must be disclosed per Ind AS 1. (Under this basis, long-term liabilities effectively become due/short-term, and inventory/WIP is valued at market/realisable/scrap value.)

Special Case C — Long-term loan arrangement breach (India-specific carve-out from IAS 10):

  • ⚠️ VERIFY — Where there is a breach of a material provision of a long-term loan on or before the end of the reporting period, causing the liability to become payable on demand at the reporting date, and the lender agrees after the reporting period but before FS approval not to demand payment as a consequence of the breach — under Ind AS 10 this is treated as an ADJUSTING event (India-specific carve-out; the loan continues to be classified as non-current). This differs from IAS 10 (IFRS), where the same waiver agreed after the reporting period is a non-adjusting event (loan remains classified as current at year-end, since no unconditional right to defer settlement existed at the reporting date).

Pitfalls / exam tips

  • The business-combination "agreement vs negotiation" distinction is a favourite trick — negotiations/MOUs before year-end do not make it adjusting; a binding agreement does.
  • The long-term loan covenant carve-out is one of very few India-specific (Ind AS vs IAS) differences examinable under FR — always state explicitly that it is a carve-out and contrast with IAS 10 treatment.

7. Appendix A to Ind AS 10 — Distribution of Non-cash Assets to Owners

Key rule(s):

  • When an entity declares a distribution and has a binding obligation to distribute non-cash assets to owners, it must recognise a liability for the dividend payable.
  • Measurement:

Dividend Payable = Fair Value of the asset(s) to be distributed

  • At the end of each reporting period and again at the date of settlement, the carrying amount of the dividend payable is remeasured to the then fair value of the assets to be distributed; the change in carrying amount is recognised in Other Equity as an adjustment to the amount of the distribution.
  • On settlement (actual distribution), the entity recognises the difference between the carrying amount of the assets distributed and the carrying amount of the dividend payable in Profit or Loss.

Gain/(Loss) on settlement = Carrying amount of Dividend Payable (at settlement) - Carrying amount of the Asset distributed

Concept: This ensures the dividend liability always reflects the current fair value of what will actually be handed over to owners, with fair-value re-measurement routed through equity (since it's a transaction with owners) until final settlement, when any leftover gain/loss (typically the difference between the settled fair value and the asset's book value) hits P&L.

Pitfalls / exam tips

  • The Appendix A liability is remeasured through Other Equity, not P&L, at each reporting date and pre-settlement — only the final settlement difference (asset's carrying amount vs the settled dividend payable) goes to P&L.
  • Scope exclusion: this Appendix does not apply to a distribution of a non-cash asset that is ultimately controlled by the same party/parties both before and after the distribution (common-control transactions) — do not apply Appendix A there.

04. Ind AS 12 - Income Taxes

Where it fits: Governs accounting for the current-period tax charge and for deferred tax arising from temporary differences between the carrying amount of assets/liabilities (per books) and their tax base — a balance-sheet approach, tested heavily in FR both as standalone theory/computation and embedded in Business Combinations, Consolidation and Ind AS 116/109 questions.

A. Current Tax

  • Definition: Current tax is the amount of income taxes payable (recoverable) in respect of the taxable profit (tax loss) for a period.
  • Measurement rule: Current tax liabilities/assets are measured using tax rates (and tax laws) enacted or substantively enacted by the end of the reporting period — i.e., the rate applicable for the current period.
  • Recognition — split rule:
  • If the income/expense item is recognised in P&L, the related current tax is also recognised in P&L.
  • If the item is recognised in OCI (or directly in equity), the related current tax is also recognised in OCI (or equity).
  • Offsetting current tax asset & current tax liability — allowed only if:
  • the entity has a legally enforceable right to set off the recognised amounts; AND
  • the entity intends either to settle on a net basis, or to realise the asset and settle the liability simultaneously.
  • Note: the legally enforceable right to set off normally exists only when the amounts relate to income taxes levied by the same taxation authority on the same taxable entity.

Pitfalls / exam tips

  • Do not confuse "enacted or substantively enacted" with "expected future rate" — for current tax always use the rate for the current period; for deferred tax use the rate expected to apply when the DTA/DTL reverses (Step 6 below).
  • The OCI-tax-follows-the-item rule is a favourite examiner twist — check where the underlying item sits before booking tax.

B. Deferred Tax — The 9-Step Framework

Concept: Deferred tax captures the future tax consequence of temporary differences between the carrying amount of an asset/liability and its tax base, so that the tax expense in P&L matches the accounting profit it relates to (matching principle applied to tax).

Step 1 — Carrying amount: balance of the asset/liability per the accounting records (books).

Step 2 — Tax base: the amount attributed to the asset/liability for tax purposes.

  • If an asset is not taxable or a liability is not tax-deductible (i.e., the difference is not "temporary" in nature), then Tax Base = Carrying Amount (no temporary difference).

Step 3 — Temporary difference:

Temporary Difference = Carrying Amount of Asset/Liability - Tax Base

Step 4 — Classification:

  • Taxable temporary difference → gives rise to a Deferred Tax Liability (DTL).
  • Deductible temporary difference (DTD) → gives rise to a Deferred Tax Asset (DTA).
  • If Carrying Amount = Tax Base → no temporary difference, no deferred tax.

Step 5 — Assess/reassess DTA on DTD, unused tax losses & unused tax credits:

  • A DTA on a DTD is recognised only when it is probable that future taxable profit will be available against which the DTD can be utilised. Three-step criteria (applied in serial order):
  1. Existence of taxable temporary differences — check whether sufficient taxable temporary differences exist whose reversal pattern matches the DTD's reversal pattern.
  2. Probability of future profits — if it is probable there will be sufficient future taxable profits (excluding those from deductible temporary differences), recognise DTA to the extent of available profits.
  3. Tax planning opportunities — if unrecognised DTD still remains, assess whether tax planning opportunities (actions to create/increase taxable income before a tax loss expires) are available.
  • Unused tax losses/credits: DTA is recognised for carry-forward of unused tax losses and unused tax credits only to the extent it is probable that future taxable profit will be available against which they can be utilised.
  • Reassessment: At each reporting date, an entity reassesses previously unrecognised DTA — it must recognise a previously unrecognised DTA once it becomes probable that sufficient future taxable profit will allow it to be utilised.

Step 6 — Determine tax rate for deferred tax:

  • Deferred tax is measured at tax rates expected to apply to the period when the DTA is realised or the DTL is settled, using rates (and laws) enacted or substantively enacted by the end of the reporting period.
  • The rate used must be consistent with the expected manner of recovery or settlement of the asset/liability.
  • Example: Asset — Carrying Amount = ₹100, Tax Base = ₹60; tax rate if sold = 20%, if used = 30%.
  • If the entity expects to sell the asset: DTL = 40 × 20% = ₹8.
  • If the entity expects to use/retain the asset and recover the carrying amount through use: DTL = 40 × 30% = ₹12.
  • Revaluation of a non-depreciable asset: the DTA/DTL is measured at the tax rate applicable to recovery of the carrying amount through sale (regardless of intended use), since a non-depreciable asset (e.g. land) is ultimately recovered only through disposal.
  • Distribution of dividends: where the tax rate/refund depends on whether profits are distributed as dividend, current and deferred tax assets/liabilities are measured at the tax rate applicable to undistributed profits.

Step 7 — Calculate & recognise deferred tax: compute DTL/DTA on each temporary difference (net of Step 5 recognition criteria) and recognise the movement as deferred tax expense/income.

  • Note: DTA/DTL are never discounted to present value.

Step 8 — Accounting for deferred tax (change in carrying amount without change in the underlying temporary difference):

  • Carrying amount of DTA/DTL can change even without a change in the related temporary difference, due to:
  • a change in tax rates or tax laws;
  • a reassessment of the recoverability of a DTA; or
  • a change in the expected manner of recovery of an asset.
  • The resulting deferred tax adjustment is recognised in P&L, except to the extent it relates to an item that was originally recognised outside P&L (i.e., in OCI/equity) — then the adjustment also goes outside P&L.

Step 9 — Offsetting DTA & DTL — allowed only if:

  • the entity has a legally enforceable right to set off current tax assets against current tax liabilities; AND
  • the DTA and DTL relate to income taxes levied by the same taxation authority on the same taxable entity (or different entities that intend to settle on a net basis / simultaneously and have the legal right to do so).

Pitfalls / exam tips

  • Remember the serial order of the three DTA-recognition criteria in Step 5 — examiners test whether you skip straight to "tax planning opportunities" without first checking existing taxable temporary differences.
  • "Probable" is not "virtually certain" — do not import the AS 22 virtual-certainty threshold into Ind AS 12 answers (see AS 22 comparison table).
  • DTA/DTL are always non-current in nature conceptually and are never discounted, and never split current/non-current on the face of the balance sheet under Ind AS (Schedule III requires them to be shown as non-current assets/liabilities).

C. Practical Applications & Exceptions

1. Business Combination

  • Identifiable assets and liabilities acquired in a business combination are recognised at fair value at the acquisition date (Ind AS 103), but the tax base of those assets/liabilities is often unaffected (or affected differently) by the combination — this creates a temporary difference.
  • Example: carrying amount stepped up to fair value but tax base remains at the previous owner's cost → taxable temporary difference → DTL, which increases goodwill (or reduces bargain purchase gain).
  • Initial recognition exception: a DTL is not recognised for a taxable temporary difference arising from the initial recognition of goodwill itself (goodwill is not "grossed up" for its own deferred tax).
  • If, on acquisition, the acquirer's previously unrecognised DTA now becomes probable of recognition (post-combination), recognising it does not impact goodwill — it is recognised as deferred tax income in the acquirer's post-combination P&L (per Ind AS 103, recognised separately from the business combination accounting once the recognition criteria are met after the measurement period, subject to Ind AS 103 measurement-period adjustments if within the window).

2. Asset Acquisition (not a business combination)

  • No DTA/DTL is recognised on initial recognition of an asset or liability if all of the following hold:
  • the transaction is not a business combination; AND
  • at the time of the transaction, it affects neither accounting profit nor taxable profit.

3. Share-Based Payments (SBP)

  • Under Ind AS 102, an entity recognises an SBP expense over the vesting period, but often gets the tax deduction only on/after exercise of the options.
  • Carrying amount of the SBP transaction (as an asset/liability for tax-base purposes) is always NIL.
  • Tax base = the amount the tax authority will permit as a deduction in future periods (often based on intrinsic value at the future date).
  • The excess of tax base over carrying amount (nil) is a deductible temporary difference → DTA.
  • Since the SBP expense is recognised in P&L, the related deferred tax is also recognised in P&L.

4. Investments in Subsidiaries, Branches, Associates & Joint Ventures

  • Temporary differences arise when the carrying amount of the investment (share of net assets, including goodwill) differs from its tax base (often original cost) — typically because of undistributed post-acquisition profits, which are taxable on distribution as dividend.
  • DTL: an entity recognises a DTL for all taxable temporary differences associated with such investments except to the extent both of the following are satisfied:
  • the parent/investor/joint venturer/operator is able to control the timing of the reversal of the temporary difference; AND
  • it is probable that the temporary difference will not reverse in the foreseeable future.
  • DTA: an entity recognises a DTA for deductible temporary differences associated with such investments only to the extent that:
  • it is probable that the temporary difference will reverse in the foreseeable future; AND
  • taxable profit will be available against which the temporary difference can be utilised.

05. Ind AS 16 - Property, Plant & Equipment

Where it fits: Governs recognition, initial/subsequent measurement, depreciation and derecognition of tangible fixed assets — the base standard that most PPE-heavy problems (component accounting, revaluation, decommissioning) in FR build on; interacts with Ind AS 36 (impairment), Ind AS 23 (borrowing costs), Ind AS 8 (change in estimate) and Ind AS 105 (assets held for sale).

Definitions & Scope

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • PPE = tangible items that (a) are held for use in production/supply of goods or services, for rental to others, or for administrative purposes, AND (b) are expected to be used during more than one accounting period.
  • Bearer plants (e.g., grape vines, tea/coffee bushes) are accounted for under Ind AS 16 (not Ind AS 41 Agriculture); the produce growing on the bearer plant is covered by Ind AS 41 until harvest, then Ind AS 2.
  • Concept: Sets the scope boundary — a living plant qualifies as "bearer plant" (and hence PPE) only if it is used to bear produce over more than one period and is not itself intended for sale as agricultural produce.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Incidental scrap sale of a bearer plant at the end of its productive life (e.g., sold as firewood) does NOT disqualify it from being a bearer plant.
  • Annual crops (e.g., wheat, sugarcane) are never bearer plants — they fall entirely under Ind AS 41.
TermMeaning
Bearer plantLiving plant used to bear produce for >1 period, with remote likelihood of sale as agricultural produce (except incidental scrap)
Class of PPEGrouping of assets of similar nature/use in operations (e.g., Land, Plant & Machinery, Vehicles, Furniture)

Recognition Criteria

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Recognise an item of PPE as an asset only if: (a) it is probable that future economic benefits associated with the item will flow to the entity, AND (b) the cost of the item can be measured reliably.
  • Concept: Applies at initial recognition and equally to subsequent costs (additions, replacements, major inspections) — both recognition conditions must be met before capitalising.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Spare parts and servicing equipment are usually expensed unless they meet the PPE definition (used during more than one period) and the recognition criteria — then capitalise and depreciate over their own useful life or the life of the main asset, whichever is shorter.

Measurement at Recognition — Components of Cost

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Cost of PPE = Purchase Price - Trade Discounts/Rebates + Non-refundable Taxes/Duties + Directly Attributable Costs + PV of Decommissioning/Restoration Cost

  • Directly attributable costs INCLUDE: employee costs of construction/acquisition, site preparation, initial delivery & handling, installation & assembly, testing costs (net of proceeds from sale of items produced while testing, per the Ind AS-specific rule below), and professional fees.
  • Directly attributable costs EXCLUDE: costs of opening a new facility/inauguration expenses, advertising & promotional costs, costs of conducting business in a new location or with a new class of customer, administration & other general overheads, initial operating losses, and costs of relocating/reorganising the entity's operations.
  • Cash discounts availed on payment are credited to P&L (not deducted from cost of PPE).
  • Income and related expenses of incidental operations (e.g., using a site under construction as a paid car park) are recognised in P&L, not adjusted against cost of PPE.
  • Concept: Cost = everything necessary to bring the asset to the location and condition necessary for it to be capable of operating in the manner intended by management.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • ⚠️ VERIFY — Ind AS-specific carve-out on testing proceeds: cost of testing is part of PPE cost; sale proceeds of test-production items are first netted against the cost of testing. If net sale proceeds exceed the cost of testing, the excess is not credited to P&L but is deducted from the directly attributable cost of the asset (i.e., it further reduces cost of PPE). This is narrower than the pure IFRS position under the 2022 IAS 16 amendment (which routes ALL such proceeds and related costs through P&L) — confirm against the latest ICAI study material/RTP for the exact wording applicable to your attempt.
  • Decommissioning/site-restoration cost is recognised at present value on initial recognition; the discount is unwound every year-end through a finance charge (Dr. Finance Charge / Cr. Provision for Decommissioning) — this unwinding is a finance cost, not eligible for borrowing-cost capitalisation under Ind AS 23.

Deferred Consideration

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Cost of PPE = Cash Price Equivalent at Recognition Date

Interest Expense = Total Payment - Cash Price Equivalent

  • Concept: When payment is deferred beyond normal credit terms, the difference between the cash price equivalent and the total payment is a financing cost, recognised as interest expense over the period of credit (not capitalised into the cost of the asset).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Only the excess over normal credit terms triggers this treatment — routine trade credit does not require discounting.

Exchange of Assets

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): Where the transaction does NOT lack commercial substance (hierarchy applied in order):

1st Preference: Cost = FV of Asset Given Up + Cash Paid

2nd Preference (if FV of asset given up is not reliably measurable): Cost = FV of Asset Acquired

3rd Preference (if neither FV is reliably measurable): Cost = Carrying Amount of Asset Given Up + Cash Paid

  • Where the transaction lacks commercial substance, only one measure applies regardless of FV availability:

Cost = Carrying Amount of Asset Given Up + Cash Paid

  • Concept: A transaction lacks commercial substance if the future cash flows (risk, timing and amount) are not expected to change as a result of the exchange — i.e., the entity is economically no better or worse off.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Always test commercial substance FIRST before applying the FV hierarchy; students commonly jump straight to "FV of asset given up" and skip this qualifying test.

Measurement after Recognition — Revaluation Model

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • An entire class of PPE must be revalued together (no cherry-picking individual assets within a class) — e.g., Land & Building, Plant & Machinery, Vehicles are separate classes; industrial and office buildings may be treated as separate classes given differing characteristics.
  • Revaluation gain (increase): credited to Other Comprehensive Income and accumulated in equity as "Revaluation Surplus," EXCEPT to the extent it reverses a revaluation decrease of the same asset previously recognised in P&L — that portion is recognised in P&L.
  • Revaluation loss (decrease): recognised in P&L, EXCEPT to the extent of any credit balance in revaluation surplus for that same asset — that portion is debited to OCI (reducing the surplus) first.
  • Concept: Revaluation model restates the carrying amount to fair value at the revaluation date, with gains/losses on the same asset offsetting each other year-on-year through the OCI/P&L split above (never net gain-vs-loss across different assets/classes).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • The offset rule works only against the same asset's accumulated surplus/deficit — not across other assets in the class.
  • Revaluations must be kept sufficiently up to date so carrying amount does not differ materially from fair value at the reporting date.

Transfer of Revaluation Surplus to Retained Earnings

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Annual Transfer = Depreciation on Revalued Carrying Amount - Depreciation on Original Cost

  • On disposal/derecognition of the asset, the entire remaining balance of revaluation surplus for that asset is transferred to retained earnings.
  • Concept: Realises the "excess" surplus tied up in higher depreciation charges (or the full balance on disposal) by moving it directly within equity.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: This transfer is made directly within equity (Revaluation Surplus → Retained Earnings) and must NEVER be routed through profit or loss.

Component Accounting

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Each significant part/component of an item of PPE with a cost that is significant in relation to the total cost of the item must be depreciated separately.
  • Even where the cost is not significant, an entity may still choose to apply component accounting.
  • Parts with the same useful life and depreciation method may be grouped and depreciated together.
  • Concept: Recognises that different parts of a single asset (e.g., aircraft airframe vs. engines, building shell vs. roof) wear out at different rates and should be depreciated accordingly.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: On replacement of a significant part — derecognise the carrying amount of the old (replaced) part and recognise the new part's cost, regardless of whether the old part was depreciated separately before.

Subsequent Costs (Major Inspection / Overhaul)

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): No fixed formula — apply component logic:
  • Cost of a major inspection is capitalised (component accounting) if recognition criteria are met, and depreciated separately over the period to the next inspection.
  • When a new inspection cost is capitalised, any remaining carrying amount of the cost of the previous inspection (if separately identified) is derecognised — regardless of whether it was depreciated fully.
  • Concept: Regular inspections (e.g., aircraft overhauls) required as a condition of continued operation are treated as a replacement/component, not routine repairs & maintenance.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Day-to-day servicing costs (labour, consumables, minor parts) are expensed to P&L as incurred — they are NOT capitalised even under component accounting.

Land & Building

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): Land and buildings are separable assets and are accounted for separately, even when acquired together as a single purchase.
  • Concept: Land normally has an unlimited useful life (not depreciated, except quarries/sites with a limited extraction life); buildings have a limited useful life and are depreciated.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: In a combined purchase, allocate the total cost between land and building on a reasonable basis (e.g., relative fair values) before depreciating only the building component.

Depreciation

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Depreciable Amount = Cost (or Revalued Amount) - Residual Value

Depreciation (Straight-Line Method) = Depreciable AmountUseful Life

  • Concept: Depreciation is the systematic allocation of the depreciable amount of an asset over its useful life, charged to P&L (or capitalised into another asset's cost where appropriate, e.g., inventory).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Depreciation does NOT cease when an asset becomes idle or is retired from active use pending sale/disposal (unless it is already fully depreciated) — it continues unless the asset is classified as held for sale under Ind AS 105.
  • Under the units-of-production method, depreciation for a period CAN be zero if there is no production, since the charge tracks output/activity rather than time.

Residual Value & Useful Life

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • If estimated residual value exceeds (or equals) the asset's carrying amount, depreciation charge is zero until residual value subsequently reduces below carrying amount.
  • Concept: Residual value is the estimated net amount an entity would currently obtain from disposal of the asset (as if already of the age and condition expected at end of useful life). Useful life is the period over which the asset is expected to be available for use by the entity — this may be shorter than the asset's total economic life.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Useful life is entity-specific (based on expected usage pattern, management's asset-replacement policy) — do not confuse it with the asset's physical/economic life.

Annual Review (per Ind AS 8)

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): Depreciation method, residual value, and useful life must be reviewed at least at each financial year-end.
  • Concept: Any revision arising from such a review is accounted for prospectively as a Change in Accounting Estimate under Ind AS 8 — Accounting Policies, Changes in Accounting Estimates and Errors — never retrospectively and never as a prior-period error.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: The revised depreciable amount is spread over the remaining useful life from the date of change — do not restate prior years' depreciation.

Impairment

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): Impairment testing and recognition of impairment losses/reversals on PPE follow Ind AS 36 — Impairment of Assets (outside the scope of Ind AS 16 itself).
  • Concept: Where a third party compensates the entity for an impairment loss (e.g., insurance claim for fire damage), such compensation is recognised in P&L when the right to receive it becomes receivable.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: It is NOT appropriate to net the compensation against the impairment loss, treat it as deferred income, or deduct it from the cost of a replacement asset — each event (impairment, compensation, replacement) is accounted for as a separate transaction.

Derecognition

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Gain/Loss on Derecognition = Net Disposal Proceeds - Carrying Amount

  • Derecognise an item of PPE (a) on disposal, OR (b) when no future economic benefits are expected from its use or disposal.
  • Concept: The resulting gain or loss is recognised in P&L in the period of derecognition.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: The gain on derecognition must NOT be classified as Revenue — present it under "Other Income" (per Ind AS 1/Schedule III), even though the loss reduces profit through normal expense presentation.

Changes in Decommissioning, Restoration & Similar Liabilities (Appendix A to Ind AS 16)

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): Changes in the estimated liability (from change in timing/amount of outflow, or change in the discount rate — excluding periodic unwinding) are accounted for as follows:
  • Cost model: the change is added to, or deducted from, the cost of the related asset in the current period, and the revised carrying amount is depreciated prospectively over the remaining useful life. If a decrease in the liability exceeds the carrying amount of the asset (i.e., would drive it negative), the excess is recognised immediately in P&L.
  • Revaluation model: an increase in the liability is recognised in OCI (reducing revaluation surplus, then P&L for any excess) as a revaluation decrease; a decrease in the liability is recognised in OCI (increasing revaluation surplus, subject to reversing any prior P&L charge on the same asset) as a revaluation increase — i.e., routed through the same OCI/P&L split used for ordinary revaluation gains/losses.
  • Periodic unwinding of the discount (passage of time) is recognised in P&L as a finance cost as it occurs, and is NOT eligible for borrowing-cost capitalisation under Ind AS 23.
  • Concept: Isolates re-measurement of the decommissioning provision (a change in estimate under Ind AS 37) from the routine time-based unwinding of the discount, and links the re-measurement to the underlying asset's measurement model.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Students often mistakenly route ALL liability changes through P&L — remember the cost-model vs. revaluation-model split, and the "excess over carrying amount → P&L immediately" cap under the cost model.

06. Ind AS 19 - Employee Benefits

Where it fits: Covers accounting for all forms of employee compensation — short-term, post-employment (defined contribution & defined benefit), other long-term, and termination benefits — with the Defined Benefit / gratuity computation (PUCM, DBO roll-forward, OCI remeasurements, asset ceiling) being the heaviest numerical territory tested in FR.

A. Scope & Classification of Employee Benefits

  • Scope: Ind AS 19 applies to all employee benefits except:
  • Benefits within the scope of Ind AS 102, Share-based Payment; and
  • Reporting by employee benefit plans (that is covered elsewhere, not by the employer's own books).
  • "Employees" for this Standard includes directors and other management personnel.
  • Four categories of employee benefits:
  • Short-term employee benefits
  • Post-employment benefits (Defined Contribution Plans & Defined Benefit Plans)
  • Other long-term employee benefits
  • Termination benefits

Concept: The classification driver across the standard is timing of settlement relative to 12 months from the end of the reporting period in which the related service is rendered — this single trigger repeatedly decides short-term vs long-term treatment and whether discounting applies.

Pitfalls / exam tips

  • Directors/management are employees for Ind AS 19 purposes — a common trick in MCQs.
  • Share-based payments (ESOPs) are carved out to Ind AS 102 even though they are also "employee benefits" in a loose sense.

B. Short-Term Employee Benefits

Key rule(s):

  • Short-term employee benefits are those expected to be settled wholly before 12 months after the end of the annual reporting period in which the employees render the related service.
  • Examples: wages & salaries, paid annual/sick leave, profit-sharing and bonuses (if payable within 12 months), non-monetary benefits (medical care, housing, cars, subsidised goods/services) for current employees.
  • Measurement: the undiscounted amount of short-term employee benefits expected to be paid in exchange for that service.

Concept: Because settlement is near-term, no actuarial assumptions or discounting are needed — it is essentially an accrual of the undiscounted cost of service rendered.

Pitfalls / exam tips

  • Short-term benefit amounts are never discounted, even if a small portion technically falls just after the year-end.
  • If the employee's service related to short-term benefits went into constructing/producing an asset or inventory, the cost is capitalised — added to the cost of the asset (Ind AS 16) or cost of inventory (Ind AS 2) — rather than expensed outright.

Profit-Sharing and Bonus Plans

  • Recognise the expected cost of profit-sharing/bonus payments only when both:
  1. The entity has a present legal or constructive obligation to make such payments as a result of past events; AND
  2. A reliable estimate of the obligation can be made.
  • Such cost is recognised as an expense (through P&L), not as a distribution of profit — the obligation arises from employee service, not a transaction with owners.
  • If profit-sharing/bonus payments are not settled within 12 months after the end of the reporting period in which the service is rendered, they are reclassified and accounted for as Other Long-Term Employee Benefits.

  • Plans are classified based on the economic substance of the plan, not merely its legal form, into two types:

D. Accounting for Defined Contribution Plans

Key formula(s)/rule(s):

  • Journal entry: Employee Benefit Expense (P&L) A/c Dr. → To Cash / Liability (if unpaid), for the contribution payable for the period.
  • Measurement basis:
  • Obligation falling due within 12 months from the end of the reporting period → measured on an undiscounted basis.
  • Obligation falling due after 12 months after the end of the reporting period in which the related service is rendered → discounted.

Concept: Since the entity's obligation is capped at the contribution amount, there is no actuarial computation — the expense equals the contribution payable for the period.

E. Accounting for Defined Benefit Plans — The PUCM Framework

Key rule:

  • The Projected Unit Credit Method (PUCM) is used to determine the present value of the Defined Benefit Obligation (DBO), the related Current Service Cost, and Past Service Cost.

The 13 building blocks tested (worked with the standard gratuity example: salary ₹10 lakh p.a., gratuity = 1% × final salary × completed years, salary growth 10% p.a., 5-year expected service, discount rate 10%):

  1. Current Service Cost (CSC) — the increase in the PV of the DBO resulting from employee service in the current year.
  2. Defined Benefit Obligation (DBO) — the PV of expected future payments required to settle the obligation arising from employee service in current and prior periods.
  3. Interest Cost on DBO

Interest Cost = Opening PV of DBO × Discount Rate
— the increase in DBO due to the passage of time (unwinding of discount).

  1. Actuarial Gain/Loss on DBO — changes in the PV of the DBO from changes in actuarial assumptions (future salary growth, service period, discount rate, mortality, etc.) and experience adjustments.
  • Increase in DBO → Actuarial Loss on DBO (OCI-NR) Dr. → To DBO A/c
  • Decrease in DBO → DBO A/c Dr. → To Actuarial Gain on DBO (OCI-NR)
  1. Past Service Cost (PSC) — change in the PV of the DBO for employee service in prior periods, arising from a plan amendment (e.g., gratuity rate increased from 1% to 3%) or a curtailment.
  • Increase in DBO → PSC A/c (P&L-EBE) Dr. → To DBO A/c
  • Decrease in DBO → DBO A/c Dr. → To PSC A/c (P&L-EBE)
  1. Plan Assets (PA) — assets held exclusively to fund the DBO; usable only to settle the DBO, unavailable to the entity's own creditors (even on bankruptcy), and not returnable to the entity except where PA exceed the DBO. Measured at Fair Value.
  • Contribution into plan → Plan Assets A/c Dr. → To Bank A/c
  • Withdrawal to pay benefits → Bank A/c Dr. → To Plan Assets A/c
  1. Expected Return on Plan Assets

Expected Return on PA = Plan Assets × Discount Rate

  • Under Ind AS 19 the expected rate of return equals the same discount rate used to compute the PV of the DBO.
  • Entry: Plan Assets A/c Dr. → To Interest Income A/c (P&L-EBE)
  1. Actuarial Gain/Loss on Plan Assets — the difference between actual return and expected return on plan assets (since PA is fair-valued).

Actual Return on PA = Expected Return on PA + Actuarial Gain on PA - Actuarial Loss on PA

  • Actual return > expected → Plan Assets A/c Dr. → To Actuarial Gain (OCI-NR)
  • Actual return < expected → Actuarial Loss (OCI-NR) A/c Dr. → To Plan Assets A/c
  1. Curtailment & Settlement:
CurtailmentSettlement
Entity significantly reduces the number of employees covered by the plan (plant closure, discontinuance/termination/suspension of an operation)Entity settles all/part of a DBP obligation before its due date (e.g., DBO of ₹1,00,000 settled for ₹90,000)
  • Curtailment entry: DBO A/c Dr. → To Gain on Curtailment / PSC (P&L-EBE)
  • Settlement entries: withdraw plan assets Bank A/c Dr. → To Plan Assets A/c; then settle DBO DBO A/c Dr. → To Bank A/c and To Gain on Settlement (P&L-EBE) for the balancing gain.
  1. Net Defined Benefit Liability / Asset — Plan Assets and PV of DBO are netted off on the Balance Sheet.
  • PV of DBO > Plan Assets → Net Defined Benefit Liability (Net Deficit) — shown on the liability side.
  • Plan Assets > PV of DBO → Net Defined Benefit Asset (Net Surplus) — shown on the asset side, subject to the Asset Ceiling.
  1. Net Interest Expense / Income — because the same discount rate is used for both interest cost (on DBO) and interest income (expected return on PA), Ind AS 19 nets them:

Net Interest Expense/Income = Interest Cost on DBO - Interest Income on Plan Assets
Shown under Employee Benefit Expense (EBE) in P&L.

  1. Asset Ceiling — where PA > DBO (Net Surplus), the excess is recognised as an asset only if:
  • the entity has a right to recover the excess via refunds from the plan; OR
  • the entity can reduce future contributions to the plan.

Net Defined Benefit Asset = min(Plan Assets - DBO, Asset Ceiling)

  • Any surplus above the Asset Ceiling is written down through OCI (NR) as a Remeasurement Loss. Asset ceiling is irrelevant in a Net Deficit (liability) situation.
  1. Remeasurement Gain/Loss — comprises (a) actuarial gains/losses on DBO, (b) actuarial gains/losses on plan assets, and (c) any change due to the asset ceiling. Recognised in OCI, and — per Ind AS 19 para 122 — never reclassified to profit or loss in a later period.

Pitfalls / exam tips

  • Employees typically get one fewer increment than the years of expected service (increment applies from year 2 onward) — a classic trap in the salary-projection step of the worked example.

  • Eligible period: starts when service first leads to benefit accrual and ends when further service no longer adds to benefit entitlement.

F. Other Long-Term Employee Benefits

Key rule(s):

  • Employee benefits expected to be settled after 12 months from the end of the reporting period (excluding post-employment benefits and termination benefits).
  • Examples: sabbatical/long-service (jubilee) benefits, long-term disability benefits, profit-sharing and bonuses not settled within 12 months, deferred remuneration.
  • Accounting: Same as Defined Benefit Plans (PUCM basis) except the entire Remeasurement Gain/Loss is recognised in P&L, not OCI.

Concept: The measurement of other long-term benefits is not usually subject to the same degree of uncertainty as post-employment benefits, so there is no need to insulate P&L via OCI — hence no separate remeasurement-in-OCI treatment.

Pitfalls / exam tips

  • Do not default to "OCI treatment" just because PUCM is used — remember the single point of difference from DBP is that remeasurements hit P&L here.

G. Termination Benefits

Key rule(s):

  • Termination benefits arise from either:
  • the entity's decision to terminate the employment; or
  • the employee's decision to accept the entity's offer of benefits in exchange for termination.
  • To qualify as termination benefits, the benefit must both (a) result from the entity's decision to terminate employment and (b) not be conditional on future service.
  • Excluded from termination benefits: benefits from termination at the employee's own request without an entity offer, or from mandatory retirement — these are post-employment benefits.
  • Recognition — liability and expense recognised at the EARLIER of:
  1. when the entity can no longer withdraw the offer of termination benefits; or
  2. when the entity recognises restructuring costs within the scope of Ind AS 37 that involve payment of termination benefits.
  • If termination benefits are expected to be settled after 12 months after the end of the reporting period, discounting applies.

Concept: Only the portion of a benefit that is exclusively linked to termination is a termination benefit; any portion conditional on continued/future service is a service cost (short-term or other long-term, as applicable), not a termination cost.

Worked example (factory closure in 10 months, 120 employees):

  • Terms: stay till closure → ₹30,000; leave early → ₹10,000. Expected 20 of 120 will leave early.
  • Total expected cash outflow = 20 × ₹10,000 + 100 × ₹30,000 = ₹32,00,000.
  • Termination benefit = the amount payable regardless of whether the employee stays or leaves = ₹10,000 × 120 employees = ₹12,00,000, recognised in full at the earlier of the plan announcement date or the Ind AS 37 restructuring-recognition date.
  • Short-term employee benefit = incremental amount for staying and rendering service for the full 10 months = (₹30,000 – ₹10,000) × 100 employees = ₹20,00,000, recognised undiscounted over the 10-month period at ₹2,00,000 (₹20,00,000 ÷ 10) per month, with a corresponding increase in liability.

Pitfalls / exam tips

  • Always split a termination package into its termination-linked component (recognise upfront) and its service-linked incremental component (spread over the future service period as a short-term/other long-term benefit) — this split is the crux of nearly every termination benefits numerical.

07. Ind AS 20 & Ind AS 21 - Government Grants and Effects of Changes in Foreign Exchange Rates

Where it fits: Ind AS 20 governs recognition and presentation of grants/subsidies received from government; Ind AS 21 governs translating foreign-currency transactions into the functional currency and translating an entity's (or foreign operation's) financial statements into a presentation currency — both feed heavily into Ind AS 110 consolidation questions (FCTR, NCI, disposal of foreign subsidiaries).

Ind AS 20 — Meaning & Scope Exclusions

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Government Grant = assistance by government in the form of transfer of resources to an entity in return for past or future compliance with certain conditions relating to the operating activities of the entity.
  • EXCLUDED from Ind AS 20 (i.e., not accounted for as a "government grant" under this standard):
  • Government assistance that cannot be reasonably valued (e.g., free technical/marketing advice, provision of guarantees).
  • Transactions with government that cannot be distinguished from the entity's normal trading transactions (e.g., a government procurement policy responsible for a portion of sales — this is a normal trading relationship, not a grant).
  • Benefits given through the tax system — income tax holidays, investment tax credits, accelerated depreciation allowances, reduced income tax rates (these are dealt with under Ind AS 12, not Ind AS 20).
  • Government participation in the ownership of the entity.
  • Government grants covered by Ind AS 41 Agriculture (grants related to biological assets).
  • Concept: Not every benefit received from government is a "grant" under Ind AS 20 — tax-system benefits, unquantifiable assistance, and indistinguishable trading transactions are carved out even though they may still need separate disclosure.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • The standard note in the source material itself flags that even when a benefit is excluded from Ind AS 20 accounting, its nature, extent and duration may still need disclosure so the financial statements are not misleading.
  • Common trap: accelerated depreciation for tax purposes is NOT a government grant — it is a temporary difference to be accounted for under Ind AS 12.

Ind AS 20 — Recognition & Measurement

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Recognise a government grant (including non-monetary grants at fair value) only when there is reasonable assurance that: (a) the entity will comply with the conditions attached to the grant; AND (b) the grant will be received.
  • Recognition of a grant is not dependent on the manner in which it is received — a grant is accounted for in the same way whether received in cash or as a reduction of a liability owed to government.
  • Grants are recognised in profit or loss on a systematic basis over the periods in which the entity recognises, as expenses, the related costs for which the grant is intended to compensate — i.e., matched to the related expense/asset life, not recognised upfront merely on receipt.
  • Concept: Two broad categories drive presentation: (i) grants related to assets (capital grants tied to acquisition/construction of a long-term asset), and (ii) grants related to income (all grants other than those related to assets, e.g., compensation for expenses already incurred, or subsidies to encourage an activity).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • A grant becoming receivable as compensation for expenses/losses already incurred (with no future related costs), or for giving immediate financial support with no future related costs, is recognised in profit or loss of the period in which it becomes receivable.
  • Grants of non-monetary assets (e.g., land, other resources) are usually accounted for at fair value of the non-monetary asset; both the asset and the grant may alternatively be recorded at a nominal amount.

Ind AS 20 — Presentation of Grants Related to Assets

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Grants related to assets, including non-monetary grants at fair value, are presented in the balance sheet under EITHER of two permitted methods (accounting policy choice, applied consistently):
  1. Deferred income method: Grant is set up as deferred income and recognised in profit or loss on a systematic basis (usually straight-line) over the useful life of the asset.
  2. Deduction from carrying amount method: Grant is deducted in arriving at the carrying amount of the asset; it is recognised in profit or loss over the life of the depreciable asset via reduced depreciation charge.
  • Concept: Both methods produce the same net profit or loss impact over the asset's life — they differ only in balance sheet presentation (deferred income liability vs. reduced asset carrying amount) and in gross vs. net asset/depreciation figures shown.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: ⚠️ VERIFY — some older ICAI/coaching material states Ind AS 20 (unlike IAS 20) permits only the deferred income presentation for asset-related grants; current MCA-notified Ind AS 20 para 24 text (as verified) permits both methods identical to IAS 20 — confirm against the latest ICAI FR study material/RTP applicable to your attempt before relying on "only one method" in an answer.

Ind AS 20 — Presentation of Grants Related to Income

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Grants related to income are presented in the statement of profit and loss EITHER:
  1. Separately, or under a general heading such as "Other Income"; OR
  2. Deducted from the related expense in reporting it.
  • Concept: As with asset-related grants, this is a presentation choice only — profit for the period is unaffected either way.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Grants recognised in P&L may either be shown under "Other Income" OR alternatively deducted from the related expense — pick one policy and disclose it; do not mix approaches for similar grants.

Ind AS 20 — Refund of Government Grants

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Grant related to income: Refund is first applied against any unamortised deferred credit (deferred income) balance remaining in respect of the grant; any excess of the refund over that unamortised balance is recognised immediately in profit or loss.
  • Grant related to an asset (recognised by deducting from carrying amount): Refund is accounted for by increasing the carrying amount of the asset. The cumulative additional depreciation that would have been recognised in profit or loss to date, had the grant never reduced the asset's cost, is recognised immediately in profit or loss on refund.
  • Concept: A refund of a government grant is treated as a change in estimate under Ind AS 8 — it reverses the original accounting rather than being restated retrospectively.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Exam numericals commonly test the "catch-up" additional depreciation adjustment on refund of an asset-related grant — do not forget to immediately expense the cumulative extra depreciation that would have been charged absent the grant.

Ind AS 20 — Government Loan at Below-Market Rate of Interest

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Government Grant (Benefit) = Fair Value of Loan on Initial Recognition (per Ind AS 109) - Actual Proceeds Received

  • The loan itself is measured and subsequently accounted for per Ind AS 109 (initially at fair value, generally using the market rate of interest for a similar instrument).
  • The benefit (grant) so computed is transferred to deferred income and recognised in profit or loss on a systematic basis over the periods in which the entity recognises the related expenses that the loan benefit is intended to compensate.
  • Concept: The below-market-rate benefit on a government loan is itself treated as a government grant, layered on top of normal Ind AS 109 loan accounting.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Do not record the loan at transaction (face) value — it must first be fair-valued under Ind AS 109; only the resulting fair-value shortfall (vs. proceeds) is the grant.

Ind AS 21 — Key Currency Definitions

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • An entity measures all its assets, liabilities, equity, income and expenses in its functional currency; any transaction in a currency other than the functional currency is a foreign currency transaction.
  • Functional currency = the currency of the primary economic environment in which the entity operates — normally the environment in which it primarily generates and expends cash; usually (but not always) the currency of the country where the entity is located.
  • Presentation currency = the currency in which the financial statements are presented; it may differ from the functional currency.
  • Concept: Functional currency is a factual determination based on economic substance, not a free management choice, whereas presentation currency is an accounting policy choice.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Functional currency, once determined, is not an arbitrary election — it must be reassessed only if the underlying transactions/events/conditions genuinely change.

Ind AS 21 — Determining Functional Currency (Indicators)

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): Primary indicators (given priority) —
  • Currency that mainly influences sales prices of goods/services (currency in which sales prices are denominated & settled; also the currency of the country whose competitive forces/regulations mainly determine sales prices).
  • Currency that mainly influences labour, material and other costs of providing goods/services (currency in which these costs are denominated & settled).
  • Secondary indicators (supporting evidence only) —
  • Currency in which funds from financing activities (debt/equity issuance) are generated.
  • Currency in which receipts from operating activities are usually retained.
  • Concept: When indicators are mixed and the functional currency is not obvious, management uses judgement, giving priority to the primary indicators over the secondary ones.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Exam MCQs/theory often test that financing-currency and receipts-retention currency are only secondary (supporting) evidence — do not treat them as decisive on their own.

Ind AS 21 — Monetary vs Non-Monetary Items

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Monetary items = units of currency held, and assets/liabilities to be received or paid in a fixed or determinable number of units of currency. Examples: cash; pensions and other employee benefits payable in cash; provisions to be settled in cash; lease liabilities; cash dividends recognised as a liability; most debt securities (fixed/determinable contractual cash flows).
  • Non-monetary items = absence of a right to receive (or obligation to deliver) a fixed/determinable number of currency units. Examples: prepaid expenses/income received in advance (where no cash will be paid/received in future); goodwill; intangible assets; inventories; PPE; right-of-use assets; provisions to be settled by delivery of a non-monetary asset.
  • Concept: The monetary/non-monetary distinction drives the entire subsequent measurement and exchange-difference recognition framework under Ind AS 21.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Lease liabilities and provisions settled in cash are monetary (common trap: students sometimes treat lease liability as non-monetary because the ROU asset side is non-monetary).

Ind AS 21 — Initial & Subsequent Measurement of Foreign Currency Items

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Initial Recognition (functional currency) = Foreign Currency Amount × Spot Exchange Rate on Transaction Date

  • At the end of each reporting period:
  • Monetary items → translated using the closing rate.
  • Non-monetary items measured at historical cost in a foreign currency → translated using the exchange rate at the date of the transaction (historical rate) — NOT retranslated.
  • Non-monetary items measured at fair value in a foreign currency → translated using the exchange rate at the date the fair value was measured.
  • Where carrying amount of a non-monetary asset is determined by comparing two amounts (e.g., inventory at lower of cost and NRV; an impaired asset at lower of carrying amount and recoverable amount):
  • The historical-cost-based amount is translated at the historical rate.
  • The comparator (NRV / recoverable amount), being determined in foreign currency at the reporting date, is translated at the closing rate.
  • The lower of the two functional-currency amounts (after translation) is the carrying amount.
  • Concept: The translation basis always mirrors the underlying measurement basis of the item (historical cost stays at historical rate; fair value moves with the rate at the fair value date; monetary items always float with the closing rate).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Worked example logic: an asset costing €200,000 recorded at ₹52/€ = ₹104 lakh carrying amount (historical rate, ignoring depreciation); recoverable amount of €175,000 at closing rate ₹60/€ = ₹105 lakh; since translated recoverable amount (₹105 lakh) exceeds carrying amount (₹104 lakh), impairment loss = NIL.
  • Do NOT retranslate the historical-cost limb of the comparison at the closing rate — only the NRV/recoverable-amount limb uses the closing rate.

Ind AS 21 — Recognition of Exchange Differences

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Monetary items: Exchange differences arising on settlement or on translation at rates different from the rate at initial recognition (or previous reporting date) are recognised in profit or loss in the period in which they arise.
  • Non-monetary items: The exchange component follows the item's own gain/loss recognition —
  • If the non-monetary item's gain/loss is recognised in OCI, its exchange component is also recognised in OCI.
  • If the non-monetary item's gain/loss is recognised in profit or loss, its exchange component is also recognised in profit or loss.
  • Concept: For non-monetary items, exchange differences are never isolated from the primary gain/loss — they "piggyback" on wherever the host gain/loss is recognised.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: A common exam trap is to route all exchange gains/losses through P&L regardless of item type — always check whether the underlying non-monetary item's remeasurement gain/loss is an OCI item (e.g., FVTOCI equity instruments, revaluation surplus on PPE) before deciding.

Ind AS 21 — Change in Functional Currency

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • A change in functional currency is accounted for prospectively from the date of change (never retrospectively).
  • On change, all items are translated into the new functional currency using the exchange rate at the date of change; for non-monetary items, the resulting translated amount becomes their new "historical cost" going forward.
  • Concept: Functional currency changes only when there is a genuine change in the underlying transactions, events or conditions — not by management choice alone.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Do not restate prior periods on a functional-currency change — only the translation basis going forward changes.

Ind AS 21 — Translation into a Presentation Currency (incl. Foreign Operations)

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • When financial statements are translated into a presentation currency different from the functional currency (this also applies to translating a foreign operation into the reporting entity's presentation currency for consolidation):
  • All assets and liabilities (including comparatives) → translated at the closing rate at the date of each balance sheet presented.
  • Income and expenses (including comparatives) → translated at the exchange rates at the dates of the transactions; an average rate for the period may be used if it is a reasonable approximation.
  • All resulting exchange differences are recognised in OCI and accumulated in a separate component of equity — the Foreign Currency Translation Reserve (FCTR).
  • Where the exchange differences relate to a foreign operation that is consolidated but not wholly owned, the accumulated exchange differences attributable to NCI are allocated to, and recognised as part of, NCI in the consolidated balance sheet.
  • Concept: This is the standard "closing rate method" used to bring a foreign subsidiary/branch's financial statements into the group's presentation currency at consolidation.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Choice of presentation currency is an accounting policy under Ind AS 8 — a change must be applied retrospectively (unlike a change in functional currency, which is prospective), unless retrospective application is impracticable.

Ind AS 21 — Intra-Group Transactions & Net Investment in a Foreign Operation

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • On consolidation, intra-group monetary balances (receivable/payable, loans) are eliminated, but the related foreign exchange gain/loss is NOT eliminated — the group still bears the currency exposure through the counterparty's need to obtain/hold foreign currency.
  • A monetary item receivable from/payable to a foreign operation whose settlement is neither planned nor likely to occur in the foreseeable future has the same economic effect as a capital contribution and forms part of the net investment in a foreign operation. (Excludes ordinary trade receivables/payables.)
  • Treatment of exchange difference on such intra-group monetary items:
  • Separate/individual financial statements (SFS/IFS): Exchange difference is recognised in profit or loss regardless of whether settlement is planned.
  • Consolidated financial statements (CFS):
  • If settlement is neither planned nor likely (net-investment item): exchange difference is recognised initially in OCI, accumulated in equity (FCTR), and reclassified from equity to profit or loss only on disposal of the net investment.
  • If settlement is planned/likely to occur in the foreseeable future: exchange difference is recognised in consolidated profit or loss (not OCI), consistent with SFS treatment.
  • Concept: A long-term intercompany loan is treated as quasi-equity (net investment) for CFS purposes only when management has genuinely not planned repayment — long duration alone is not sufficient; absent an expressed intention, settlement is presumed to be planned in the foreseeable future.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: In the standard illustration (Parent lends USD to Subsidiary, EUR strengthens against USD from 1.5 to 2.0 per USD over the year), the EUR 50 exchange loss survives elimination in CFS and is taken to consolidated P&L unless the loan is part of net investment, in which case it goes to OCI/FCTR instead.

Ind AS 21 — Goodwill & Fair Value Adjustments on Business Combination

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Goodwill and any fair value adjustments to the carrying amounts of assets/liabilities arising on acquisition of a foreign operation are treated as assets and liabilities of the foreign operation.
  • They are therefore expressed in the functional currency of the foreign operation and translated at the closing rate at each reporting date, like other assets/liabilities of that foreign operation.
  • Concept: Goodwill on a foreign acquisition is NOT frozen at the acquisition-date rate — it is retranslated every period along with the rest of the foreign operation's net assets, so it fluctuates with exchange rate movements through OCI/FCTR.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: A frequent error is translating goodwill at the historical (acquisition-date) rate as if it were a non-monetary item measured at historical cost in the parent's currency — it must instead be treated as a foreign-operation asset and retranslated at the closing rate.

Ind AS 21 — Disposal / Partial Disposal of Foreign Operations

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Full disposal (other than a subsidiary, e.g. associate/JV/branch fully disposed): Cumulative exchange differences previously recognised in OCI and accumulated in equity (FCTR) are reclassified from equity to profit or loss (a reclassification adjustment) on disposal.
  • Full disposal of a foreign subsidiary resulting in loss of control (including a partial disposal that still results in loss of control):
  • Parent's share of cumulative FCTR is reclassified to profit or loss.
  • NCI's share of the cumulative exchange differences is derecognised but NOT reclassified to profit or loss.
  • Partial disposal of a foreign subsidiary WITHOUT loss of control: The entity re-attributes the proportionate share of the cumulative FCTR to NCI (transfer within equity; no P&L impact).
  • Partial disposal of a foreign operation other than a subsidiary (e.g., associate/JV, retaining significant influence/joint control): Only the proportionate share of the cumulative FCTR is reclassified to profit or loss.
  • Concept: Whether cumulative FCTR moves to P&L, to NCI, or partly both depends entirely on (a) whether the foreign operation is a subsidiary, and (b) whether the disposal results in loss of control/significant influence/joint control.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Worked ratios from the standard illustrations: full disposal of a 100%-owned subsidiary with loss of control → entire parent-share FCTR balance reclassified to P&L; sale of 10% (out of 100%) retaining control → 10% of FCTR reclassified to NCI; sale of 5% out of a 35% associate stake, retaining significant influence → 5/35 (one-seventh) of FCTR reclassified to P&L.
  • A write-down of a foreign operation's carrying amount due to its own losses or an impairment recognised by the investor is NOT a disposal — no part of the OCI-recognised FCTR is reclassified to profit or loss merely because of a write-down.

Ind AS 21 — Difference in Reporting Dates of Foreign Operation

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Ideally the foreign operation prepares additional statements as at the reporting entity's reporting date.
  • If not prepared, Ind AS 110 permits using the foreign operation's financial statements drawn up to a different date, provided the difference is not greater than three months, with adjustments made for the effects of significant transactions/events occurring between the two dates.
  • Assets and liabilities of the foreign operation are translated at the exchange rate at the end of the foreign operation's own reporting period, adjusted for significant exchange rate movements up to the reporting entity's reporting date (per Ind AS 110). A similar approach applies under the equity method for associates/JVs per Ind AS 28.
  • Concept: This three-month tolerance is a consolidation-mechanics rule (sourced in Ind AS 110, cross-referenced here) rather than a standalone Ind AS 21 threshold.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: The "3-month" cap and the "adjust for significant transactions" requirement are tested together — do not apply the shortcut date without also checking for significant intervening events.

Ind AS 21 — Tax Effect of Exchange Differences

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Tax effects of gains/losses on foreign currency transactions, and of exchange differences arising on translating the results and financial position of an entity (including a foreign operation) into a different currency, are accounted for under Ind AS 12 Income Taxes (not within Ind AS 21 itself).
  • Concept: Ind AS 21 determines the exchange difference; Ind AS 12 determines its tax consequence (current/deferred tax, including on OCI-routed FCTR movements).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Where an exchange difference is recognised in OCI (e.g., FCTR), its related tax effect is also recognised in OCI per Ind AS 12's "follows the item" principle.

08. Ind AS 23 & Ind AS 24 - Borrowing Costs and Related Party Disclosures

Where it fits: Ind AS 23 governs when interest/borrowing cost is capitalised into the cost of a qualifying asset instead of being expensed — a standalone numerical favourite and a feed-in to PP&E/CWIP questions; Ind AS 24 governs who counts as a related party and what must be disclosed about dealings with them — mostly theory/identification-based, frequently tested with practical relationship scenarios.

A. Ind AS 23 — Scope & Key Definitions

Key rule(s):

  • Applies to accounting for borrowing costs; does not apply to the actual or imputed cost of equity, including preference capital not classified as a liability under Ind AS 32.
  • The general capitalisation requirement is not mandatory for:
  • (a) qualifying assets measured at fair value (e.g., a biological asset under Ind AS 41); and
  • (b) inventories manufactured/produced in large quantities on a repetitive basis, even if they take a substantial period to complete (exemption exists because allocating and tracking borrowing costs for such inventory is impracticable).

Concept: Borrowing costs are interest and other costs an entity incurs in connection with the borrowing of funds — e.g., interest computed using the effective interest rate (EIR) method per Ind AS 109, finance charges on a finance lease per Ind AS 116, and exchange differences from foreign-currency borrowings to the extent regarded as an interest-cost adjustment (see Section D below).

Definitions

TermMeaning
Borrowing costsInterest and other costs incurred by an entity in connection with borrowing of funds
Qualifying asset (QA)An asset that necessarily takes a substantial period of time to get ready for its intended use or sale

Pitfalls / exam tips

  • ⚠️ VERIFY — Ind AS 23 does not define "substantial period"; judgement is required on facts. Practitioner commentary commonly treats 12 months or more as an indicative benchmark, but this figure is not stated in the standard itself — do not quote it as a bright-line rule in the exam; state that it is a matter of judgement.
  • Examples of QA: manufacturing plants, power generation facilities, intangible assets under development, investment properties, bearer plants.
  • NOT a QA: inventory produced over a short period, financial assets (e.g. investments), and assets that are ready for intended use/sale at acquisition.
  • Dividend on equity shares, cost of issuing equity shares, and cost of preference capital not classified as a liability are outside the scope of "borrowing cost."

B. Treatment & Eligibility for Capitalisation

Key rule(s):

  • Borrowing costs directly attributable to the acquisition, construction or production of a QA → capitalised as part of the cost of that asset.
  • All other borrowing costs → recognised as an expense in the period incurred.

Key formula(s) — Specific borrowing:

Borrowing Costs Eligible for Capitalisation = Actual Borrowing Costs Incurred - Investment Income on Temporary Investment of Those Borrowings

Key formula(s) — General borrowing (pool funding):

Step 1 — Capitalisation Rate (CR):

CR = Weighted Average Borrowing Cost on Outstanding Borrowings of the Entity (excluding specific borrowings)Total Outstanding Borrowings of the Entity during the Period (excluding specific borrowings)

Step 2 — Borrowing cost to be capitalised:

Borrowing Cost Capitalised = CR × Expenditure Incurred on the Qualifying Asset

Concept: A specific borrowing is one taken expressly to fund a particular QA — its own cost (net of any income earned by temporarily parking the unspent funds) is capitalised. A general borrowing is any borrowing not raised specifically for a QA; when a QA is funded out of a general pool, a weighted-average capitalisation rate is applied to QA expenditure to arrive at the amount to capitalise.

Pitfalls / exam tips

  • Ceiling rule: borrowing cost capitalised in a period must never exceed the actual borrowing cost incurred by the entity during that period — always cap the general-borrowing computation against total actual interest cost.
  • Exclude specific-borrowing amounts and their cost from both numerator and denominator of the CR formula — mixing them is the most common calculation error.
  • If QA expenditure exceeds total borrowings available in the pool, capitalise only up to actual borrowing costs incurred (ceiling applies).

C. Period of Capitalisation

Key rule(s):

  • Commencement — begins only when all three conditions are met simultaneously:
  1. Expenditure on the QA has been incurred;
  2. Borrowing costs have been incurred; and
  3. Activities necessary to prepare the asset for its intended use or sale are in progress (includes not just physical construction but also technical work, legal/registration formalities, and site-preparation planning).
  • Suspension — capitalisation is suspended during extended periods in which active development is interrupted.
  • Not suspended for a temporary delay that is a necessary part of the process of getting the asset ready (e.g., a technologically required curing/settling period).
  • Cessation — capitalisation ceases when substantially all activities necessary to prepare the QA for its intended use or sale are complete.
  • Minor outstanding work (e.g., cosmetic decoration) does not prevent treating the asset as substantially complete.
  • Where a QA is completed in separately usable parts, capitalisation ceases part-by-part as each part is completed and ready for use.

Concept: Only the period during which funds are actively being used to bring the asset to a usable/saleable state attracts capitalised interest — idle borrowing cost during genuine construction stoppages must be expensed.

Pitfalls / exam tips

  • Test each of the three commencement conditions separately in numericals — a common trap is expenditure incurred before borrowing is drawn (no capitalisation until the borrowing cost condition is also met).
  • Distinguish "necessary technical delay" (continue capitalising) from "development stalled/on hold" (suspend) — this is a frequent theory-cum-practical question.

D. Exchange Differences as Borrowing Cost & Other Concepts

Key rule(s):

  • Exchange differences arising on foreign-currency borrowings, to the extent they represent an adjustment to interest cost (i.e., approximate the interest differential that would have arisen had the entity borrowed in local currency at a comparable rate), are treated as borrowing cost and capitalised/expensed following the normal Ind AS 23 rules.
  • Reversal note: if an exchange loss was treated as a borrowing-cost adjustment in the current year, and in a subsequent year an exchange gain arises on the same borrowing, that gain must be netted off against interest to the extent of the loss previously capitalised/expensed as interest (i.e., the gain is not recognised beyond reversing the earlier loss treatment).
  • Dividends on preference shares classified as financial liabilities (per Ind AS 32) meet the definition of borrowing cost.
  • Group financial statements: where a QA is developed by one group entity but the borrowing sits in another group entity's books, it may be appropriate to capitalise the interest in the consolidated financial statements even though it appears in a different entity's separate books; however, the entity actually carrying out the development cannot capitalise interest in its own separate financial statements unless it itself has borrowings (including intra-group borrowings) on which interest is capitalised.
  • Maturing inventories (e.g., whisky/wine): borrowing costs may continue to be capitalised as long as it can be demonstrated that the inventory's value keeps rising solely due to ageing, and not due to market/inflation factors. If this cannot be demonstrated, the inventory is treated as held for sale and further capitalisation stops.

Pitfalls / exam tips

  • Exchange-difference-as-interest is a classic adjustment question in FR — always check for a subsequent-year reversal of a prior loss via a gain before finalising interest cost.
  • Remember: the developing entity capitalises interest only on its own (including intra-group) borrowings, never on borrowings that legally belong to another group company, in its standalone financial statements.

E. Ind AS 24 — Objective, Scope & Disclosure Exemption

Key rule(s):

  • Objective: ensure financial statements disclose the possibility that profit/loss and financial position may have been affected by the existence of related parties and by transactions/outstanding balances/commitments with them.
  • Scope covers: identifying related party relationships, related party transactions, outstanding balances, commitments, the circumstances requiring disclosure, and the disclosures themselves.
  • Disclosures are made in: (a) the entity's individual (standalone) financial statements, and (b) the consolidated and separate financial statements of a parent.
  • Disclosure not required when either:
  • it would conflict with the entity's duties of confidentiality (e.g., a bank's statutory duty of customer confidentiality); or
  • the entity is prohibited by statute from disclosing.

Concept: A related party relationship can distort figures either by influencing transactions that would not occur with unrelated parties (e.g., sale at cost to a parent) or by influencing dealings with genuinely unrelated third parties (e.g., a group directing purchases toward a fellow subsidiary).

Pitfalls / exam tips

  • The confidentiality/statute exemption is narrow — it does not exempt disclosure merely because the related party would prefer non-disclosure.

F. Who Is a Related Party — Person-to-Entity Relationships

Key rule(s): A person (or a close family member of that person) is related to the reporting entity if the person:

  • has control or joint control over the reporting entity;
  • has significant influence over the reporting entity; or
  • is a member of the Key Management Personnel (KMP) of the reporting entity, or of a parent of the reporting entity.

Definitions

TermMeaning
KMPPersons having authority and responsibility for planning, directing and controlling the activities of the entity, directly or indirectly — includes any director (executive or non-executive)
Close members of the familyThose who may be expected to influence, or be influenced by, the person in dealings with the entity — includes the person's children, spouse/domestic partner, brother, sister, father and mother; children of the spouse/domestic partner; and dependents of the person or of the spouse/domestic partner

Pitfalls / exam tips

  • A non-executive director still qualifies as KMP — do not restrict KMP to executive/whole-time directors.
  • If a person is related to the entity, close family members of that person are automatically also related parties (via that person) — apply this transitively in relationship-chain questions.

G. Who Is a Related Party — Entity-to-Entity Relationships

Key rule(s): Two entities are related if any of the following holds:

  • Both are members of the same group (parent, subsidiary and fellow subsidiary are all related to one another).
  • One entity is an associate or joint venture of the other, or of another member of a group of which the other entity is a member.
  • Both entities are joint ventures of the same third party.
  • One entity is a joint venture of a third entity and the other is an associate of that same third entity.
  • The entity is a post-employment benefit plan for employees of the reporting entity or of an entity related to it.
  • The entity is controlled or jointly controlled by a person identified under Section F.
  • A person with control/joint control over the entity is also a member of KMP of the other entity (or its parent).
  • The entity, or any group member, provides KMP services to the reporting entity or its parent.
  • An associate is deemed to include the associate's own subsidiaries, and a joint venture is deemed to include the joint venture's own subsidiaries, for purposes of this definition.

Concept: The definition is deliberately wide and exhaustive, emphasising substance over legal form — relationships are traced through control, joint control, significant influence and KMP linkages, not merely through direct shareholding.

Pitfalls / exam tips

  • Note the asymmetry in the associate/JV extension: an associate's or JV's own subsidiaries are pulled into the related-party net, but the associate's/JV's own further associates or joint ventures are not automatically related parties of the original reporting entity (test this carefully in multi-tier group scenarios).
  • KMP-services-provider chains (an entity providing KMP services to a parent) make that provider related to the parent and all its subsidiaries/associates, not just the immediate recipient.

H. Who Is NOT a Related Party

Key rule(s): The following are not, by themselves, related-party relationships:

  • Two entities sharing a common director/KMP, or a KMP of one having significant influence over the other, with no other interest and no transactions between them.
  • Two venturers merely because they jointly control the same joint venture (the venturers are not related to each other).
  • Providers of finance, trade unions, public utilities, and government departments/agencies that do not control/jointly control/significantly influence the entity, purely by virtue of their normal dealings (even if this affects the entity's freedom of action).
  • A customer, supplier, franchisor, distributor or general agent, purely because of the resulting economic dependence from a significant volume of business.

Pitfalls / exam tips

  • If facts show that a common-director scenario also involves potential control/significant influence in substance (e.g., one entity buys 100% of another's output at non-arm's-length or dominant terms), re-test — the "not related" carve-out only holds when the relationship is genuinely at arm's length with no other interest.
  • A bank holding a non-executive observer seat on the board solely under a loan covenant remains a non-related finance provider.

I. Related Party Transactions & Disclosure Requirements

Key rule(s):

  • A related party transaction is a transfer of resources, services or obligations between the reporting entity and a related party, regardless of whether a price is charged — consideration need not pass for the transaction to be a related-party transaction.

Category 1 — Parent/subsidiary relationship disclosure:

  • Disclose the name of the entity's parent, and, if different, the ultimate controlling party (which may be a person).
  • If neither the parent nor the ultimate controlling party produces consolidated financial statements available for public use, disclose the name of the next most senior parent that does produce such statements.

Category 2(i) — KMP compensation:

  • Disclose total compensation to KMP.
  • If the entity obtains KMP services from a separate management entity, it is not required to disclose the compensation that management entity pays to its own employees/directors for providing those services.

Category 2(ii) — Minimum disclosures where related party transactions occurred during the year (in addition to the above):

  • The nature of the related party relationship; and information about the transactions/balances/commitments, including at minimum:
  • the amount of the transactions;
  • the amount of outstanding balances, including commitments, their terms and conditions (including whether secured) and the nature of consideration to be provided in settlement, plus details of any guarantees given or received;
  • provisions for doubtful debts related to such outstanding balances; and
  • the expense recognised during the period for bad or doubtful debts due from related parties.
  • A statement that related party transactions were on an arm's-length basis should be made only if such terms can be substantiated/verified.

Pitfalls / exam tips

  • "Regardless of whether a price is charged" is a favourite trick — a free-of-charge transfer of goods/services between related parties is still disclosable.
  • Do not confuse Category 1 (structural parent/ultimate-controlling-party disclosure, always required) with Category 2 (transaction-driven disclosures, required only when transactions/balances exist).

J. Exemption for Government-Related Entities

Key rule(s):

  • A reporting entity is exempt from the disclosure requirements otherwise applicable to related party transactions, outstanding balances and commitments with:
  • (a) a government that has control, joint control or significant influence over the reporting entity; AND
  • (b) another entity that is a related party only because the same government has control, joint control or significant influence over both the reporting entity and that other entity.
  • Even where the exemption applies, the entity must still disclose:
  • the name of the government and the nature of its relationship with the entity; and
  • sufficient detail to let users understand the effect of related-party transactions, including the nature and amount of each individually significant transaction, and for transactions that are not individually significant but are collectively significant, a qualitative or quantitative indication of their extent.

Concept: This exemption addresses the practical burden on government-controlled entities (e.g., PSUs) of disclosing every dealing with other entities under common government control, while still requiring transparency about genuinely significant transactions.

Pitfalls / exam tips

  • The exemption relieves the volume of disclosure, not disclosure of significant items — individually significant transactions with fellow government-controlled entities must still be disclosed in detail.
  • Judgement is applied by the reporting entity itself in deciding the level of detail needed for the "qualitative or quantitative indication" of collectively significant transactions.

  • Concept: Bonus issue and share split increase the number of shares with no additional resources; reverse split (consolidation) reduces shares with no reduction in resources. In all three cases, adjust the number of shares outstanding as if the event occurred at the beginning of the earliest period presented — irrespective of the actual date of the event — and restate all prior-period EPS figures accordingly.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: This is a pure retrospective adjustment (no time-weighting needed) — apply to both current and comparative year share counts.

6. Rights Issue

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): Rights issue has two elements — a bonus element (discount below theoretical value, treated like a bonus issue) and a "shares issued for consideration" element (treated like a normal fresh issue at fair value).

Step 1 — Theoretical ex-rights fair value per share:

Theoretical Ex-Rights Fair Value per Share = Fair Value of Shares Before Exercise of Rights + Total Amount Received from Rights IssueNo. of Shares Outstanding after Exercise of Rights

Step 2 — Bonus factor for rights issue:

Bonus Factor = Fair Value per Share Immediately Before Exercise of RightsTheoretical Ex-Rights Fair Value per Share

Step 3: Apply this Bonus Factor to the shares outstanding before the rights issue, for both the current and the previous (comparative) year.

  • Concept: The bonus factor isolates the "free" (discounted) portion of the rights issue so that only the genuinely new-money shares are treated as a normal fresh issue (time-weighted from the actual exercise date), while the discount portion is retrospectively applied like a bonus issue.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Fair value used is the market price of the share cum-rights (immediately before the rights issue), not the issue/exercise price.
  • Bonus factor > 1 always (since ex-rights value < cum-rights value when rights are issued at a discount).

7. Partly Paid-Up Shares

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): Partly paid ordinary shares are treated in Basic EPS as a fraction of a fully paid ordinary share, to the extent they were entitled to participate in dividends relative to a fully paid share during the period.
  • Concept: Weights partly-paid shares down proportionately to their paid-up entitlement so EPS reflects the actual dividend/participation rights.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Disclosure is required separately for each class of share.

8. Diluted EPS — Scope, Meaning and Formula

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Diluted EPS = Profit/Loss Attributable to ESH ± Effect of Dilutive Potential Shares on EarningsWeighted Avg. No. of Equity Shares + Weighted Avg. No. of Dilutive Potential Ordinary Shares

  • Concept: Diluted EPS adjusts both numerator and denominator of Basic EPS for the effect of all dilutive potential ordinary shares (financial instruments/contracts that could result in issue of ordinary shares at the option of issuer or holder — e.g., convertible debentures, convertible preference shares, options/warrants including ESOPs, contingently issuable shares).
  • Definitions:
TermMeaning
Potential ordinary shareA financial instrument/contract that might result in issue of ordinary shares to its holder, at the option of the issuer or holder
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Dilutive potential shares are deemed converted at the beginning of the period or the date of issue of the potential shares, whichever is later.
  • Potential shares cancelled/lapsed during the period → included in Diluted EPS only for the portion of the period they were outstanding.
  • Potential shares converted during the period → included in Diluted EPS from the beginning of the period to the conversion date; from conversion date onward, resulting shares go into both Basic and Diluted EPS.
  • Assume the most advantageous conversion rate/exercise price from the holder's standpoint.
  • Only dilutive potential shares are used — dilutive means conversion would decrease EPS / increase loss per share from continuing operations; anti-dilutive effects are ignored.

9. Steps to Calculate Diluted EPS (Ranking Method)

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Incremental EPS = Effect of Potential Share on Earnings to Equity HoldersEffect of Potential Share on Weighted Avg. No. of Shares

  • Step 1: Calculate Incremental EPS for each potential ordinary share (considered separately, not in aggregate).
  • Step 2: Rank each potential share — the lowest Incremental EPS gets Rank 1 (most dilutive); rank the rest in ascending order.
  • Step 3: Add the effect of each potential share to Basic EPS one at a time, in rank order. If EPS decreases on adding an instrument → dilutive; if it increases → anti-dilutive. Once anti-dilutive, stop testing further (no need to test remaining instruments once EPS starts rising, since further instruments only add to Incremental EPS in ascending order).
  • Step 4: Diluted EPS = Basic EPS adjusted only for the dilutive potential shares identified in Step 3.
  • Concept: Because the sequence of considering instruments affects whether each is dilutive, always work from most dilutive (lowest incremental EPS) to least dilutive to maximise dilution.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips (Alternate shortcut test):
  • Convertible preference shares are anti-dilutive if Incremental EPS (preference dividend saved per share obtainable on conversion) exceeds Basic EPS.
  • Convertible debt is anti-dilutive if Incremental EPS (interest net of tax per share obtainable on conversion) exceeds Basic EPS.

10. Options, Warrants and Employee Stock Options

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): Treasury-stock-style approach — the incremental shares for Diluted EPS =

Incremental Shares = No. of Shares Issuable on Exercise - ≤ft(Exercise Price × No. of Shares IssuableAverage Market Price during the Period)

Only this incremental (no-consideration) portion is added to the denominator for Diluted EPS.

  • Concept: Options/warrants have a dilutive effect only when the average market price of the ordinary shares during the period exceeds the exercise price; if exercise price ≥ average market price, they are anti-dilutive and ignored.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Previously reported EPS is never retroactively adjusted for later changes in share price.
  • Employee share options with fixed/determinable terms (and non-vested shares) are treated as options from the grant date, even if contingent on vesting.
  • Performance-based employee share options are treated as contingently issuable shares (contingent on more than passage of time).

11. Contingently Issuable Shares

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): Included in Diluted EPS based on the number of shares that would be issuable if the end of the reporting period were the end of the contingency period, assuming the outcome is as at that date; if conditions are not met by period end, no shares are included (unless already included via Basic EPS date-of-inclusion rules).
  • Concept: These are contracts where issuance depends on satisfying specified future conditions (often tied to a future date, earnings target, or market price).

12. Retrospective Adjustments — What Restates, What Does Not

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s) (rules):
  • Diluted EPS of prior periods is NOT restated for changed assumptions on contingently issuable shares, or for conversion of potential ordinary shares (e.g., convertible debt) outstanding at the end of the previous period — these were already factored into that period's own EPS.
  • Prior-period EPS IS restated for the effects of errors and retrospective accounting policy changes (per Ind AS 8).
  • Both Basic and Diluted EPS of current and prior periods must reflect bonus issues, share splits, consolidations and similar events that change share count without a corresponding change in resources (full retrospective application).

13. Participating Equity Instruments and Two-Class Ordinary Shares

  • Concept: Some entities have instruments participating in dividends with ordinary shares per a pre-set formula (usually capped), or classes of ordinary shares with different dividend rates but no seniority. Profit/loss for the period is allocated across these classes and participating instruments according to their contractual dividend/participation rights before computing EPS for each class.

14. Entities with Discontinued Operations — the "Control Number"

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s) / rule: Income from continuing operations (attributable to ordinary equity holders of the parent, adjusted for preference dividends) is used as the "control number" to test whether potential ordinary shares are dilutive or anti-dilutive.
  • Concept: The same set of potential ordinary shares determined as dilutive for continuing operations must be used consistently to compute all other diluted per-share figures (discontinued operations, total operations) — even if that produces an anti-dilutive effect on those other figures viewed in isolation.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Basic and Diluted EPS must be presented separately for continuing operations, discontinued operations, and total operations on the face of the P&L.

PART B: IND AS 34 - INTERIM FINANCIAL REPORTING

15. Objective, Scope and Key Definitions

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s) (rules):
  • The Standard prescribes (a) the minimum content of an interim financial report, and (b) the recognition and measurement principles for interim-period financial statements.
  • It does not mandate which entities must publish interim reports, how frequently, or how soon after period-end — it applies only if an entity is required, or elects, to publish an interim financial report under Ind AS.
  • Definitions:
TermMeaning
Interim periodA financial reporting period shorter than a full financial year
Interim financial reportA report containing either a complete set of FS (per Ind AS 1) OR a set of condensed FS (per Ind AS 34) for an interim period

16. Minimum Contents of an Interim Financial Report

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): At minimum, an interim financial report must include:
  • A condensed Balance Sheet
  • A condensed Statement of Profit and Loss
  • A condensed Statement of Changes in Equity
  • A condensed Statement of Cash Flows
  • Notes comprising significant accounting policies, and other explanatory information
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: If condensed statements are presented, they must include at least the headings and subtotals from the most recent annual financial statements, plus selected explanatory notes — line items must not be so condensed as to be misleading.

17. Significant Events and Transactions — Disclosure

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): An entity must explain events/transactions significant to understanding changes since the last annual reporting date, e.g. (illustrative, not exhaustive):
  • Write-down of inventories to NRV, and reversal of such write-down
  • Recognition/reversal of impairment loss on financial assets, PPE, intangible assets
  • Reversal of restructuring cost provisions
  • Acquisitions and disposals of PPE
  • Litigation settlements
  • Corrections of prior period errors
  • Loan defaults or breaches
  • Related party transactions
  • Changes in classification of financial assets
  • Changes in contingent liabilities/contingent assets

18. Periods for Which Interim Financial Statements Are Presented

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Balance Sheet: end of current interim period, with comparative as at end of immediately preceding financial year.
  • Statement of Profit and Loss: current interim period + cumulative year-to-date, each with comparative figures for the corresponding interim/year-to-date period of the immediately preceding financial year.
  • Statement of Changes in Equity: cumulative current financial year-to-date, with comparative for the corresponding year-to-date period of the immediately preceding financial year.
  • Statement of Cash Flows: cumulative current financial year-to-date, with comparative for the corresponding year-to-date period of the immediately preceding financial year.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • For highly seasonal businesses, disclosing trailing-twelve-month information (current and prior) MAY BE useful, though not mandatory.
  • Quarterly reporters must show BOTH the 3-month current-quarter figures AND the 6-month (half-year-to-date) cumulative figures for the P&L (with matching comparatives) — a common numbers-mix-up in exam questions.

19. Recognition and Measurement Principles

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s) (rules):
  • Same accounting policies: Apply the same accounting policies in interim statements as in the annual financial statements, except for policy changes made after the date of the most recent annual FS.
  • Seasonal/cyclical/occasional revenue (e.g., dividend revenue, royalties, government grants): recognise when it occurs — do NOT defer/smooth it across interim periods just because it's uneven, if deferral would not be appropriate at year-end either.
  • Costs incurred unevenly (e.g., prepaid rent, insurance, property taxes): defer for interim reporting if and only if it would also be appropriate to defer that type of cost at financial year-end.

20. Measuring Interim Income Tax Expense

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Interim Period Tax Expense = Interim Period Pre-Tax Income × Estimated Average Annual Effective Income Tax Rate

  • Concept: Since income tax is assessed annually, interim tax expense uses the rate expected to apply to total annual earnings (reflecting the progressive/graduated rate structure expected for the full year), not merely the interim period's own marginal rate.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Include enacted or substantively enacted tax rate changes scheduled to take effect later in the year (per Ind AS 12 guidance on substantive enactment).
  • Re-estimate the average annual rate on a year-to-date basis at each interim date; disclose significant changes in estimate.
  • To the extent practicable, use a separate estimated rate for each taxing jurisdiction and for each category of income taxed differently (e.g., capital gains); if not practicable, a reasonable weighted average rate across jurisdictions/categories is acceptable.

21. Restatement of Previously Reported Interim Periods

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s) (rules): For a change in accounting policy (other than one with a transition method specified by a new Ind AS):
  • (a) Restate prior interim periods of the current financial year and comparable interim periods of prior financial years that will be restated in the annual FS per Ind AS 8; OR
  • (b) If it is impracticable to determine the cumulative effect at the beginning of the financial year, adjust prior/comparable interim periods prospectively from the earliest date practicable.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: A change in accounting estimate (not policy) during the current interim period is applied prospectively from the current interim period only — never restate prior interim periods for an estimate change.

22. Interim Reporting and Impairment

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s) (rule): An entity shall not reverse an impairment loss recognised in a previous interim period in respect of goodwill.
  • Concept: This is a specific carve-out — while Ind AS 36 impairment losses on other assets can sometimes be reversed on reassessment, goodwill impairment recognised at an interim date is never reversed, consistent with the annual-period prohibition on reversing goodwill impairment.

10. Ind AS 36, 37 & 38 - Impairment of Assets, Provisions & Contingencies, and Intangible Assets

Where it fits: These three standards form a connected "asset quality & obligation recognition" block — Ind AS 36 caps the carrying amount of assets at what they can genuinely recover, Ind AS 37 governs when a present obligation must be provided for (vs merely disclosed as contingent), and Ind AS 38 governs recognition/measurement/amortisation of the intangible assets whose impairment Ind AS 36 tests; all three interact heavily with Ind AS 16, Ind AS 103, Ind AS 105 and Ind AS 8.

PART A — IND AS 36: IMPAIRMENT OF ASSETS

Scope

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Ind AS 36 applies to all assets EXCEPT: inventories (Ind AS 2); contract assets (Ind AS 115); deferred tax assets (Ind AS 12); assets arising from employee benefits (Ind AS 19); financial assets within the scope of Ind AS 109; biological assets related to agricultural activity measured at fair value less costs to sell (Ind AS 41); non-current assets/disposal groups classified as held for sale (Ind AS 105).
  • It DOES apply to: investments in subsidiaries, associates and joint ventures accounted for at cost/equity method (Ind AS 110, Ind AS 28, Ind AS 111).
  • Concept: Ind AS 36 is the "backstop" standard ensuring assets are not carried above their recoverable amount.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Students often wrongly apply Ind AS 36 to financial assets covered under Ind AS 109 (which have their own ECL impairment model) — check scope carve-outs first.

Identifying Impairment & Recoverable Amount

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Asset is Impaired ⇔ Carrying Amount > Recoverable Amount

Recoverable Amount = Higher of Fair Value Less Costs of Disposal (FVLCTS), Value in Use (VIU)

  • Concept: Fair value (per Ind AS 113) is the exit price in an orderly market transaction; costs of disposal are incremental costs directly attributable to disposal (e.g., legal costs, stamp duty, removal costs) — a valuation fee is NOT a cost of disposal.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Do not deduct costs of disposal already recognised as liabilities/provisions elsewhere; do not include finance costs or income-tax expense as costs of disposal.
TermMeaning
Fair Value Less Costs of DisposalPrice receivable to sell an asset in an orderly transaction between market participants, less incremental disposal costs
Value in UsePresent value of future cash flows expected from an asset (or CGU)

Value in Use (VIU)

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

VIU = Σt=1n Estimated Future Cash Flowt(1+r)t

  • Cash flow projections must be based on forecasts covering a maximum period of 5 years, unless a longer period can be justified; cash flows beyond year 5 are extrapolated using a steady or declining growth rate (not increasing, unless justified).
  • Foreign currency cash flows: discount the foreign-currency cash flows using a discount rate appropriate to that currency to get a foreign-currency PV, then translate that PV into the reporting/home currency using the spot rate on the date of the VIU calculation.
  • Concept: VIU has two key inputs — estimation of future cash flows, and the discount rate (a pre-tax rate reflecting current market assessments of the time value of money and asset-specific risks).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Do not translate each year's cash flow at a forward/future spot rate and then discount — the OCR/standard approach is: discount in foreign currency first, translate the resulting present value at the spot rate on the valuation date.

When Both FVLCTS & VIU Need Not Be Calculated

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • If FVLCTS cannot be determined (no active market/no binding sale agreement), Recoverable Amount = VIU.
  • If there is no reason to believe VIU materially exceeds FVLCTS (e.g., the asset is held only for disposal, or near-term cash flows are insignificant relative to disposal value), Recoverable Amount = FVLCTS, and VIU need not be separately estimated.
  • Concept: These are practical shortcuts to avoid computing both measures when one is clearly a reasonable proxy for the higher amount.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: ⚠️ VERIFY — the reverse is NOT true: if VIU cannot be estimated, the standard does not default Recoverable Amount to FVLCTS; instead, Recoverable Amount itself is treated as not determinable for that asset (relevant when deciding whether to test at the CGU level instead).

Recognising & Measuring an Impairment Loss

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Impairment Loss = Carrying Amount - Recoverable Amount

  • Impairment loss is recognised immediately in P&L, unless the asset is carried at revalued amount under another Ind AS (e.g., Ind AS 16/38 revaluation model), in which case it is treated as a revaluation decrease (first against that asset's own revaluation surplus, balance to P&L).
  • After recognising the impairment loss, depreciation/amortisation for future periods is charged on the revised carrying amount (post-impairment) less residual value, over the remaining useful life.
  • Concept: The impairment loss immediately reduces the asset's carrying amount, and all future depreciation is recalculated on this lower base.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: A very common numerical trap — students forget to rebase depreciation after an impairment write-down and continue depreciating on the original cost.

Frequency of Impairment Testing

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Annual mandatory testing (irrespective of any indication) for: goodwill acquired in a business combination; intangible assets with an indefinite useful life; intangible assets not yet available for use.
  • All other assets: test only when there is an indication of impairment at the reporting date.
  • An intangible asset first recognised during the current period must be tested for impairment before the end of the current annual period.
  • Concept: Annual tests may be performed at any time during the year, provided it's performed at the same time every year.
  • Definitions:
Indicator typeExamples
ExternalMarket value decline; adverse changes in technology/market/economic/legal environment; rise in market interest rates affecting discount rate
InternalObsolescence/physical damage; asset idle or plans to discontinue/restructure/dispose early; useful life changed from indefinite to finite; worse-than-expected economic performance
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: These indicator lists are illustrative, not exhaustive/conclusive — an impairment indication can exist even if not listed.

Cash-Generating Units (CGU)

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • A CGU is the smallest identifiable group of assets that generates cash inflows largely independent of cash inflows from other assets/groups of assets.
  • Asset allocation: allocate only assets directly attributable or allocable on a reasonable & consistent basis; a liability is generally excluded from a CGU unless the recoverable amount cannot be determined without considering it (e.g., a decommissioning/restoration provision).
  • Concept: Used when an individual asset's recoverable amount cannot be estimated because the asset does not generate independent cash inflows.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: CGU identification should be consistent from period to period for the same asset/type of asset, unless a change is justified.

Allocation of Goodwill & Corporate Assets to CGUs

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Goodwill: does not generate independent cash flows → allocate to the CGU(s)/group of CGUs expected to benefit from the synergies of the combination.
  • If allocable to an individual CGU → test that CGU (with goodwill) for impairment.
  • If allocable only to a group of CGUs → (a) first test each individual CGU without goodwill; then (b) test the larger group of CGUs with goodwill.
  • Corporate assets (e.g., head-office building): same two-step logic — test individual CGUs without the corporate asset first, then the group with the corporate asset, if it cannot be allocated to a single CGU on a reasonable basis.
  • Concept: Both goodwill and corporate assets are "shared" assets that only make sense to test alongside the group(s) of CGUs they support.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Exam problems typically require a two-pass impairment test (individual CGUs first, then the combined group) — skipping the first pass is a frequent scoring error.

Impairment Loss for a CGU — Order of Allocation

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Impairment Loss for CGU = Carrying Amount of CGU - Recoverable Amount of CGU

  • Allocate the CGU's impairment loss in this strict order:
  1. First, reduce the carrying amount of goodwill allocated to the CGU (down to nil).
  2. Then, reduce the carrying amount of all other assets in the CGU (including corporate assets) pro rata on the basis of their carrying amounts — but never below the highest of: FVLCTS (if determinable), VIU (if determinable), or zero, for that individual asset.
  • Assets outside the scope of Ind AS 36 within the CGU (e.g., trade receivables, inventory, investments) are excluded from this allocation.
  • If an asset is tested individually and separately impaired, no further impairment loss from the CGU allocation is assigned to it.
  • If an individual asset's recoverable amount cannot be determined (VIU not determinable) and the CGU as a whole is not impaired, that individual asset is also treated as not impaired — even if its FVLCTS alone is below its carrying amount.
  • Concept: Goodwill absorbs the loss first because it has no independent recoverable amount of its own.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Do not allocate impairment loss to an asset below its own recoverable amount floor — this is a key numerical-question distinction from straight-line pro rata allocation.

Reversal of Impairment Loss

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Impairment loss on any asset other than goodwill can be reversed if indications show the loss has decreased or no longer exists (mirror-image indicators of the original impairment triggers: rising market value, favourable tech/market/economic/legal changes, falling interest rates, better-than-expected performance).
  • Impairment loss on goodwill is NEVER reversed.

Maximum Reversal = Carrying Amount (net of depreciation) had no impairment been recognised - Carrying Amount (net of depreciation) after impairment

  • Concept: Reversal is capped so the asset is never written back up beyond what its depreciated historical cost would have been absent the original impairment.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: A rise in VIU purely from the unwinding of discount (cash flows "coming closer" with the passage of time) does not justify reversal if the asset's service potential itself hasn't improved.

NCI Impact on Goodwill Impairment Testing

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Under Ind AS 103, NCI may be measured at (a) proportionate share of net assets (PSNA method) or (b) fair value.
  • Under the PSNA method, goodwill attributable to NCI is included in the CGU's recoverable amount but not recognised in its carrying amount → the carrying amount of goodwill must be grossed up (notionally) to include NCI's share before comparing to recoverable amount.
  • Any resulting impairment loss is allocated between parent and NCI in proportion to their respective ownership interests; the portion of loss attributable to NCI's (unrecognised) goodwill is not recognised — only the parent's share of goodwill impairment is actually booked.
  • Concept: This "gross-up" ensures like-for-like comparison of CA vs RA of the CGU when NCI's goodwill isn't on the books.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Under the fair-value method for NCI, goodwill is already grossed up in full, so no notional adjustment is needed.

PART B — IND AS 37: PROVISIONS, CONTINGENT LIABILITIES & CONTINGENT ASSETS

Obligations — Legal vs Constructive

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): A provision requires a present obligation — either:
  • Legal obligation: derived from a contract, legislation, or other operation of law.
  • Constructive obligation: derived from an entity's past practice, published policy, or sufficiently specific current statement, such that it has created a valid expectation in other parties that it will discharge those responsibilities.
  • Concept: Constructive obligations extend provisioning beyond strict legal liability to genuine business/reputational commitments.
TermMeaning
Legal obligationArises from a contract, legislation, or other operation of law
Constructive obligationArises from past practice/policy creating a valid expectation, even without a legal requirement
ProvisionA liability of uncertain timing or amount
Contingent liabilityA possible obligation from past events, confirmed only by uncertain future events, not wholly in the entity's control; OR a present obligation not recognised because outflow is not probable, or the amount can't be reliably measured

Recognition of a Provision

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): A provision is recognised only when all three conditions are met:
  1. The entity has a present obligation (legal or constructive) as a result of a past event;
  2. It is probable that an outflow of resources embodying economic benefits will be required to settle it; AND
  3. A reliable estimate can be made of the amount of the obligation.
  • Concept: All three conditions are cumulative — failing any one means no provision (assess for contingent liability disclosure instead).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Only obligations the entity has no realistic alternative but to settle qualify — if the entity can avoid the expenditure by its own future actions, no provision.
  • A proposed new law creates an obligation only when the law is virtually certain to be enacted, not merely proposed/tabled.
  • Warranty provision: past event = original sale. Land contamination: past event = original contaminating act (not the year of discovery/cleanup order).

Measurement of a Provision

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Best estimate:
  • Large population of items (e.g., warranties, refunds): use expected value, i.e., weight all possible outcomes by their probabilities.
  • Single/individual obligation: use the most likely outcome, adjusted for other possible outcomes if they would materially change the estimate.
  • Present value: discount to present value only when the time value of money is material; use a pre-tax discount rate reflecting current market assessments of time value of money and risks specific to the liability.
  • Risks & uncertainties: must be factored in — but do not create excessive provisions or deliberately overstate liabilities.
  • Future events: factor in only when there is sufficient objective evidence they will occur (e.g., a technology-driven cost reduction is only recognised in the provision if objectively evidenced — absent evidence, no adjustment).
  • Expected disposal of assets: gains on expected disposal are NOT netted against/recognised within the provision, even if closely linked to the event giving rise to the provision — such gains are recognised separately under the relevant standard (e.g., Ind AS 16) when the disposal actually occurs.
  • Concept: Measurement blends probability-weighting, discounting discipline, and a strict boundary against anticipating unrelated future gains.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Classic exam trap — a restructuring provision plus an expected gain on sale of related property: recognise only the committed closure cost as the provision; the sale gain is recognised only on actual disposal, never netted off in advance.

Use, Review & Reversal of Provisions

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • A provision can be used only for the expenditure for which it was originally recognised.
  • Reviewed at each reporting date and adjusted to the current best estimate; reversed in full if an outflow is no longer probable.
  • Once the obligation crystallises with no remaining uncertainty, it ceases to be a "provision" and is reclassified as a normal liability (e.g., trade/other payable).
  • Unwinding of the discount (passage of time) increases the carrying amount of the provision each period and is recognised as a finance cost in P&L:

Dr. Finance Cost A/c To Provision A/c

  • Concept: Provisions are dynamic estimates, not one-time bookings — they must be actively reassessed every period.

Future Operating Losses

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): No provision is recognised for future operating losses.
  • Concept: Fails the definition of a liability — there is no past event giving rise to a present obligation, and the entity can avoid the loss through its own future actions (e.g., ceasing operations).

Contingent Liabilities

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • A contingent liability is never recognised in the balance sheet; it is only disclosed in the notes.
  • No disclosure required if the possibility of an outflow is remote.
  • Joint & several liability: for the entity's own expected share of the obligation → recognise a liability/provision; for the portion expected to be met by other parties → treat as a contingent liability (disclose, unless remote).
  • Reviewed continuously — if it becomes probable that an outflow is required and it can be reliably estimated, recognise a provision and remove the contingent liability disclosure.
  • Concept: The dividing line between "provision" and "contingent liability" is essentially the probability of outflow (probable → provision; not probable but not remote → disclose as contingent).

Contingent Assets

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • A contingent asset is never recognised (prudence) — only disclosed in the notes when an inflow of economic benefits is probable.
  • When realisation of income is virtually certain, the related asset is no longer a contingent asset — it qualifies for recognition as an asset.
  • Concept: Contingent assets are treated more conservatively than contingent liabilities — mere "probable" inflow only earns disclosure, not recognition; only virtual certainty triggers recognition.
  • Definitions / illustrative probability bands (commonly used teaching convention to apply the "probable / possible / remote" language of Ind AS 37 — ⚠️ VERIFY: Ind AS 37/IAS 37 itself does not prescribe fixed numeric percentage thresholds in the standard text; these bands are widely-used illustrative benchmarks for exam purposes, not a defined bright line in the standard):
Likelihood (illustrative)Contingent LiabilityContingent Asset
Virtually certain (>~95%)Recognise as provision/liabilityRecognise as an asset
Probable (~50%-95%)Recognise as provisionDisclose
Possible but not probable (~5%-50%)DiscloseNo disclosure permitted
Remote (<~5%)No disclosure requiredNo disclosure permitted
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: In answers, anchor language to the standard's own tests — "probable" (more likely than not), "possible", and "remote" — rather than treating the percentage bands above as if quoted directly from the Ind AS text.

Reimbursements

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Recognise a reimbursement asset only when virtually certain it will be received; otherwise, merely disclose it.
  • Present the provision (liability) and the reimbursement (asset) separately in the balance sheet — do not offset.
  • In the P&L, the provision expense and reimbursement income may be netted and presented together.
  • The reimbursement recognised must not exceed the amount of the related provision.
  • Concept: Ind AS 37 permits net presentation in P&L but requires gross presentation in the balance sheet.

Onerous Contracts

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • An onerous contract is one where the unavoidable costs of meeting the contractual obligations exceed the economic benefits expected to be received under it.
  • Recognise the unavoidable net cost as a provision, measured as:

Unavoidable (Provision) Cost = Lower of Cost of Fulfilling the Contract, Compensation/Penalties from Failure to Fulfil it

  • Amendment (cost of fulfilling a contract): the cost of fulfilling a contract comprises costs that relate directly to the contract — both the incremental costs of fulfilling it (e.g., direct labour, materials) and an allocation of other costs that relate directly to fulfilling contracts (e.g., an allocation of depreciation on plant used to fulfil the contract). This also requires considering impairment (Ind AS 36) on assets dedicated to fulfilling the contract before assessing whether it is onerous.
  • Concept: The provision captures the net unavoidable loss the entity cannot escape, whichever exit route (performing vs paying a penalty) is cheaper.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Impair dedicated contract assets under Ind AS 36 first, then assess/measure the onerous contract provision on the remaining unavoidable net cost — don't do these in the reverse order.

Restructuring Provisions

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): A constructive obligation for restructuring exists only when:
  1. The entity has a detailed formal plan identifying at least: the business/part of business concerned; the principal locations affected; the location, function and approximate number of employees to be compensated for termination; the expenditures to be undertaken; and when the plan will be implemented; AND
  2. The entity has raised a valid expectation in those affected that it will carry out the restructuring — by either starting to implement the plan, or announcing its main features to those affected.
  • Restructuring provision INCLUDES ONLY direct expenditures that are both (a) necessarily entailed by the restructuring, and (b) not associated with the entity's ongoing activities.
  • EXCLUDES: retraining/relocating continuing staff, marketing, and investment in new systems/distribution networks — these relate to the future conduct of the business, not to the restructuring itself.
  • Concept: A mere board decision/management intention to restructure, without a sufficiently detailed and communicated plan, does not create an obligation.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Costs like staff retraining or new IT systems are commonly (wrongly) included by students in restructuring provisions — these are always expensed as incurred, never provided for as part of the restructuring cost.

PART C — IND AS 38: INTANGIBLE ASSETS

Definitions & Identifiability

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): An intangible asset is an identifiable non-monetary asset without physical substance. It is identifiable if it is either:
  • Separable — capable of being separated/divided from the entity and sold, transferred, licensed, rented or exchanged, individually or together with a related contract; OR
  • Arises from contractual or other legal rights, whether or not those rights are transferable/separable from the entity or other rights/obligations.
  • Concept: Identifiability is what distinguishes an intangible asset from goodwill (which is not separately identifiable).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: For assets with both tangible and intangible elements (e.g., software on a machine), assess which element is more significant — software purchased on a CD → intangible asset; a computer with embedded operating software → tangible asset (PPE).

Recognition Criteria

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): Recognise an intangible asset if and only if:
  1. It is probable that expected future economic benefits attributable to the asset will flow to the entity; AND
  2. The cost of the asset can be measured reliably.
  • Concept: Same twin conditions as PPE (Ind AS 16), applied to non-physical assets.

Measurement — Purchased Intangibles

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Cost of Purchased Intangible = Purchase Price (net of Trade Discounts & Rebates) + Non-refundable Taxes + Directly Attributable Expenses

  • Directly attributable expenses INCLUDE: employee costs directly attributable to bringing the asset to working condition, costs of testing, and professional fees directly attributable.
  • Directly attributable expenses EXCLUDE: costs of introducing a new product/service, advertising and promotional costs, staff-training costs, administration and other general overhead costs, and initial operating losses.
  • Concept: Mirrors Ind AS 16's cost model — only costs necessary to bring the asset to its intended condition/use are capitalised.

Deferred Consideration

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Cost = Cash Price Equivalent at Recognition Date

Interest Expense (over credit period) = Total Payment - Cash Price Equivalent

  • Concept: Where payment is deferred beyond normal credit terms, the excess over the cash-price equivalent is a financing cost recognised over the credit period, not part of the intangible's cost.

Exchange of Assets

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): Where the exchange transaction does NOT lack commercial substance (in order of preference):

1st Preference: Cost = FV of Asset Given Up + Cash Paid (if any)

2nd Preference (if FV of asset given up not reliable): Cost = FV of Asset Acquired

3rd Preference (if neither FV reliable): Cost = Carrying Amount of Asset Given Up + Cash Paid (if any)

  • Where the transaction lacks commercial substance, only one basis applies regardless of FV availability:

Cost = Carrying Amount of Asset Given Up + Cash Paid (if any)

  • Concept: "Lacks commercial substance" means the future cash flows (risk, timing, amount) of the entity are not expected to change as a result of the exchange.

Intangibles Acquired in a Business Combination

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Cost = fair value at the acquisition date.
  • Recognised separately from goodwill, irrespective of whether the acquiree had recognised the asset before the combination (e.g., internally generated brands/customer lists not on the acquiree's books can still be recognised by the acquirer).
  • If an intangible is not separable from goodwill (or from another asset/liability) → subsume it into goodwill.
  • If separable only together with a related item, recognise the two as a single combined asset at their combined fair value (e.g., a magazine's publishing title and its subscriber database, where the title cannot be reliably valued/sold independent of the database).
  • Concept: Business combinations are the one context where even internally-developed-style intangibles of the acquiree (brands, customer relationships) get recognised, because they are now being fair-valued afresh by the acquirer.

Internally Generated Intangible Assets

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Research phase: all expenditure is expensed to P&L as incurred — no intangible asset is ever recognised from research.
  • Development phase: capitalise if and only if the entity can demonstrate all of the following (the classic "PIRATE"/six-condition test):
  1. Technical feasibility of completing the asset so it will be available for use or sale;
  2. Intention to complete and use or sell it;
  3. Ability to use or sell the intangible asset;
  4. How the asset will generate probable future economic benefits (existence of a market, or usefulness if for internal use);
  5. Availability of adequate technical, financial and other resources to complete development and to use/sell the asset;
  6. Ability to reliably measure the expenditure attributable to the asset during its development.
  • Capitalisation of development costs starts from the date the development phase begins (i.e., the date all six conditions are first met) — costs expensed in earlier periods (research phase, or before conditions were met) are never reinstated/capitalised retrospectively.
  • Internally generated brands, mastheads, publishing titles, customer lists and similar items are never recognised as intangible assets (cannot be distinguished from the cost of developing the business as a whole).
  • Concept: The strict six-condition test is what prevents indiscriminate capitalisation of ordinary R&D spend.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: If a project is acquired (e.g., an in-process R&D project bought in a business combination) and the entity later incurs its own further spend on it, that further spend must again be split into research vs development phases — self-incurred research-phase spend post-acquisition is expensed, even though the original acquired project itself was capitalised.

Measurement After Recognition (Cost Model vs Revaluation Model)

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Revaluation model is permitted only if fair value can be determined by reference to an active market for that class of intangible asset (rare in practice — e.g., taxi licences, fishing quotas). If no active market exists, the asset must be carried under the cost model.
  • Revaluation must be applied to the entire class of intangible assets to which the asset belongs — except that if no active market exists for one particular asset within an otherwise-revalued class, that one asset is carried at cost while the rest of the class continues under revaluation.
  • Revaluation surplus (increase) → recognised in Other Comprehensive Income and accumulated in equity (revaluation surplus), unless it reverses a previous revaluation decrease of the same asset previously recognised in P&L (then credit P&L to that extent).
  • Revaluation decrease → recognised in P&L, unless there is a credit balance in the revaluation surplus for that same asset (then debited to OCI/that surplus to the extent of the existing balance, only the excess going to P&L).
  • On transfer of the revalued asset out of use (disposal/derecognition), any related revaluation surplus balance may be transferred directly to retained earnings — this transfer is never routed through P&L.
  • Concept: Same revaluation mechanics as Ind AS 16 PPE, but gated by a much stricter "active market" precondition.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Students commonly try to reverse a revaluation gain/loss through P&L on derecognition — the transfer to retained earnings must bypass profit or loss entirely.

Subsequent Expenditure

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): Most subsequent expenditure on an already-recognised intangible asset is expensed to P&L as incurred, because it typically only maintains (rather than enhances beyond original) the asset's expected future economic benefits.
  • Concept: Capitalisation of subsequent spend is the exception, not the rule, for intangibles.

Expenditure Always Recognised as an Expense

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): Always expense: research expenditure (unless acquired as part of a business combination); start-up costs; training expenditure; advertising and promotional expenditure; and expenditure on relocating/reorganising part or all of an entity.
  • Concept: These items never qualify for capitalisation, regardless of how the six development-phase conditions might otherwise be argued to apply.

Amortisation

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Amortisation per period = Cost (or Revalued Amount) - Residual ValueUseful Life (straight-line, if pattern of consumption not reliably determinable)

  • Amortisable amount = cost (or revalued amount) less residual value, allocated systematically over the useful life.
  • Amortisation of an intangible asset with a finite useful life does NOT cease merely because the asset is idle or no longer actively used — it continues until the asset is fully amortised or classified as held for sale under Ind AS 105.
  • An intangible asset with an indefinite useful life is NOT amortised — it is instead tested for impairment at least annually under Ind AS 36.
  • Concept: "Indefinite" does not mean "infinite" — it means no foreseeable limit to the period the asset will generate net cash inflows.

Residual Value

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): Residual value is assumed to be ZERO, unless:

a. There is a commitment by a third party to purchase the asset at the end of its useful life; OR b. There is an active market for the asset, and it is probable such a market will still exist at the end of the asset's useful life (so residual value can be estimated by reference to that market).

  • If residual value is subsequently estimated to exceed the asset's carrying amount, the amortisation charge is zero until the residual value later falls below the carrying amount.
  • Concept: The default zero-residual assumption reflects the difficulty of reliably forecasting a resale market for most intangibles.

Useful Life

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • For an intangible asset arising from contractual or other legal rights, useful life shall NOT exceed the period of those rights — but may be shorter if the entity expects to use the asset for a shorter period.
  • If the right is renewable, include the renewal period(s) in useful life only if ALL of:
  1. There is evidence (e.g., past experience) that the right will be renewed;
  2. Any conditions necessary for renewal will be satisfied; AND
  3. The cost of renewal is not significant compared to the future economic benefits expected to flow from the renewal.
  • Concept: Useful life assessment for contractual/legal-right-based intangibles is capped by the legal term unless renewal is virtually assured and cheap.

Annual Review (per Ind AS 8)

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): The amortisation method, residual value, and useful life of an intangible asset must be reviewed at least at each financial year-end. Any change is accounted for prospectively as a change in accounting estimate under Ind AS 8 (not a retrospective restatement, not a prior-period error).
  • Concept: Keeps the amortisation profile aligned with the latest information without disturbing past periods' reported figures.

Impairment

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): Impairment testing/losses on intangible assets follow Ind AS 36 in full. Intangible assets with an indefinite useful life require an impairment review at least annually, whether or not there is any indication of impairment.
  • Concept: Cross-reference — Ind AS 38 does not have its own impairment mechanics; it defers entirely to Ind AS 36.

Derecognition

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): Derecognise an intangible asset when it is disposed of, or when no future economic benefits are expected from its use or disposal.

Gain/Loss on Derecognition = Net Disposal Proceeds - Carrying Amount

  • The gain/loss is recognised in P&L, and — critically — a gain is NOT classified as revenue; it is presented as Other Income.
  • Concept: Disposal gains on intangibles (and PPE generally) never inflate the revenue line — they sit below it as other income.

11. Ind AS 40, 41, 101, 105, 108 & 113 - Investment Property, Agriculture, First-time Adoption, Non-current Assets Held for Sale, Operating Segments & Fair Value Measurement

Where it fits: A "miscellaneous standards" chapter bundling six otherwise-independent topics that recur as 4-8 mark theory/practical questions — Ind AS 40 (property held for rent/appreciation), Ind AS 41 (biological assets), Ind AS 101 (the transition rulebook that touches every other standard), Ind AS 105 (assets held for sale, tested with Ind AS 16/36), Ind AS 108 (segment disclosures, management-approach based) and Ind AS 113 (the fair value measurement standard that Ind AS 109, 40, 41, 16, 38 etc. all lean on).

Ind AS 40 — Investment Property: Definition & Scope

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Investment property = land or building (or part) held (by owner or lessee as a right-of-use asset) to earn rentals or for capital appreciation, or both — NOT for (a) use in production/supply/administration, or (b) sale in the ordinary course of business.
  • In scope: land held for long-term capital appreciation; land held for undetermined future use; a building leased out under an operating lease; property being constructed/developed for future use as investment property.
  • Out of scope: property held for sale (Ind AS 2), owner-occupied property (Ind AS 16/116), owner-occupied property awaiting disposal (Ind AS 105).
  • Concept: The dividing line is the purpose of holding the asset — rental/appreciation income vs. own use/trading.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: An investment property held by a lessee as a right-of-use asset is still recognised and measured following Ind AS 40 principles (initial recognition per Ind AS 116, but classification test is Ind AS 40's).
TermMeaning
Investment propertyProperty held to earn rentals/capital appreciation, not for own use or sale
Owner-occupied propertyProperty held for use in production/supply of goods/services or admin purposes — Ind AS 16

Ind AS 40 — Recognition & Initial Measurement

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Recognise as an asset only if: (a) future economic benefits are probable, AND (b) cost can be measured reliably.

Cost of Investment Property (if purchased) = Purchase Price - Trade Discounts/Rebates + Non-refundable Taxes + Directly Attributable Expenses

  • Directly attributable expenses INCLUDE professional/legal fees, property transfer taxes, other transaction costs; EXCLUDE start-up costs, operating losses before planned occupancy, abnormal amounts of wasted material/labour during construction.
  • Deferred consideration: cost = cash price equivalent at recognition date; difference vs. total payment = interest expense over the credit period.
  • Exchange of assets (order of preference, unless the exchange lacks commercial substance):
  1. Fair value of asset given up + cash paid (if any)
  2. Fair value of asset acquired
  3. Carrying amount of asset given up + cash paid (if any) — used only if the transaction lacks commercial substance, or if neither fair value above is reliably measurable.
  • Concept: Same cost-build-up logic as Ind AS 16, adapted for investment property; a transaction "lacks commercial substance" when the risk, timing and amount of the resulting cash flows are not expected to differ from the transferred asset's cash flows.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Don't confuse the exchange hierarchy with Ind AS 16's — the logic is identical (FV of asset given up first), so cross-reference the same 3-step waterfall when solving.

Ind AS 40 — Subsequent Measurement, Transfers & Derecognition

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Cost model only — Ind AS 40 does NOT permit the revaluation/fair value model for subsequent measurement of investment property (unlike IAS 40 globally, which allows an FV model option). Fair value must still be disclosed (not recognised) for every investment property.
  • If an investment property meets the Ind AS 105 held-for-sale criteria, it is measured per Ind AS 105 (i.e., at the lower of carrying amount and fair value less costs to sell).
  • Transfers to/from investment property are made only on a change in use, evidenced by e.g. commencement/end of owner-occupation (↔ Ind AS 16), commencement of development for sale (→ Ind AS 2), or inception of an operating lease to a third party (Ind AS 2 → Ind AS 40). A transfer does NOT change the carrying amount or the cost of the property for measurement/disclosure purposes.

Gain/Loss on Derecognition = Net Disposal Proceeds - Carrying Amount

  • Derecognise on disposal or when no future economic benefits are expected; the gain/loss goes to P&L and must not be classified as revenue.
  • Concept: Ind AS 40 is deliberately cost-model-only in India (a carve-out from IAS 40) — this is one of the most tested "India vs IFRS" differences in the standard.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • A mere change in management's intention is NOT evidence of change in use — actual physical/legal change (start of construction, start of owner-occupation, etc.) is required.
  • Investment property is depreciated like PPE, except land, which is not depreciated.
  • Property held partly for rental/appreciation and partly for own use: if the portions could be sold/leased separately, account for each portion separately (Ind AS 40 for the rental part, Ind AS 16 for the owner-occupied part); if not separable, treat as investment property only if the owner-occupied portion is insignificant.
  • Ancillary services to occupants (e.g., security/maintenance in an office building) — insignificant services still allow investment property classification; significant services (e.g., an owner-managed hotel) make it owner-occupied property instead.
  • Property leased to a parent/fellow subsidiary is investment property only in the lessor's separate/individual financial statements — on consolidation it is owner-occupied from the group's perspective.

Ind AS 41 — Agriculture: Scope & Recognition

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Ind AS 41 applies to biological assets (living animals/plants) and agricultural produce at the point of harvest, and to government grants related to biological assets — related to agricultural activity.
  • Bearer plants (e.g., tea bushes, grape vines, oil palms, rubber trees) are scoped OUT of Ind AS 41 and accounted for under Ind AS 16; the produce growing on them (tea leaves, grapes, oil palm fruit, latex) remains within Ind AS 41 scope until harvest.
  • Recognise a biological asset or agricultural produce only if: (a) the entity controls the asset as a result of past events, (b) it is probable that future economic benefits will flow, AND (c) the fair value or cost can be measured reliably.
  • Concept: Ind AS 41 governs the biological transformation (growth, degeneration, production, procreation) of living assets up to the point of harvest; post-harvest produce is Ind AS 2 inventory.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Watch for exam traps distinguishing bearer plants (Ind AS 16) from the produce they bear (Ind AS 41) — a single tea bush is bifurcated conceptually this way.

Ind AS 41 — Measurement (Biological Assets & Agricultural Produce)

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Biological assets are measured at Fair Value Less Costs to Sell (FVLCTS) at initial recognition and at each reporting date.
  • Agricultural produce harvested from an entity's biological assets is measured at FVLCTS at the point of harvest; that amount becomes the "cost" for Ind AS 2 purposes on transfer to inventory.
  • Grouping of biological assets/agricultural produce for FV measurement purposes is permitted.
  • Contract price is NOT relevant to fair value, since fair value reflects the current market and contracts are typically for future delivery.
  • Cost may approximate fair value only when: little biological transformation has occurred since initial cost incurrence (e.g., newly acquired livestock), OR the impact of biological transformation on price is not expected to be material.
  • Where biological assets are attached to land and no separate market exists for the biological asset:

Fair Value of Biological Asset = Fair Value of Combined Asset (Land + Biological Asset) - Fair Value of Raw Land & Land Improvements

  • All gains/losses on initial recognition and on subsequent remeasurement to FVLCTS are recognised in profit or loss.
  • ⚠️ VERIFY — the OCR notes a 2022 amendment requiring that "tax cash flows" be included in the fair value measurement of biological assets; this is the 2022 amendment to Ind AS 41 aligning with the IASB's May 2020 amendment to IAS 41 (removal of the "fair value calculated using a discounted cash flow methodology excludes taxation" language, i.e., taxation cash flows are no longer excluded) — confirm the exact wording against the latest ICAI study material/RTP.
  • Concept: Fair value changes can arise from physical changes (growth) and price changes (market movement) — both routed to P&L, unlike the revaluation model elsewhere which routes gains to OCI.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Government grants on biological assets measured at FVLCTS are recognised in P&L when the grant becomes receivable; if a conditional grant (e.g., "farm in location X for 5 years or repay") is involved, recognise in P&L only once the condition is met (or pro-rata if partial retention is allowed as time elapses).

Ind AS 101 — First-time Adoption: Core Definitions

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • First Ind AS Financial Statements = the first annual FS with an explicit and unreserved statement of compliance with Ind AS (partial compliance does not qualify).
  • Date of Transition to Ind AS = beginning of the earliest period for which full comparative Ind AS information is presented.
  • Opening Ind AS Balance Sheet = the entity's balance sheet at the date of transition.
  • Worked example: if Ind AS first applies from 1.4.20X1 (First Ind AS reporting period = 1.4.20X1 to 31.3.20X2), then Date of Transition = 1.4.20X0 and Opening Ind AS Balance Sheet is drawn up as at 1.4.20X0.
  • Concept: Because one year of comparatives is mandatory, the transition date is always one full year before the first Ind AS reporting period begins.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Students often confuse "date of transition" with "date of first Ind AS reporting period" — the transition date is always the earlier of the two.

Ind AS 101 — General Principle & Mandatory Exceptions

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): In the opening Ind AS balance sheet, an entity must: recognise all assets/liabilities Ind AS requires; NOT recognise items Ind AS does not permit as assets/liabilities; reclassify items previous GAAP called one type of asset/liability/equity into their correct Ind AS classification; and measure all recognised assets/liabilities per Ind AS — subject to the mandatory exceptions and optional exemptions below.
  • Mandatory exceptions (retrospective application is NOT allowed) cover: (i) estimates — must be consistent with previous GAAP estimates at the same date (adjusted only for accounting policy differences), unless there is objective evidence of error; (ii) derecognition of financial assets/liabilities — Ind AS 109 derecognition rules applied prospectively from the transition date (retrospective application allowed only if the information needed was obtained at the time of the original transaction); (iii) non-controlling interests — Ind AS 110 NCI attribution rules (including NCI deficit) applied prospectively; (iv) classification & measurement of financial assets — the Ind AS 109 SPPI/business-model assessment is done based on facts at the date of transition (not original recognition); (v) impairment of financial assets — Ind AS 109 ECL applied retrospectively subject to reasonable/available information without undue cost/effort; (vi) government loans — classified per Ind AS 32 at transition, with Ind AS 109/20 measurement applied prospectively (no retrospective grant recognition for a below-market-rate loan).
  • Concept: Mandatory exceptions exist where hindsight bias would distort the reconstruction of past judgments (e.g., re-doing an ECL/SPPI assessment with knowledge of what actually happened).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: A first-time adopter classifies all government loans under Ind AS 32 (financial liability or equity) but does not retrospectively recognise the benefit of a below-market government loan as a grant under Ind AS 20 — that treatment is prospective only from the transition date.

Ind AS 101 — Optional Exemptions (Key Ones)

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): Optional exemptions (entity MAY elect not to apply full retrospective Ind AS) include, among others:
  • Business combinations — Ind AS 103 need not be applied to combinations before the transition date; but if one past combination is restated, all later ones must also be restated.
  • Deemed cost for PPE, intangible assets & right-of-use assets — choice, on an item-by-item basis, of: (a) full retrospective Ind AS 16/38/116 cost; (b) fair value at transition date as deemed cost; (c) previous GAAP revaluation amount (if broadly comparable to fair value or depreciated cost adjusted for a price index); or (d) previous GAAP carrying amount as deemed cost (this last option must be applied to all asset classes together, and may also be used for investment property).
  • Deemed cost for investment property — since Ind AS 40 mandates the cost model, the "fair value as deemed cost" option is not available for investment property (only the previous-GAAP-carrying-amount route above, if elected for all PPE classes).
  • Investments in subsidiaries, JVs, associates (in separate FS) — measured at cost per Ind AS 27, OR at deemed cost = fair value at transition date, OR previous GAAP carrying amount at transition date.
  • Cumulative translation differences (FCTR) — may be reset to zero at transition date; subsequent disposal gain/loss on that foreign operation excludes translation differences arising before transition.
  • Non-current assets held for sale / discontinued operations — measure at the lower of carrying amount and FVLCTS at the transition date per Ind AS 105; recognise any difference from previous GAAP carrying amount directly in retained earnings.
  • Share-based payment — application of Ind AS 102 to equity instruments vested before the transition date is encouraged but not required.
  • Concept: Optional exemptions are cost-benefit relief valves — full retrospective restatement is often impracticable or not worth the cost for these specific items.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: The PPE deemed-cost "previous GAAP carrying amount" option is an all-or-nothing election across all asset classes, but the fair-value-as-deemed-cost and full-retrospective options can both be applied item-by-item/asset-by-asset.

Ind AS 105 — Held for Sale: Scope & Classification Criteria

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Scope exclusions from the measurement requirements (classification/presentation still applies): deferred tax assets (Ind AS 12), assets from employee benefits (Ind AS 19), financial assets within Ind AS 109, biological assets/agricultural produce measured at FVLCTS (Ind AS 41), and contractual rights under insurance contracts (Ind AS 104).
  • A non-current asset (or disposal group) is classified as held for sale if its carrying amount will be recovered principally through a sale transaction rather than continuing use, AND both of the following are met:
  1. It is available for immediate sale in its present condition, subject only to terms that are usual and customary for such sales.
  2. The sale is highly probable, i.e., all five conditions hold: (a) management is committed to a plan to sell; (b) an active programme to locate a buyer has been initiated; (c) it is marketed for sale at a price reasonable to its current fair value; (d) the sale is expected to complete within one year of classification; and (e) it is unlikely that significant changes to, or withdrawal of, the plan will occur.
  • Exception to the one-year rule: classification can continue beyond one year if the delay is caused by events/circumstances beyond the entity's control AND there is sufficient evidence the entity remains committed to the sale plan.
  • Asset acquired exclusively with a view to subsequent disposal: classified as held for sale at the acquisition date if the sale is expected within one year (the entity gets a short period — 3 months — to meet the other classification criteria, though the one-year sale-completion requirement itself is not relaxed).
  • Concept: "Held for sale" reclassifies an asset off the "continuing use" P&L (no more depreciation) onto a "recover through sale" basis, tested by a checklist rather than management intent alone.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Criteria met after the reporting period but before FS authorisation: do NOT classify as held for sale in that year's FS — only disclose in the notes.
  • Assets to be abandoned (used to the end of economic life, or closed rather than sold) are never "held for sale" — recovery is through continuing use, not sale; assets merely temporarily idle are not "abandoned."
  • A subsidiary sale involving loss of control: classify all assets and liabilities of the subsidiary as held for sale once the criteria are met (not just a partial stake).

Ind AS 105 — Measurement, Impairment, Presentation

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Non-current Asset/Disposal Group (Held for Sale) = min(Carrying Amount, Fair Value Less Costs to Sell (FVLCTS))

Impairment Loss = Carrying Amount - FVLCTS

  • Depreciation/amortisation on assets classified as held for sale ceases from the date of classification.
  • Order of allocating an impairment loss within a disposal group: first reduce goodwill allocated to the group; then allocate to the other assets pro-rata on carrying amount (excluding assets outside Ind AS 105's measurement scope, e.g., inventories, financial assets).
  • Reversal of impairment is permitted for a subsequent increase in FVLCTS, but capped at the cumulative impairment loss previously recognised under Ind AS 105 or Ind AS 36 — except no reversal is permitted against goodwill.
  • Ceasing held-for-sale classification: measure at the LOWER of (a) carrying amount had the asset never been classified as held for sale (i.e., with catch-up depreciation as if never reclassified), and (b) recoverable amount at the date of the change.
  • Presentation: present the held-for-sale asset/disposal group separately from other assets on the balance sheet; for a disposal group, assets and liabilities must NOT be offset/netted into a single line.
  • Concept: The FVLCTS ceiling test parallels Ind AS 36 impairment logic but is specific to the "recover through sale" measurement basis.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Don't net disposal-group assets against disposal-group liabilities — both are shown gross and separately from continuing assets/liabilities.

Ind AS 105 — Discontinued Operations

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): A discontinued operation is a component of an entity (a cash-generating unit, or group of CGUs, distinguishable operationally and for financial reporting) that either has been disposed of, or is classified as held for sale, and represents a separate major line of business/geographical area of operations, is part of a single co-ordinated plan to dispose of such a line/area, or is a subsidiary acquired exclusively with a view to resale.
  • Present and disclose discontinued-operations results separately from continuing operations (typically a single line for post-tax profit/loss of discontinued operations, split further in the notes).
  • If a component ceases to be held for sale, its results previously shown as discontinued must be reclassified into continuing operations for all periods presented, with prior-period amounts described as "re-presented."
  • Concept: Discontinued-operations reporting isolates the results of exited/exiting business lines so users can assess continuing operations cleanly.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: A discontinued operation can still be classified as an Ind AS 108 "operating segment" if it continues business activities reviewed by the CODM with discrete information available.

Ind AS 108 — Operating Segments: Identification

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): An operating segment is a component of an entity that: (a) engages in business activities from which it may earn revenues and incur expenses (including start-up operations not yet earning revenue); (b) whose operating results are regularly reviewed by the Chief Operating Decision Maker (CODM) to allocate resources and assess performance; and (c) for which discrete financial information is available.
  • Ind AS 108 uses the management approach (segments = however the CODM actually organises internal reporting) vs. AS 17's risk-and-rewards approach (business + geographical segments).
  • CODM is a function, not necessarily a single named individual — often the CEO/COO but could be a group of executive directors.
  • An integral function (e.g., in-house R&D) can be an operating segment if discrete information on it is regularly reviewed by the CODM.
  • Concept: Segment identification is entirely driven by how management actually monitors the business internally, not by an externally-imposed classification scheme.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Segment information is disclosed only if the entity's equity or debt is publicly traded (or it is in the process of issuing) and, where CFS and separate FS are both presented in one report, segment disclosure is required only in the consolidated financial statements.

Ind AS 108 — Aggregation & Reportable Segments

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Aggregation: two or more operating segments may be combined into one reportable segment if aggregation is consistent with the core principle AND the segments have similar economic characteristics AND are similar in: (a) nature of products/services, (b) nature of production processes, (c) type/class of customer, (d) methods of distributing products/services (and distribution regulatory environment, where relevant).
  • Quantitative thresholds — a segment is reportable if it meets any of:
  • Reported revenue (external + intersegment) is ≥ 10% of the combined revenue (internal + external) of all operating segments; OR
  • Absolute amount of reported profit or loss is ≥ 10% of the greater, in absolute amount, of (i) combined reported profit of all segments not reporting a loss, and (ii) combined reported loss of all segments that reported a loss; OR
  • Assets are ≥ 10% of the combined assets of all operating segments.
  • 75% external revenue test: if total external revenue of all reportable segments identified is < 75% of total entity revenue, additional segments must be identified as reportable (even if below the 10% thresholds) until at least 75% of entity revenue is covered.
  • A segment reportable in the immediately preceding period that management judges to be of continuing significance continues to be reported separately even if it no longer meets the thresholds in the current period.
  • Management may voluntarily designate a below-threshold segment as reportable if it believes the information is useful to users.
  • Segments not separately reportable are combined and disclosed under "All other segments."
  • Concept: The 10% tests are OR conditions (any one triggers reportability), while the 75% test is a backstop ensuring segment disclosure covers most of the entity's revenue.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: The profit/loss threshold uses the greater of total profit-making segments' profit or total loss-making segments' loss (in absolute terms) as the base — a common numerical trap in MCQs/practical problems.

Ind AS 113 — Fair Value: Definition, Initial Recognition, Markets

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Fair value = the price that would be received to sell an asset or paid to transfer a liability in an orderly transaction between market participants at the measurement date (an exit price), where "orderly" means not a forced transaction/distress sale.
  • At initial recognition, transaction price (entry price) generally equals fair value (exit price); if another Ind AS requires initial fair value measurement and the transaction price differs (e.g., a pre-existing relationship per Ind AS 109), the difference is recognised as a gain/loss in P&L unless that other Ind AS specifies a different treatment.
  • Fair value is measured assuming the transaction occurs in the principal market (the market with the greatest volume/level of activity for the asset/liability); in its absence, the most advantageous market (the market that maximises the amount receivable, after transport AND transaction costs, though transaction costs are used only to identify the market, not to adjust the fair value itself).

Fair Value = Market Price - Transportation Cost

(Transaction costs are excluded from the fair value figure itself — they are dealt with under the specific Ind AS in which fair value is used.)

  • Concept: Fair value is a market-based, not entity-specific, exit-price measurement — this is the conceptual anchor for every fair-value cross-reference across Ind AS 40, 41, 109, 16, 38, etc.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Don't deduct transaction costs from fair value — only transport costs are deducted; transaction costs merely help identify which market is "most advantageous."

Ind AS 113 — Non-financial Assets, Liabilities & Own Equity

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • For a non-financial asset, fair value considers the asset's highest and best use by market participants — a use that is physically possible, legally permissible, and financially feasible; entity-specific restrictions on use are ignored; if the highest and best use cannot be reliably determined, current use is presumed to be the highest and best use.
  • For a liability or an entity's own equity instrument: use the quoted price in an active market if held by another party as an asset; if unavailable, use the quoted price of an identical or similar liability/equity instrument held by another party as an asset; if that too is unavailable, use a valuation technique (income approach, market approach, etc.).
  • Concept: Fair value of non-financial assets is about market-participant use potential (highest & best use), while liabilities/own equity fair value is anchored to what a holder of the corresponding asset would see.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: "Highest and best use" is assessed from the market participant's perspective, not management's actual intended use — a frequently tested distinction.

Ind AS 113 — Fair Value Hierarchy & Valuation Techniques

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): Three-level hierarchy, prioritised for maximum use of observable inputs and minimum use of unobservable inputs:
  • Level 1: Quoted prices (unadjusted) in active markets for identical assets/liabilities, accessible at the measurement date — the most reliable evidence; used without adjustment whenever available.
  • Level 2: Observable inputs other than Level 1 quoted prices (directly or indirectly observable) — e.g., quoted prices for similar assets/liabilities in active markets, or for identical/similar assets/liabilities in markets that are not active. Adjustments to Level 2 inputs must be insignificant; a significant adjustment reclassifies the measurement to Level 3.
  • Level 3: Unobservable inputs (e.g., an entity's own financial forecasts, historical volatility data).
  • Three broad valuation technique families: Market approach (prices from actual market transactions in identical/comparable assets), Cost approach (current replacement cost), and Income approach (present value of future cash flows/earnings, e.g., discounted cash flow).
  • Concept: Maximum weight is given to Level 1 inputs and minimum weight to Level 3 — the hierarchy governs disclosure classification, not which valuation technique to use.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: If a Level 1 quoted price in an active market is available, no valuation technique/approach is needed or permitted to override it.

12. Ind AS 115 - Revenue from Contracts with Customers

Where it fits: Replaces the old "risk & reward" trigger for revenue with a single control-based, five-step model applicable to (almost) every contract with a customer — the highest-weightage standard in the FR revenue/consolidation block and a favourite for practical problems on variable consideration, financing components, and over-time vs point-in-time recognition.

1. Core Principle, Scope & the Five-Step Model

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Core recognition principle: recognise revenue to depict the transfer of promised goods/services to the customer in an amount that reflects the consideration the entity expects to be entitled to in exchange for those goods/services.
  • The Five-Step Model:
  1. Identify the contract(s) with a customer
  2. Identify the separate performance obligations (POs) in the contract
  3. Determine the transaction price
  4. Allocate the transaction price to the POs
  5. Recognise revenue when (or as) the entity satisfies a PO
  • Ind AS 115 does not apply to: lease contracts (Ind AS 116), insurance contracts, financial instruments (Ind AS 109/110/111/112/113), non-monetary exchanges between entities in the same line of business to facilitate sales to customers, and certain guarantees other than product warranties.
  • Concept: The standard's central shift from earlier Indian GAAP (AS 9) is recognising revenue on transfer of control, not transfer of risks and rewards — control being the ability to direct the use of, and obtain substantially all the remaining benefits from, an asset.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Always answer numericals by explicitly walking through Steps 1–5 in order — examiners award step-wise marks.
  • Do not confuse "control" (Ind AS 115) with "risk and rewards" (old AS 9) — a common trap in transition-based questions.
  • Definitions:
TermMeaning
ContractAn agreement between two or more parties creating enforceable rights and obligations
Performance obligation (PO)A promise to transfer a distinct good/service (or a series of substantially similar distinct goods/services with the same pattern of transfer) to the customer
Transaction priceThe amount of consideration an entity expects to be entitled to in exchange for transferring promised goods/services, excluding amounts collected on behalf of third parties (e.g. GST)

2. Step 1 — Identify the Contract (incl. Combining & Modifications)

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): A contract exists (falls within Ind AS 115) only when all five criteria are met:
  1. The parties have approved the contract (in writing, orally, or as per customary business practice) and are committed to perform;
  2. The entity can identify each party's rights regarding the goods/services to be transferred;
  3. The entity can identify the payment terms;
  4. The contract has commercial substance; and
  5. It is probable that the entity will collect the consideration to which it will be entitled (assessed considering only the customer's ability and intention to pay).
  • Combine two or more contracts entered into at/near the same time with the same customer (or related parties) into a single contract if any one applies:
  • Negotiated as a package with a single commercial objective;
  • Consideration in one contract depends on the price/performance of the other contract; or
  • The goods/services promised are (or a portion of them are) a single performance obligation.
  • Contract modification — account for as a separate contract only if both: (a) scope increases due to addition of distinct goods/services, and (b) price increases by an amount reflecting the standalone selling price of the added goods/services (adjusted for contract-specific circumstances). Otherwise, account for the modification prospectively (termination of old contract + creation of new, if remaining goods/services are distinct), as a cumulative catch-up adjustment (if remaining goods/services are not distinct — i.e. part of a partially satisfied single PO), or as a combination of both.
  • Concept: Step 1 is a gatekeeping test — if the five criteria are not (yet) met, any consideration received is recognised as a liability, not revenue, until the criteria are subsequently met or the contract is terminated/cancelled.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • "Probable" collectability is judged net of any price concessions the entity is expected to grant — not the stated contract price.
  • If criteria are not met but non-refundable consideration is received, recognise revenue only when either the entity has no remaining obligation and consideration is non-refundable, or the contract has been terminated.

3. Step 2 — Identify Performance Obligations

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): A promised good/service is distinct (and hence a separate PO) only if both conditions are met:
  1. Capable of being distinct — the customer can benefit from the good/service either on its own or together with other readily available resources; and
  2. Distinct within the context of the contract — the entity's promise to transfer the good/service is separately identifiable from other promises in the contract.
  • Indicators that a good/service is not separately identifiable (i.e., should be bundled with others into one PO): the entity provides a significant integration service combining goods/services into a combined output; one good/service significantly modifies/customises another; or the goods/services are highly interdependent/interrelated.
  • Concept: A contract may contain one PO (e.g., a single integrated construction contract) or many (e.g., sale of goods + extended warranty + installation, each sold separately elsewhere).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • A "series" of substantially the same distinct goods/services transferred over time with the same pattern of transfer is treated as one single PO (relevant for over-time recognition of, e.g., a cleaning/facility-management contract).
  • Test both distinctness conditions — students often stop after checking only "capable of being distinct."

4. Step 3 — Determine the Transaction Price

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): Transaction price = consideration entity expects to be entitled to, adjusted for:
  • Variable consideration — estimated using whichever method better predicts the amount:

Expected Value = Σi=1n (Probabilityi × Possible Consideration Amounti)
(used where the entity has a large number of contracts with similar characteristics), or the Most Likely Amount (the single most likely outcome; appropriate where the contract has only two possible outcomes, e.g. a performance bonus is either earned in full or not at all).

  • Constraint on variable consideration: include variable consideration in the transaction price only to the extent it is highly probable that a significant reversal in the cumulative revenue recognised will not occur when the uncertainty is subsequently resolved.
  • Significant financing component: adjust the promised consideration for the time value of money using the discount rate that would apply to a separate financing transaction between the entity and the customer at contract inception, reflecting the customer's credit characteristics.
  • Practical expedient: an entity need not adjust for a financing component if, at contract inception, it expects the period between transfer of the good/service and payment by the customer to be one year or less.
  • Non-cash consideration is measured at its fair value (or, if fair value cannot be reasonably estimated, by reference to the standalone selling price of the goods/services promised).
  • Consideration payable to a customer (e.g., coupons, rebates) is deducted from the transaction price unless it is in exchange for a distinct good/service that the customer provides to the entity.
  • Concept: The transaction price is not necessarily the stated contract price — it must be adjusted for uncertainty, timing, and non-cash/negative elements before allocation.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Apply the constraint test at each reporting date — re-estimate and re-assess, don't just estimate once at inception.
  • The "one year or less" financing practical expedient is measured from the point goods/services are transferred, not from contract signing — a frequent trap in advance-payment vs deferred-payment numericals.
  • Amounts collected on behalf of third parties (e.g. GST/sales tax) are excluded from the transaction price.

5. Step 4 — Allocate the Transaction Price to Performance Obligations

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): Allocate on a relative standalone selling price (SSP) basis:

Transaction Price Allocated to POi = Total Transaction Price × fracSSPiΣj=1n SSPj

  • Best evidence of SSP = the observable price of the good/service when the entity sells it separately. If not observable, estimate using: (a) adjusted market assessment approach, (b) expected cost plus a margin approach, or (c) residual approach:

SSPresidual = Total Transaction Price - Σ(Observable SSPs of Other Goods/Services in the Contract)
— the residual approach is permitted only where the price of that good/service is highly variable or uncertain (not for goods/services with a determinable, non-variable SSP).

  • A discount in the contract is allocated proportionately to all POs on a relative SSP basis, unless there is observable evidence that the entire discount relates to only one or some (but not all) of the POs.
  • Variable consideration may be allocated entirely to one distinct good/service (rather than spread across all POs) if both: (a) the variable payment terms relate specifically to that good/service, and (b) allocating the entire amount to it is consistent with the overall allocation objective.
  • Concept: Allocation ensures each PO's revenue reflects the price the customer would have paid for that PO on a standalone basis, even where the bundled contract price differs.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Never allocate on the basis of cost or an arbitrary basis — SSP (or an approved estimation method) is mandatory.
  • Watch for "free" or heavily discounted items bundled with a paid item (e.g. a free warranty with a machine sale) — a portion of the transaction price must still be allocated to it if it is a separate PO.

6. Step 5 — Recognise Revenue (Over Time vs Point in Time)

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): A PO is satisfied over time if any one of the following is met (else it is satisfied at a point in time):
  1. The customer simultaneously receives and consumes the benefits as the entity performs (e.g. routine/recurring services); or
  2. The entity's performance creates or enhances an asset that the customer controls as it is created/enhanced (e.g. asset built on customer's land); or
  3. The entity's performance does not create an asset with an alternative use to the entity, and the entity has an enforceable right to payment for performance completed to date (e.g. a customised/build-to-order asset).
  • Point-in-time indicators (used when none of the above apply) — control has passed when the customer has: present right to payment; legal title; physical possession; the significant risks and rewards of ownership; and has accepted the asset.
  • Measuring progress for over-time POs:
  • Output methods — direct measurement of value transferred (units delivered, milestones reached, surveys of performance).
  • Input methods (most common — cost-to-cost):

Revenue Recognised to Date = Costs Incurred to DateTotal Estimated Costs to Complete × Total Transaction Price
Exclude costs that do not depict transfer of control (e.g. cost of wasted/abnormal materials or labour, or costs of uninstalled materials where the entity is not the one adding value to those materials) from both numerator and total-cost denominator, or recognise revenue for such costs at zero margin.

  • Concept: This step converts the allocated transaction price into an actual revenue recognition pattern — the "when/as" of the five-step model.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Re-assess progress at each reporting date and update estimates prospectively (a change in accounting estimate, per Ind AS 8) — not retrospectively.
  • If outcome of an over-time PO cannot be reasonably measured but costs are expected to be recovered, recognise revenue only to the extent of costs incurred (zero-margin/cost-recovery approach) until the outcome can be reliably estimated.

7. Contract Costs (Costs to Obtain & Costs to Fulfil)

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Incremental costs of obtaining a contract (e.g. sales commissions) — capitalise as an asset if the entity expects to recover them.
  • Practical expedient: may expense such costs immediately if the amortisation period of the resulting asset would have been one year or less.
  • Costs to fulfil a contract (not within the scope of another standard, e.g. Ind AS 2, 16, 38) — capitalise only if all of the following are met:
  1. Costs relate directly to an identifiable contract (or specific anticipated contract);
  2. Costs generate or enhance resources that will be used to satisfy performance obligations in the future; and
  3. Costs are expected to be recovered.
  • Amortise the capitalised asset on a systematic basis consistent with the transfer to the customer of the goods/services to which the asset relates; update amortisation for contract modifications/renewals; test for impairment using the carrying amount vs. remaining consideration expected less remaining costs to fulfil.
  • Concept: Distinguishes costs that create a contract asset (deferred and amortised over the contract) from costs expensed as incurred (general administration, costs of obtaining a contract that would have been incurred regardless of whether the contract was won, and costs relating to satisfied/partially satisfied POs).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Commission that would have been paid regardless of whether the contract is won (non-incremental) is expensed, not capitalised.
  • Do not capitalise costs of past performance already recognised as revenue.

8. Contract Assets, Contract Liabilities & Receivables

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): Presentation on the balance sheet depends on the relationship between the entity's performance and the customer's payment:
  • Receivable — an unconditional right to consideration (only the passage of time is required before payment is due).
  • Contract asset — a right to consideration that is conditional on something other than the passage of time (e.g. entity must complete further performance before invoicing/billing).
  • Contract liability — an obligation to transfer goods/services for which the entity has received consideration (or has an unconditional right to it) from the customer, i.e. amounts billed/collected in advance of performance.
  • Concept: These captions replace the older "unbilled revenue/receivable" and "advance from customer" line items and must be disclosed and reconciled (opening to closing balance) in the notes.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • A contract asset is not the same as a financial asset/receivable for impairment purposes under Ind AS 109 in all respects, but it is tested for impairment using Ind AS 109's expected credit loss model.
  • Watch numericals where partial billing creates simultaneous contract asset (unbilled excess of revenue over billing) and contract liability (billing in excess of revenue) across different contracts of the same entity — they are not netted against each other unless they relate to the same contract and right of set-off exists.

9. Principal vs Agent

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): An entity is a principal (recognises revenue on a gross basis) if it controls the specified good/service before it is transferred to the customer; it is an agent (recognises revenue on a net basis — i.e., only its fee/commission) if its role is to arrange for another party to provide the good/service.
  • Indicators of being a principal: primary responsibility for fulfilling the promise; inventory risk before/after the customer's order or on return; discretion in establishing the price for the good/service; exposure to credit risk for the amount receivable from the customer.
  • Concept: Determines whether revenue is reported at the full transaction value or only at the net margin/commission earned — a key issue for marketplaces, travel agents, and commission-based businesses.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Control (not just risk indicators) is the overriding principle — the indicators only support the control assessment, they don't override it.

10. Licences of Intellectual Property

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): Determine whether the licence provides:
  • Right to access the IP as it exists throughout the licence period → recognise revenue over time; applies when all of: (a) the contract requires (or the customer reasonably expects) the entity to undertake activities that significantly affect the IP; (b) the rights directly expose the customer to the positive/negative effects of those activities; and (c) those activities do not transfer a separate good/service as they occur. (Typically applies to "symbolic" IP such as brands, trade names, logos, franchise rights.)
  • Right to use the IP as it exists at the point the licence is granted → recognise revenue at a point in time. (Typically applies to "functional" IP such as software, completed media/content, drug formulas that have significant standalone functionality and are not expected to be updated by ongoing licensor activity.)
  • Concept: A licence is distinct from other promises in the contract unless it is not capable of being distinct (e.g., a licence that is simply a component of a combined tangible good).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Sales-based or usage-based royalties for licences of IP are recognised only when (or as) the later of the usage/sale occurs or the PO is satisfied — a specific exception to the general variable-consideration constraint approach.

11. Warranties

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • If the customer has the option to purchase the warranty separately (priced/negotiated separately) → account for it as a distinct performance obligation (service-type warranty) — allocate a portion of the transaction price to it and recognise revenue over the warranty period.
  • If no separate purchase option — assess whether the warranty provides a service beyond assurance that the product complies with agreed-upon specifications (factors: whether warranty is required by law, length of coverage period, nature of tasks promised).
  • Assurance-type warranty only → not a separate PO; account for it as a provision under Ind AS 37 (expected cost of repairs/replacement).
  • Includes a service element → split into an assurance-type element (Ind AS 37 provision) and a service-type element (separate PO under Ind AS 115).
  • Concept: Distinguishes a warranty that is merely a quality guarantee (cost accrual) from one that is effectively a separately-priced extended-service contract (revenue deferral).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • A statutorily-mandated warranty is a strong (not conclusive) indicator of an assurance-type warranty.

12. Sale with a Right of Return, Bill-and-Hold & Repurchase Agreements

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Sale with a right of return: recognise revenue only for the consideration the entity expects to be entitled to (i.e., excluding expected returns), recognise a refund liability for the amount expected to be refunded, and recognise a separate asset (with corresponding adjustment to cost of sales) for the entity's right to recover the returned products.
  • Bill-and-hold arrangements — customer obtains control (revenue can be recognised) only if all of the following are met:
  1. The reason for the bill-and-hold arrangement is substantive (requested by the customer);
  2. The product is identified separately as belonging to the customer;
  3. The product is currently ready for physical transfer to the customer; and
  4. The entity cannot use the product or direct it to another customer.
  • Repurchase agreements:
  • Forward/call option (entity has an obligation/right to repurchase the asset): if the repurchase price is less than the original selling price and there is no significant economic incentive to exercise → account as a lease under Ind AS 116 (unless part of a sale and leaseback); if the repurchase price is equal to or greater than the original selling price → account as a financing arrangement.
  • Put option (customer has the right to require repurchase): if the customer has a significant economic incentive to exercise the right, or the repurchase price is ≥ the original selling price → account as a financing arrangement; otherwise, if repurchase price < expected market value of the asset and no significant economic incentive exists → account as a sale with a right of return.
  • Concept: These are specific application guidance carve-outs of the general five-step model for common structured/negotiated arrangements.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • ⚠️ VERIFY — the precise repurchase-price comparison thresholds above (repurchase price vs. original selling price vs. expected market value) are standard textbook articulations of Ind AS 115 Application Guidance; confirm exact wording/paragraph against the notified standard text before quoting paragraph numbers in an answer.
  • In bill-and-hold questions, examiners frequently test whether the "cannot direct the product to another customer" condition is genuinely met — a common failure point.

13. Presentation & Disclosure

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): Present contract assets, contract liabilities, and receivables from contracts with customers separately from other assets/liabilities in the balance sheet (or disclose separately in notes). Disclose:
  • Disaggregation of revenue (e.g. by type, geography, timing of transfer, market/customer type);
  • Opening and closing balances of contract assets, contract liabilities, and receivables, and significant changes during the period;
  • Revenue recognised in the period that was included in the opening contract liability balance;
  • Transaction price allocated to remaining (unsatisfied or partially satisfied) performance obligations, and when the entity expects to recognise it as revenue;
  • Significant judgements made in applying the standard (timing of satisfaction of POs, determining transaction price and amounts allocated to POs);
  • Information about assets recognised for costs to obtain or fulfil a contract, including the amortisation method used.
  • Concept: The disclosure objective is to enable users to understand the nature, amount, timing, and uncertainty of revenue and cash flows arising from contracts with customers.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Disclosure questions in exams often ask students to identify which disclosure is missing/inadequate in a given fact pattern — know the checklist above, not just the recognition mechanics.

13. Ind AS 116 - Leases

Where it fits: Replaces Ind AS 17; brings (almost) all leases onto the lessee's Balance Sheet as a Right-of-Use (ROU) asset and a Lease Liability — a favourite for practical problems combining PV, effective-interest amortisation and sale-and-leaseback numericals.

Note on this chapter's sourcing: The teacher's concept-note pages mapped to this chapter did not actually contain Ind AS 116 material (that OCR range covers Ind AS 2/7/8/10). This chapter has therefore been rebuilt entirely from the Ind AS 116 text and ICAI CA Final FR study material/BoS material, cross-checked against the sources listed at the end.

1. Definition of a Lease & Identifying a Lease

  • Key rule(s):
  • A contract is, or contains, a lease if it conveys the right to control the use of an identified asset for a period of time in exchange for consideration.
  • Two-part control test — BOTH must be met:
  1. The customer has the right to obtain substantially all of the economic benefits from use of the identified asset; AND
  2. The customer has the right to direct the use of the identified asset (decide how and for what purpose it is used).
  • An asset is not "identified" if the supplier has a substantive right to substitute the asset throughout the period of use (substitution right must be practically capable of being exercised AND economically beneficial to the supplier).
  • Concept: This "control" test (not "risk/reward" as under old Ind AS 17) decides whether a contract is a lease at all, before any lessee/lessor accounting question arises.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • A right of substitution that is merely protective (e.g., for repairs) does not fail the identified-asset test.
  • Capacity portions of a larger asset can be an identified asset only if physically distinct (e.g., a specified floor of a building) — a capacity/output portion that is not physically distinct is not an identified asset unless it represents substantially all the capacity.
  • Definitions:
TermMeaning
Lease termNon-cancellable period of the lease + periods covered by an option to extend if the lessee is reasonably certain to exercise it + periods covered by an option to terminate if the lessee is reasonably certain not to exercise it
Commencement dateDate on which the lessor makes the underlying asset available for use by the lessee
Interest rate implicit in the leaseThe rate that causes the PV of (a) the lease payments and (b) the unguaranteed residual value to equal the sum of the fair value of the underlying asset and any initial direct costs of the lessor
Incremental borrowing rate (IBR)Rate the lessee would have to pay to borrow, over a similar term and with similar security, funds to obtain an asset of similar value in a similar economic environment
Initial direct costsIncremental costs of obtaining a lease that would not have been incurred if the lease had not been obtained

2. Recognition Exemptions — Short-Term Leases & Low-Value Assets

  • Key rule(s):
  • Short-term lease: lease term of 12 months or less at the commencement date, AND does not contain a purchase option.
  • Low-value asset: assessed on an absolute basis (value when new), based on the value of the asset, not its significance to the lessee. Ind AS 116 does not prescribe a specific monetary threshold — illustrative examples given in the standard's material are tablets, PCs, small items of office furniture, telephones, etc.
  • Both exemptions are elections: short-term lease election is made by class of underlying asset; low-value asset election can be made on a lease-by-lease basis.
  • If elected, the lessee recognises lease payments as an expense on a straight-line basis (or another systematic basis if more representative) over the lease term — i.e., no ROU asset / lease liability is recognised.
  • Concept: These are the two lessee-side practical expedients that keep small/short leases off balance sheet, avoiding disproportionate compliance cost.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • A lease is not short-term merely because the remaining term at the reporting date is ≤ 12 months — the test is applied at the commencement date.
  • ⚠️ VERIFY — no rupee/dollar figure is fixed by the standard for "low value"; some commentary illustratively cites an amount (often quoted as USD 5,000 when new) purely as a rule-of-thumb from IASB's Basis for Conclusions — this is not a bright-line test in Ind AS 116 itself and should not be quoted as a hard threshold in the exam.
  • A sub-lease of an asset should be assessed by reference to the ROU asset (not the underlying asset) for the low-value exemption.

3. Lessee Accounting — Initial Recognition

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Initial measurement of the Lease Liability:

Lease Liability = PV(Lease payments not yet paid at commencement date)
discounted using:

  • the interest rate implicit in the lease, if that rate can be readily determined; else
  • the lessee's incremental borrowing rate (IBR).

Lease payments comprised in the above PV include:

  • Fixed payments (less any lease incentives receivable)
  • Variable lease payments that depend on an index or a rate (e.g., linked to CPI)
  • Exercise price of a purchase option, if reasonably certain to be exercised
  • Payments of penalties for terminating the lease, if the lease term reflects the lessee exercising a termination option
  • Amounts expected to be payable under residual value guarantees

Initial measurement of the Right-of-Use (ROU) Asset (cost model):

ROU Asset (Cost) = Initial measurement of lease liability + Lease payments made at/before commencement (less incentives received) + Initial direct costs + Estimated dismantling/restoration costs (Ind AS 37 basis)

  • Concept: Every lease within scope (other than the two exemptions above) puts a liability (PV of future rentals) and a corresponding right-of-use asset on the lessee's balance sheet at commencement.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Variable payments linked to sales/usage (contingent rents) are excluded from the lease liability measurement and expensed as incurred — only index/rate-linked variable payments go into the PV.
  • Dismantling/restoration cost estimate is included in ROU asset cost only if the lessee incurs an obligation for it — not automatically for every lease.

4. Lessee Accounting — Subsequent Measurement

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Lease Liability — unwind using the effective interest method:

Interest Expense for the period = Opening Lease Liability × Discount rate
Closing Lease Liability = Opening Lease Liability + Interest Expense - Lease payments made

ROU Asset (cost model) — depreciated per Ind AS 16 principles:

  • If ownership transfers to the lessee by the end of the lease term, OR the cost of the ROU asset reflects that the lessee will exercise a purchase option → depreciate over the useful life of the underlying asset.
  • Otherwise → depreciate over the shorter of the lease term and the useful life of the ROU asset.
  • ROU asset carrying amount is also reduced for impairment (Ind AS 36) and adjusted for any remeasurement of the lease liability.
  • Concept: The lessee effectively splits each rental payment into a finance-cost (interest) component and a liability-reduction component (like a loan repayment schedule), while separately depreciating the ROU asset — very similar to old finance-lease accounting, now applied almost universally.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Interest expense (P&L, financing) + Depreciation (P&L, operating) together will typically exceed straight-line rent expense in the early years of a lease and be lower in later years — a classic exam discussion point (front-loaded P&L charge under Ind AS 116 vs straight-line expense under the old operating-lease model).
  • Do not straight-line the ROU asset depreciation and simultaneously ignore the interest unwinding on the liability — both movements must be tracked every year.

5. Reassessment & Lease Modifications

  • Key rule(s):
  • Reassessment of the lease liability (no change to the scope of the contract) is triggered by: a change in the lease term (revised assessment of extension/termination options), a change in assessment of a purchase option, a change in amounts expected under a residual value guarantee, or a change in future lease payments from a change in an index/rate once the adjustment to payments takes effect.
  • Discount rate on reassessment:
  • Where the change relates to the lease term or purchase option assessment → use a revised discount rate.
  • Where the change is only due to a change in index/rate (e.g., CPI-linked rent reset) → continue to use the original (unchanged) discount rate, unless the change in lease payments results from a change in floating interest rates, in which case a revised discount rate is used.
  • Lease modification (change in scope/consideration of the contract):
  • If the modification increases scope by adding a right to use additional assets AND consideration increases by an amount commensurate with the stand-alone price of the increase (with appropriate adjustments) → account for it as a separate, new lease.
  • All other modifications → remeasure the lease liability using a revised discount rate on the modification's effective date; for a decrease in scope, decrease the ROU asset carrying amount proportionately and recognise any resulting gain/loss in P&L; for other modifications, adjust the ROU asset by the amount of remeasurement.
  • Concept: Reassessment updates estimates within the same contract; modification changes the contract's scope/price and can trigger fresh lease accounting.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Students commonly forget that a reduction in lease scope requires a partial de-recognition gain/loss, not just a liability reduction — this is a favourite numerical twist.

6. Sale and Leaseback

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Step 1 — Apply Ind AS 115 to determine whether the transfer of the asset qualifies as a sale (i.e., control of the asset transfers to the buyer).
  • Step 2 (if it IS a sale): the seller-lessee measures the retained ROU asset and gain/loss as follows:

ROU Asset retained by seller-lessee = Previous carrying amount of asset × PV(Leaseback payments)Fair value of the asset at date of sale
Gain/loss recognised in P&L = Total gain/loss on sale × ≤ft(1 - PV(Leaseback payments)Fair value of the asset)
i.e., the seller-lessee recognises only the gain/loss relating to the rights transferred to the buyer-lessor; the portion relating to the ROU retained is deferred (not recognised immediately).

  • Step 2 (if it is NOT a sale, e.g., a repurchase option exists that fails the Ind AS 115 sale test): the transaction is a financing arrangement — the seller-lessee continues to recognise the transferred asset and recognises a financial liability equal to the sale proceeds (accounted for under Ind AS 109).
  • Concept: Sale-and-leaseback accounting first asks "was this really a sale?" under Ind AS 115, then defers recognition of gain attributable to the portion of the asset the seller continues to use via the leaseback.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • ⚠️ VERIFY — the ROU-asset-proportion / gain-recognition mechanics above are the standard illustrative-example approach used in ICAI numericals; work through the specific numbers in the standard's illustrative example (or the ICAI study material worked example) to confirm the exact allocation logic before an exam attempt, since variations exist when sale proceeds/fair value differ from the asset's fair value.
  • ⚠️ VERIFY — an amendment to Ind AS 116 (aligned with the IASB's "Lease Liability in a Sale and Leaseback" amendment to IFRS 16) refines how a seller-lessee subsequently measures the lease liability in a sale-and-leaseback so that it continues to not recognise any gain/loss relating to the retained right-of-use even on subsequent remeasurement; confirm the applicable effective date/notification for the current CA Final syllabus attempt before relying on the exact mechanics in a numerical.
  • If sale proceeds are not at fair value, adjustments are made: (a) any below-market element is treated as prepayment of lease payments, and (b) any above-market element is treated as additional financing provided by the buyer-lessor to the seller-lessee.

7. Lessor Accounting — Classification & Measurement

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Lessor accounting is largely unchanged from Ind AS 17 — a lease is classified, at inception, as either a Finance Lease or an Operating Lease.
  • Classified as a Finance Lease if it transfers substantially all the risks and rewards incidental to ownership of the underlying asset; indicators include:
  • Ownership transfers to the lessee by the end of the lease term
  • Lessee has a purchase option reasonably certain to be exercised
  • Lease term is for the major part of the economic life of the asset, even if title is not transferred
  • PV of lease payments amounts to substantially all of the fair value of the underlying asset at inception
  • The underlying asset is of such a specialised nature that only the lessee can use it without major modification
  • If none of the above apply substantially → classify as an Operating Lease.
  • Finance lease (Lessor):

Net Investment in the Lease = PV(Lease payments receivable) + PV(Unguaranteed residual value)
both discounted at the interest rate implicit in the lease; and:
Unearned Finance Income = Gross Investment in the Lease - Net Investment in the Lease
where Gross investment in the lease = lease payments receivable + unguaranteed residual value. Finance income is recognised over the lease term reflecting a constant periodic rate of return on the net investment.

  • Operating lease (Lessor): the lessor continues to recognise the underlying asset (depreciating it under Ind AS 16/Ind AS 38/Ind AS 40 as applicable) and recognises lease income (usually) on a straight-line basis over the lease term; initial direct costs are added to the carrying amount of the underlying asset and expensed over the lease term on the same basis as lease income.
  • Concept: Unlike the lessee model (single on-balance-sheet approach for almost all leases), the lessor dual model (finance vs operating) survives unchanged from the earlier standard.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Do not confuse the lessee's "single model" (ROU asset + lease liability for all leases, subject to the two exemptions) with the lessor's continuing "dual model" (finance vs operating) — a very common conceptual mix-up in exams.
  • In a finance lease, the lessor derecognises the underlying asset and recognises a receivable (net investment in the lease) instead.

8. Presentation & Disclosure

  • Key rule(s):
  • Lessee presents ROU assets either separately on the face of the Balance Sheet or discloses them separately in the notes (by class of underlying asset), and presents lease liabilities separately from other liabilities (or discloses which line items include them).
  • In the Statement of Profit and Loss, a lessee presents interest expense on the lease liability (as part of finance costs) separately from the depreciation charge on the ROU asset.
  • In the Statement of Cash Flows: cash payments for the principal portion of the lease liability are presented within financing activities; interest paid is classified per the entity's accounting policy for interest paid (consistently with Ind AS 7); payments for short-term leases, low-value asset leases and variable lease payments not included in the lease liability are presented within operating activities.
  • Concept: Presentation rules ensure the split between financing (principal + interest on lease liability) and operating (short-term/low-value/variable rent not capitalised) cash flows is transparent to users.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • A common cash-flow-statement question twist: only the principal repayment component of a lease rental goes to financing activities; short-term/low-value lease payments remain in operating activities since no liability was ever recognised for them.

14. Ind AS 102 - Share Based Payments

Where it fits: Governs how an entity accounts for transactions in which it receives goods or services (most commonly employee services) in exchange for its own equity instruments (ESOPs/ESPS), or for cash/other assets whose amount is linked to the price of its equity instruments (SARs) — a high-frequency practical/theory topic in FR and a key linkage point with Ind AS 12 (deferred tax) and Ind AS 101 (first-time adoption).

Scope, Meaning & Types of SBP

  • Key rule(s):
  • Ind AS 102 applies to equity-settled, cash-settled, and share-based payment transactions with a choice of settlement (by the entity or the counterparty), for goods or services received (including employee services).
  • It applies whether or not the entity can identify specifically the goods/services received.
  • Concept: Covers Employee Stock Option Plans (ESOP), Employee Stock Purchase Schemes (ESPS), and Share Appreciation Rights (SAR) granted to employees or other parties (e.g., suppliers) in return for goods/services.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Transactions with employees are always measured with reference to the fair value of the equity instruments granted (employee services usually cannot be measured reliably); transactions with non-employees are measured, wherever possible, at the fair value of the goods/services received.
  • Definitions:
TermMeaning
Grant dateDate the entity and counterparty agree to the SBP arrangement (both parties have a shared understanding of terms/conditions).
Vesting dateDate on which the counterparty becomes unconditionally entitled to the award.
Vesting periodPeriod between grant date and vesting date.
SARShare Appreciation Right — cash-settled award linked to share-price appreciation.

Equity-Settled SBP — Recognition & Measurement

  • Key formula(s):

Cumulative Expense (to date) = No. of instruments expected to vest × Fair Value at Grant Date × Time elapsedTotal vesting period

Expense for the period = Cumulative expense to date - Cumulative expense already recognised

  • Concept: For equity-settled awards, goods/services (and a corresponding increase in other equity) are recognised over the vesting period at the fair value of the equity instruments at grant date — this grant-date fair value is fixed and never remeasured for subsequent share-price movements.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Fair value of the equity instrument is used, not intrinsic value, as the primary/default basis.
  • ⚠️ VERIFY — In the rare case that the fair value of equity instruments granted cannot be estimated reliably (e.g., unquoted entities lacking data for an option-pricing model), the standard permits using the intrinsic value method instead, measured initially at grant date and remeasured at the end of each reporting period and at settlement, with changes recognised in P&L — confirm this fallback wording against the current ICAI study material before quoting it as a primary rule in an answer, since it is an exception, not the norm.
  • Do not confuse this with cash-settled remeasurement — under the fair-value method (the default for equity-settled), there is no remeasurement after grant date.

Vesting Conditions & Graded Vesting

  • Key rule(s):
  • Vesting conditions are classified as Service conditions, Performance conditions (further split into Market conditions and Non-market performance conditions).
  • Market conditions (e.g., a target share price or TSR hurdle) are built into the grant-date fair value estimate itself and are never revisited, irrespective of whether the condition is actually met — expense is recognised as long as all other (non-market) vesting conditions are satisfied.
  • Service conditions and non-market performance conditions are not factored into the fair value; instead, the entity revises, at each reporting date, its estimate of the number of instruments expected to vest, and the cumulative expense is trued up accordingly.
  • Graded vesting (an award vesting in tranches over different dates) is treated as multiple separate grants — each tranche has its own vesting period and is valued/expensed independently.
  • Concept: Distinguishes what changes the number of instruments recognised (non-market conditions — estimate is trued up) from what is baked permanently into the valuation (market conditions — never trued up).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • A classic exam trap: students wrongly reverse expense already recognised when a market condition (e.g., share price target) is missed — this is incorrect; expense is not reversed for failure of a market condition as long as the service condition is met.
  • For graded vesting, compute fair value and vesting period separately for each tranche — do not average across the whole award.

Modification, Cancellation & Settlement

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Incremental Fair Value = FV of modified instrument at modification date - FV of original instrument at modification date (immediately before modification)

  • If the modification increases the fair value of the award (or otherwise benefits the employee, e.g., reduces exercise price, adds instruments, shortens vesting), the incremental fair value is recognised as additional expense over the remaining vesting period, in addition to the expense based on the original grant-date fair value.
  • If the modification decreases total fair value (or is otherwise disadvantageous), the entity ignores the decrease and continues to recognise the expense as if the modification had never occurred (based on original grant-date fair value).
  • Cancellation/settlement during the vesting period (other than forfeiture for failing a vesting condition) is accounted for as an acceleration of vesting — the entity immediately recognises the amount that would otherwise have been recognised for services over the remainder of the vesting period.
  • Concept: Modifications and cancellations are designed so that a company cannot use them to reduce a recognised (or to-be-recognised) expense below what employees have already earned.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • "Cancellation" and "forfeiture for not meeting a service/non-market condition" are not the same — forfeiture simply stops future expense (with a true-up to zero for that tranche, per the graded-vesting logic); cancellation by the entity (not due to forfeiture) triggers immediate acceleration of the remaining unrecognised expense.
  • Any payment made to the employee on cancellation/repurchase up to the fair value of the equity instruments is charged to equity; any excess payment above that fair value is an expense in P&L.

Cash-Settled SBP (e.g., SARs)

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Liability (at each reporting date) = No. of SARs vested / expected to vest × Fair Value of SAR at that reporting date × Time elapsedVesting period

  • The entity remeasures the fair value of the liability at the end of each reporting period and at the date of settlement, with all changes in fair value recognised in profit or loss.
  • Concept: Cash-settled awards create a liability, not equity, because the entity's obligation is to pay cash (or other assets) based on the value of its equity instruments (e.g., Stock Appreciation Rights).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • The clearest exam differentiator versus equity-settled: cash-settled = liability, remeasured every period through P&L, until settlement; equity-settled = equity, grant-date fair value fixed, never remeasured.

SBP With a Choice of Settlement (Compound Instruments)

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Fair Value of Equity Component = Fair Value of Equity Alternative - Fair Value of Cash (Debt) Alternative at Grant Date

  • Counterparty has the choice of cash or equity settlement → the entity has granted a compound financial instrument with a debt component (counterparty's right to demand cash) and an equity component (counterparty's right to demand equity instead). The debt component is measured first at fair value and remeasured each period through P&L until settlement; the (residual) equity component is fixed at grant date and not remeasured.
  • Entity has the choice of cash or equity settlement → treatment is binary: the whole transaction is accounted for as cash-settled only if the entity has a present obligation to settle in cash; otherwise it is accounted for as equity-settled.
  • Concept: Who holds the settlement option (counterparty vs entity) drives whether you split the instrument into debt + equity components or pick one treatment wholesale.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Do not default to "equity settled" whenever the entity has historically settled similar past awards in cash — a present obligation (constructive or legal) to pay cash forces cash-settled treatment even where the contract nominally gives the entity a choice.

Deferred Tax Interaction (Cross-reference: Ind AS 12)

  • Key rule(s):
  • The carrying amount of an SBP transaction for tax-base purposes is always Nil (no asset/liability is recognised for tax on the underlying options themselves in the entity's books in the same way).
  • Tax base = the amount the tax authorities will permit as a deduction in future periods; if this is not known at the reporting date, it is estimated using information available at that date, and where the future tax deduction depends on the entity's future share price, the estimate is based on the option's intrinsic value at the end of the reporting period.
  • The difference between this tax base and the (nil) carrying amount is a deductible temporary difference (DTD), giving rise to a Deferred Tax Asset (DTA), subject to the normal Ind AS 12 recognition criteria (probable future taxable profits).
  • Deferred tax on this DTA is recognised in P&L, except: if the estimated/actual future tax deduction exceeds the related cumulative remuneration expense already recognised, the excess of the associated current/deferred tax is recognised directly in equity.
  • Concept: This links directly to Ind AS 12's "Practical Application & Exceptions" coverage of SBP (verified against the same source material used for the Ind AS 12 chapter of this guide).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Common examiner trap: students recognise the entire deferred tax on SBP through P&L without checking whether the estimated tax deduction exceeds cumulative book expense — the excess portion must go to equity, not P&L.
  • Definitions:
TermMeaning
Deductible Temporary Difference (DTD) on SBPTax base (estimated future deduction, often based on intrinsic value) minus Nil carrying amount.

First-Time Adoption Exemption (Cross-reference: Ind AS 101)

  • Key rule(s):
  • Ind AS 101 gives a first-time adopter an optional exemption: it is encouraged, but not required, to apply Ind AS 102 to equity instruments that had already vested before the date of transition to Ind AS.
  • Concept: Avoids forcing entities to restate/re-value long-vested employee equity awards purely for transition purposes.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • This is an optional relief, not mandatory — an entity may still choose to apply Ind AS 102 retrospectively to pre-transition vested awards if it wishes.

Disclosures (Brief)

  • Key rule(s):
  • An entity discloses information that enables users to understand: (a) the nature and extent of SBP arrangements during the period, (b) how the fair value of goods/services or of the equity instruments granted was determined, and (c) the effect of SBP transactions on the entity's profit/loss for the period and its financial position.
  • Concept: Standard three-limb disclosure objective (nature/extent, measurement basis, P&L/balance-sheet effect) common to most Ind AS disclosure requirements — useful as a fallback answer structure for disclosure-based theory questions.

15. Ind AS 103 - Business Combinations

Where it fits: Governs the accounting for acquisitions of businesses under the acquisition method — identifying the acquirer, measuring identifiable assets/liabilities/NCI at fair value, and computing goodwill or a bargain purchase gain. It is the foundation standard for the whole consolidation block (Ind AS 110/111/112) and links closely with Ind AS 36 (goodwill impairment), Ind AS 38 (intangibles recognised on acquisition), Ind AS 12 (deferred tax on fair value adjustments), and Ind AS 109/32 (contingent consideration, acquisition costs of debt/equity).

Scope & Definition of a Business Combination

  • Key rule(s)/definitions:
  • A Business Combination is a transaction or event in which an acquirer obtains control of one or more businesses (control as defined in Ind AS 110).
  • Ind AS 103 does not apply to: (a) formation of a joint venture/joint arrangement (Ind AS 111); (b) acquisition of an asset or group of assets that does not constitute a business (allocate cost among identifiable assets/liabilities based on relative fair values — no goodwill arises); and (c) combinations of entities/businesses under common control (dealt with separately under Appendix C to Ind AS 103, using the pooling of interests method).
  • Definition of a "Business" (2020 amendment, applicable to acquisitions on/after 1 April 2020): an integrated set of activities and assets that is capable of being conducted and managed to provide a return (dividends, lower costs, or other economic benefits) to investors/owners. It must include, at a minimum, an input and a substantive process applied to that input that together significantly contribute to the ability to create outputs.
  • Optional Concentration Test: an entity may elect a simplified screen — if substantially all of the fair value of the gross assets acquired is concentrated in a single identifiable asset (or group of similar assets), the set is not a business and no further assessment is needed (no goodwill, treat as an asset acquisition).
  • Concept: The very first exam step is to decide "business" vs "mere asset(s)" — this drives whether Ind AS 103 (goodwill possible) or ordinary asset-acquisition accounting (cost allocated by relative FV, no goodwill) applies.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Common-control combinations are explicitly excluded from the main Ind AS 103 acquisition method — do not compute goodwill/bargain purchase there; apply Appendix C (pooling of interests: assets/liabilities at existing book values, no fair valuation, no new goodwill other than pre-existing goodwill in the transferor's books, difference between consideration and net assets adjusted against reserves/capital reserve).
  • If the concentration test is not applied (or not met), fall back to the full input/process/output assessment before concluding it isn't a business.
  • Definitions:
TermMeaning
AcquirerThe entity that obtains control of the acquiree.
AcquireeThe business (or businesses) over which the acquirer obtains control.
Acquisition dateThe date the acquirer effectively obtains control of the acquiree.
BusinessAn integrated set of activities/assets capable of providing a return via outputs, requiring an input and a substantive process.

The Acquisition Method — Four Steps

  • Key rule(s): Every business combination in scope is accounted for by applying the acquisition method, comprising:
  1. Identify the acquirer (guidance from Ind AS 110's control model; in a reverse acquisition the legal acquirer may be the accounting acquiree — see below).
  2. Determine the acquisition date (normally the closing/completion date, i.e., the date control legally transfers).
  3. Recognise and measure the identifiable assets acquired, liabilities assumed, and any non-controlling interest (NCI) in the acquiree.
  4. Recognise and measure goodwill or a gain from a bargain purchase.
  • Concept: This four-step sequence is the exam skeleton for every Ind AS 103 numerical — always work through it in this order.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Acquisition date drives which set of identifiable assets/liabilities (and their fair values) are brought on to the acquirer's books — not the agreement/signing date if different from the control-transfer date.

Recognition & Measurement of Identifiable Assets, Liabilities and Intangibles

  • Key rule(s):
  • Identifiable assets acquired and liabilities assumed are recognised separately from goodwill only if they meet the Ind AS Conceptual Framework's definitions of assets/liabilities at the acquisition date (and, for intangibles, meet the "identifiable" criterion — arising from contractual/legal rights, or separable).
  • Recognition is required irrespective of whether the item was recognised in the acquiree's own pre-combination financial statements (e.g., an internally generated brand or customer list not recognised by the acquiree can still be recognised by the acquirer if identifiable and reliably measurable).
  • Measurement principle: identifiable assets acquired and liabilities assumed are measured at their acquisition-date fair values (limited exceptions exist, e.g., income taxes per Ind AS 12, employee benefits per Ind AS 19, indemnification assets, share-based payment awards per Ind AS 102, and assets held for sale per Ind AS 105 at fair value less costs to sell).
  • If an intangible asset is not separable from goodwill (cannot be sold/transferred/licensed separately), it is not recognised separately and instead subsumed into goodwill.
  • Concept: Fair-valuing the whole net-asset package (not book values) is what makes Ind AS 103 different from the pooling-of-interests approach used for common-control deals.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • A classic trap: students only fair-value assets that were already on the acquiree's balance sheet — remember to also recognise new identifiable intangibles (brands, customer relationships, order backlog, non-compete agreements, technology) that meet the separability/contractual-legal criterion, at fair value.
  • Contingent liabilities of the acquiree that meet the definition of a liability are recognised at acquisition-date fair value even if not probable/reliably measurable under Ind AS 37 in the acquiree's own books (Ind AS 103 has a lower recognition threshold at the acquisition date for this purpose).

Consideration Transferred

  • Key formula(s):

Consideration Transferred = FV of Assets given by Acquirer + Liabilities incurred by Acquirer to former owners + FV of Equity Instruments issued + FV of Contingent Consideration

  • Concept: Consideration is measured at acquisition-date fair value, and can include cash, other assets, a business/subsidiary of the acquirer, contingent consideration, and equity instruments (ordinary/preference shares, options, warrants) issued by the acquirer.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Acquisition-related costs (legal, due diligence, valuation, advisory/finder's fees, general administrative costs of running an internal acquisitions department) are expensed as incurred — they are not part of consideration transferred and do not enter the goodwill computation.
  • Exception: costs to issue debt securities are accounted for under Ind AS 109 (deducted from the liability/effective interest), and costs to issue equity securities are accounted for under Ind AS 32 (deducted from equity/securities premium) — i.e., issuance costs of the financing instruments themselves, not the M&A advisory costs.
  • Contingent consideration is recognised at acquisition-date fair value as part of consideration; the resulting obligation is classified as a liability or as equity per Ind AS 32. Liability-classified contingent consideration is subsequently remeasured at each reporting date with changes recognised in profit or loss (unless it is a measurement-period adjustment); equity-classified contingent consideration is not remeasured.

Goodwill / Bargain Purchase Gain

  • Key formula(s):

Goodwill = [Consideration Transferred + Amount of NCI in Acquiree + FV of Acquirer's Previously Held Equity Interest (if any)] - Net Identifiable Assets Acquired (FV of identifiable assets − FV of identifiable liabilities)

  • If the above figure is negative, it represents a Bargain Purchase Gain rather than goodwill.
  • Concept: Goodwill is the residual — the premium paid over and above the fair value of the identifiable net assets acquired — and represents future economic benefits from assets not individually identifiable (assembled workforce, synergies, etc.).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Before recognising a bargain purchase, the acquirer must reassess whether it has correctly identified all assets acquired/liabilities assumed and correctly measured all the goodwill-formula components — a bargain purchase is meant to be a rare outcome (e.g., a forced/distress sale), not a default plug for a negative result.
  • Ind AS carve-out from IFRS 3: under Ind AS 103, a bargain purchase gain is recognised in Other Comprehensive Income (OCI) and accumulated in equity as Capital Reserve — this differs from IFRS 3, which recognises it straight in profit or loss. Do not recognise it in P&L on an Ind AS paper.
  • Goodwill is never amortised; it is tested for impairment annually (and whenever there is an indicator) under Ind AS 36, allocated to the relevant cash-generating unit(s).

Measurement of Non-Controlling Interest (NCI)

  • Key rule(s): Ind AS 103 permits, on a transaction-by-transaction basis, a choice between two measurement bases for NCI at the acquisition date (for NCI components that are present ownership interests entitling holders to a proportionate share of net assets on liquidation):
  1. Fair Value Method ("full goodwill" method): NCI recognised at its acquisition-date fair value. Under this method goodwill attributable to the NCI is also recognised (i.e., goodwill on 100% of the acquiree).
  2. Proportionate Share of Net Assets Method ("PSNA" / partial-goodwill method): NCI recognised at its proportionate share of the acquiree's identifiable net assets (at their acquisition-date fair values). Under this method, goodwill is recognised only in respect of the parent's share (no goodwill attributed to NCI).
  • Concept: The choice only changes how much of NCI's share of goodwill is grossed up on the balance sheet — it does not change the parent's share of net assets/goodwill.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • The election is available only for present-ownership NCI instruments; all other components of NCI are measured at acquisition-date fair value (no choice).
  • Re-read the question carefully — many numericals specify "NCI at fair value" vs "NCI at proportionate share"; picking the wrong method changes both the NCI balance and the goodwill figure.

Business Combinations Achieved in Stages (Step Acquisitions)

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • When the acquirer already holds a non-controlling equity interest in the acquiree and then obtains control (a "step acquisition"), the previously held equity interest is remeasured at its acquisition-date fair value.

Gain/Loss on Remeasurement = Acquisition-date Fair Value of Previously Held Interest - Carrying Amount of Previously Held Interest

  • This gain/loss is recognised in profit or loss (or, if the previously held equity interest had its changes in value recognised in OCI, it is recognised in OCI as appropriate — reflecting how the instrument's gains/losses were being tracked before the step-up).
  • The remeasured fair value of the previously held interest then feeds into the goodwill formula above as one of the "consideration" components.
  • Concept: Ind AS 103 treats the step-up to control as effectively a deemed disposal-and-reacquisition of the old stake at fair value, followed by a fresh acquisition-method computation for the whole entity.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • ⚠️ VERIFY — where the previously held stake was classified as an equity instrument measured at FVTOCI under Ind AS 109 (an irrevocable OCI election), amounts already accumulated in OCI are, per Ind AS 109's general rule, not recyclable to P&L even on this remeasurement; confirm the precise interaction/treatment prescribed in the current ICAI study material before quoting it as a definitive rule in an answer, since sources vary in how explicitly they state this interaction for Ind AS 103 step-acquisition scenarios.

Reverse Acquisitions

  • Key rule(s): A reverse acquisition occurs when the entity that issues shares (the legal acquirer/parent, e.g., a smaller listed shell) is identified as the accounting acquiree, while the entity whose owners receive the shares (the legal subsidiary, e.g., a larger operating company) is identified as the accounting acquirer — because, applying Ind AS 110's control guidance (e.g., relative voting rights of former owners post-combination, composition of governing body, relative size), the legal subsidiary's former owners actually gain control of the combined entity.
  • Concept: Legal form (who issued shares / who is the parent) is overridden by substance (who controls the combined group) in identifying the acquirer.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: In consolidated financial statements of a reverse acquisition, they are prepared as a continuation of the accounting acquirer's financial statements (with an adjustment to the legal capital structure to reflect that of the legal parent).

Measurement Period

  • Key rule(s)/thresholds:
  • If the initial accounting for a business combination is incomplete by the end of the reporting period in which the combination occurs, the acquirer reports provisional amounts for the items not yet finalised.
  • During the measurement period, the acquirer retrospectively adjusts the provisional amounts recognised at the acquisition date (with a corresponding adjustment to goodwill) to reflect new information about facts and circumstances that existed as of the acquisition date.
  • The measurement period ends as soon as the acquirer receives the information it was seeking (or learns it cannot be obtained), but in no case can it exceed one year (12 months) from the acquisition date.
  • Adjustments arising after the measurement period ends, or from events occurring after the acquisition date, are not measurement-period adjustments — they are accounted for prospectively as normal changes in estimate (Ind AS 8) or as separate transactions/events in the relevant period's P&L, not against goodwill.
  • Concept: Gives acquirers up to a year to finalise fair valuations (valuers, actuaries, tax positions, contingent consideration) without a fresh P&L hit for every subsequent refinement, provided the new information relates back to acquisition-date conditions.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Exam trap: a fair-value refinement made 14 months after acquisition, or one driven by a post-acquisition-date event (e.g., an asset damaged after acquisition), is not a measurement-period adjustment even if within 12 months — check both the time limit and whether the new information pertains to acquisition-date facts.

Subsequent Measurement & Related Cross-References

  • Key rule(s):
  • Goodwill: carried at cost less accumulated impairment losses (Ind AS 36); never amortised; never reversed once impaired.
  • Reacquired rights, indemnification assets, contingent liabilities recognised at acquisition, and assets/liabilities arising from contingent consideration are subsequently measured per the applicable standard (Ind AS 37, Ind AS 109/32, etc.), consistent with their nature.
  • Deferred tax consequences of acquisition-date fair value adjustments (e.g., on assets stepped up to FV) are recognised per Ind AS 12, and any impact on goodwill is a measurement-period item only if it relates to acquisition-date facts.
  • Concept: Ind AS 103 hands off subsequent-period accounting for each recognised item to its own governing standard — it only governs the acquisition-date recognition/measurement and goodwill computation.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Don't keep applying Ind AS 103 logic in year 2+ — switch to the item's normal standard (Ind AS 36 for goodwill, Ind AS 38 for intangibles, Ind AS 12 for tax, etc.) once initial recognition is complete.

16. Ind AS 110, 27 & 112 - Consolidated & Separate Financial Statements, Disclosure of Interests in Other Entities

Where it fits: Ind AS 110 decides who must be brought into a single set of group accounts (the "control" test) and how to mechanically consolidate them (Cost of Control/Goodwill, NCI, elimination); Ind AS 27 governs how a parent measures those same investments in its own (separate) financial statements; Ind AS 112 is the disclosure standard that forces the group to explain its interests — consolidated or not — to users. Read together with Ind AS 103 (Business Combinations, for fair-valuing the acquiree) and Ind AS 28 (Associates & JVs, equity method).

PART A — IND AS 110: CONSOLIDATED FINANCIAL STATEMENTS (CFS)

Objective & Scope

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Ind AS 110 requires an entity that is a parent to present CFS, consolidating all entities it controls — subsidiaries are consolidated on a line-by-line basis (not proportionate/equity method).
  • CFS combine the FS of parent and subsidiaries as if they were a single economic entity.
  • Concept: The standard is built entirely around one test — does the reporting entity have "control" over the investee? If yes → consolidate; if no (but significant influence/joint control) → Ind AS 28/111 (equity method) applies instead.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Do not confuse consolidation (Ind AS 110, line-by-line) with the equity method (Ind AS 28, one-line "share of profit"); ownership % alone is not decisive — control is a substance-based test.

Definition of Control

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Control exists ⇔ Power over the investee ∩ Exposure/rights to variable returns ∩ Ability to use power to affect own returns

  • All three elements must be present simultaneously; control is reassessed if facts and circumstances indicate a change in one or more of these elements.
  • Definitions:
ElementMeaning
PowerExisting rights that give the current ability to direct the investee's relevant activities (the activities that significantly affect the investee's returns)
Variable returnsReturns that are not fixed and can vary with the investee's performance — can be positive, negative, or both (dividends, remuneration, fees, synergies, tax benefits, residual interests, etc.)
LinkageThe investor must be able to use its power to actually affect the amount of its own variable returns — power without this linkage (e.g., a pure agent) does not give control
  • Concept: Voting rights are the most common source of power (usually >50% of voting rights = power over relevant activities decided by vote), but power can also arise from contractual arrangements, potential voting rights, or de facto (practical) control.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: A large minority shareholding (e.g., 40%) can still amount to control if the remaining shares are widely dispersed among many small unrelated holders and past voting patterns show the investor's resolutions consistently pass — this is de facto control, tested using the size of the holding relative to other holders, potential voting rights held, and historical voting patterns at general meetings.

Assessing Power — Potential Voting Rights & Principal vs Agent

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Potential voting rights (options, convertible instruments, warrants) are considered in the control assessment only if they are substantive — i.e., the holder has the practical ability to exercise them (not merely protective/contingent on a remote future event).
  • Principal vs Agent test (relevant when a decision-maker exercises power on behalf of others, e.g., a fund manager): a decision-maker that is merely an agent does NOT control the investee even though it has decision-making power delegated to it.
  • Factors to distinguish principal from agent:
  • Scope of the decision-maker's authority over the investee;
  • Rights held by other parties (substantive removal/"kick-out" rights);
  • Remuneration to which the decision-maker is entitled (is it commensurate with services, arm's length?);
  • The decision-maker's exposure to variability of returns from other interests it holds in the investee (beyond its management fee).
  • Concept: An agent acts primarily on behalf of, and for the benefit of, another party (the principal) and therefore does not itself have control despite holding decision-making power.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Don't assume every fund/asset manager automatically escapes consolidation — if its own economic exposure (e.g., a large co-investment) combined with its decision-making rights is substantial, it may in substance be acting as principal.

Exemption from Preparing CFS

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): A parent need not present CFS if and only if ALL of the following are met:
  • It is a wholly-owned subsidiary, or a partially-owned subsidiary of another entity, and all its other owners (including non-voting owners) have been informed about, and do not object to, the parent not presenting CFS;
  • Its debt or equity instruments are not traded in a public market (domestic/foreign stock exchange or OTC market, incl. local/regional markets);
  • It did not file, nor is it in the process of filing, its FS with a securities regulator (e.g., SEBI) for the purpose of issuing instruments in a public market; AND
  • Its ultimate or any intermediate parent produces CFS available for public use that comply with Ind AS.
  • Concept: This exemption avoids duplicate consolidation up a multi-tier group where a higher-level parent already consolidates.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: All four conditions are cumulative (AND, not OR) — missing even one condition (e.g., listed debt) means CFS must still be prepared.

Investment Entities Exception

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): An investment entity does not consolidate its subsidiaries (except subsidiaries that provide investment-related services); instead, it measures investments in subsidiaries at fair value through profit or loss under Ind AS 109.
  • An entity qualifies as an investment entity if it meets all of the following typical characteristics:
  • Obtains funds from one or more investors for the purpose of providing them with investment management services;
  • Commits to its investors that its business purpose is solely to generate returns from capital appreciation, investment income, or both; AND
  • Measures and evaluates the performance of substantially all of its investments on a fair value basis.
  • Concept: This is a narrow exception aimed at genuine investment funds/PE vehicles, where fair value information is more decision-useful to investors than a consolidated line-by-line picture.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: ⚠️ VERIFY — a parent of an investment entity that is not itself an investment entity must still consolidate the investment entity (at the investment entity's fair-valued amounts flowing up), rather than looking through to the underlying investee-of-investee.

Consolidation Procedures — Uniform Policies, Reporting Dates, Elimination

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Uniform accounting policies: if a group member uses different accounting policies, appropriate adjustments are made to its FS before consolidation so the whole group reports on consistent policies.
  • Same reporting date: parent and subsidiary FS used in consolidation must be drawn up to the same reporting date. If impracticable, the subsidiary's most recent FS are used, adjusted for the effects of significant transactions/events between the two dates, provided the difference between reporting dates is no more than three months (and is consistent period to period).
  • Elimination: intragroup balances, transactions, income and expenses are eliminated in full — including unrealised profits/losses on intragroup transactions — regardless of the NCI percentage (i.e., 100% elimination, not restricted to the parent's share).
  • Concept: These procedures ensure the CFS reflect the group as a single reporting entity with no artificial intragroup profit or double-counting.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: A very common exam trap — students eliminate unrealised intragroup profit only to the extent of the parent's shareholding; Ind AS 110 requires full elimination, with the resulting adjustment then allocated between parent and NCI in their respective proportions.

Non-Controlling Interest (NCI)

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

NCI at Acquisition (Proportionate Share Method) = NCI % × Fair Value of Subsidiary's Identifiable Net Assets at Acquisition Date

NCI at Acquisition (Fair Value Method) = Fair Value of NCI's Shareholding at Acquisition Date

NCI at Reporting Date = NCI at Acquisition + (NCI % × Post-acquisition change in Net Assets/Reserves) - NCI's share of Goodwill Impairment (Fair Value method only)

  • The choice between the two acquisition-date NCI measurement methods is available business-combination by business-combination (an Ind AS 103 choice that feeds directly into Ind AS 110 consolidation).
  • Concept: NCI represents the equity in a subsidiary not attributable, directly or indirectly, to the parent — it is presented within equity, separately from the parent's equity, in the Consolidated Balance Sheet.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Profit/loss and each component of OCI are attributed to the parent and NCI even if this results in the NCI having a deficit (negative) balance — unlike the old AS 21 approach of capping NCI losses at nil, Ind AS 110 requires full allocation, including into negative territory.

Goodwill / Capital Reserve on Consolidation (Cost of Control)

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Goodwill / (Capital Reserve) = [Consideration Transferred + NCI at Acquisition + FV of Previously Held Equity Interest (step acquisitions)] - Fair Value of Net Identifiable Assets Acquired

  • If the bracketed sum exceeds net identifiable assets → recognise as Goodwill (an asset, tested for impairment under Ind AS 36, not amortised).
  • If net identifiable assets exceed the bracketed sum → the excess is a bargain purchase gain, recognised directly as Capital Reserve (not routed through P&L, unlike Ind AS 103's general "gain on bargain purchase to P&L" rule for standalone business combinations — the CFS convention is to credit Capital Reserve).
  • Concept: This is the traditional "Cost of Control" working — it reconciles what the parent paid (plus NCI's stake) against the fair value of what was actually acquired.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Net identifiable assets must be measured at fair value as of the acquisition date (Ind AS 103 principles), not book value, unless the question states fair value equals book value; pre-acquisition (capital) profits of the subsidiary are used only in this working, never in Consolidated Reserves.

Consolidated Reserves & Surplus

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Consolidated Reserves = Parent's Own Reserves + (Parent's % × Post-acquisition Profits/Reserves of Subsidiary) - Unrealised Profit on Intragroup Transactions (Parent's share) - Parent's share of Goodwill Impairment

  • Concept: Only post-acquisition profits of the subsidiary (i.e., profits earned after the date control was obtained) are added to the parent's own reserves; pre-acquisition profits are "capital profits" absorbed into the Goodwill/Capital Reserve working instead.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: A classic error is mixing up capital profit (pre-acquisition, → Cost of Control working) vs revenue profit (post-acquisition, → Consolidated Reserves) when the acquisition happens mid-year — always time-apportion the subsidiary's profit for the year of acquisition unless actual figures as of the acquisition date are given.

Changes in Ownership Interest Without Loss of Control

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Adjustment Directly in Equity = Consideration Paid/Received - Change in NCI's Share of Subsidiary's Net Assets

  • When a parent buys additional shares in an already-controlled subsidiary, or sells some shares while retaining control, this is accounted for as an equity transaction between owners — no gain/loss is recognised in P&L, and goodwill is not remeasured.
  • Concept: As long as control is retained, changes in ownership % are transactions "between owners in their capacity as owners," not economic events of the reporting entity.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Do not recompute goodwill on a partial stake increase/decrease while control is retained — only the equity split between parent and NCI changes.

Loss of Control

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Gain/(Loss) on Loss of Control = [FV of Consideration Received + FV of any Retained Interest] - [Carrying Amount of Subsidiary's Net Assets (incl. Goodwill) + Carrying Amount of NCI at that date]

  • The gain/loss is recognised in P&L attributable to the parent; any amounts previously recognised in OCI relating to that subsidiary (e.g., FCTR) are reclassified/recycled to P&L (or transferred directly to retained earnings, depending on the nature of the OCI item) on the same basis as would apply if the parent had directly disposed of the related assets/liabilities.
  • Any retained interest is remeasured at fair value on the date control is lost, and that fair value becomes its new cost basis (for subsequent equity-method or Ind AS 109 accounting).
  • Concept: Loss of control is treated as a significant re-measurement event — the whole of the old relationship is "closed out" at fair value.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: On partial disposal without loss of control, only a proportionate share of cumulative OCI (e.g., FCTR) is re-attributed to NCI (not derecognised); on full loss of control, the entire cumulative OCI balance is derecognised/reclassified to P&L — except the portion already attributed to NCI, which is simply derecognised, not reclassified to P&L.

PART B — IND AS 27: SEPARATE FINANCIAL STATEMENTS (SFS)

Meaning & Purpose of SFS

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • SFS are those presented by a parent (investor in an associate/JV) in which investments are accounted for on the basis of the direct equity interest, rather than on the basis of the investees' reported results and net assets (i.e., not consolidated, not equity-accounted).
  • SFS are presented in addition to CFS (or FS where the investor has no subsidiary but has associates/JVs required to use equity method), never as a substitute for them, except where the exemption in Ind AS 110/Ind AS 28 applies.
  • Concept: SFS answer "what did the parent itself invest and what is it worth on the parent's own books," as distinct from CFS's "group as a single economic entity" view.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: SFS are optional in many jurisdictions but where prepared under Ind AS, Ind AS 27 governs them; do not confuse SFS with "standalone" financial statements of an entity with no subsidiaries/associates/JVs at all (the latter are simply that entity's only FS, not "separate" FS in the Ind AS 27 sense).

Measurement of Investments in Subsidiaries, Associates & JVs (in SFS)

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): In its SFS, an entity accounts for investments in subsidiaries, joint ventures and associates using one of:
  • Cost (purchase price + directly attributable transaction costs); or
  • In accordance with Ind AS 109 — i.e., at fair value, with changes either through P&L (FVTPL) or, for equity instruments, through OCI (FVOCI, via irrevocable election).
  • The entity must apply the same accounting policy for each category of investment (all subsidiaries together, all associates together, all JVs together) — though it may choose a different policy across categories (e.g., cost for subsidiaries, FVTPL for associates).
  • Investments accounted for at cost that meet held-for-sale criteria are accounted for under Ind AS 105 instead.
  • Concept: Unlike full IFRS/IAS 27 (which also permits the equity method in SFS), Ind AS 27 does NOT permit the equity method for SFS — only cost or Ind AS 109 fair value — because the equity method is viewed as a consolidation technique, not a measurement basis.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: This "no equity method in SFS" point is a favourite examiner distinction between Ind AS and IFRS — do not import the IFRS option into an Ind AS answer.

Dividend Recognition in SFS

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Dividend Income (in SFS) = Recognised in P&L when the investor's right to receive payment is established

  • Concept: Dividend recognition in SFS is not linked to whether the dividend is paid out of pre- or post-acquisition profits of the investee (that pre/post-acquisition split matters for CFS/equity-method workings, not for SFS dividend recognition).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: ⚠️ VERIFY — where a dividend received from a subsidiary/JV/associate exceeds the investee's total comprehensive income for the period the dividend relates to, or where the carrying amount of the investment in SFS exceeds the carrying amount of the investee's net assets (including attributable goodwill), this is an indicator that the investment may be impaired, requiring an Ind AS 36 impairment assessment (amendment aligning Ind AS 27/Ind AS 36/Ind AS 109 dividend-impairment interaction).

PART C — IND AS 112: DISCLOSURE OF INTERESTS IN OTHER ENTITIES

Objective & Scope

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):
  • Objective: require disclosures that enable users to evaluate (a) the nature of, and risks associated with, an entity's interests in other entities, and (b) the effects of those interests on its financial position, financial performance and cash flows.
  • Scope: applies to entities with an interest in subsidiaries, joint arrangements (joint operations and joint ventures), associates, and unconsolidated structured entities.
  • Concept: Ind AS 112 is purely a disclosure standard — it does not change recognition or measurement (which remain governed by Ind AS 110, Ind AS 111, Ind AS 28, Ind AS 109).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Ind AS 112 disclosures are required in CFS primarily, but an investment entity or an entity with only associates/JVs (no subsidiaries) also applies it in its own (single set of) FS.

Significant Judgements & Assumptions

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): An entity must disclose the significant judgements and assumptions made (and changes therein) in determining:
  • That it controls another entity (or does not, despite holding less/more than half the voting rights);
  • The type of joint arrangement (joint operation vs joint venture) in which it has an interest, where structured through a separate vehicle;
  • That it has significant influence over another entity; AND
  • That it meets the definition of an investment entity, and, if so, the reasons why it does not have all the typical characteristics of one.
  • Concept: These judgement disclosures are where the "control/significant influence" reasoning from Ind AS 110/Ind AS 28 is made transparent to users.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Do not skip this section for "obvious" majority-owned subsidiaries — disclosure is required whenever judgement was genuinely exercised (e.g., de facto control cases, potential voting rights, agent/principal calls).

Disclosures — Subsidiaries

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): Disclose, for each subsidiary with material NCI:
  • Name, principal place of business/country of incorporation, proportion of ownership/voting rights held by NCI;
  • Profit or loss allocated to NCI, and NCI's accumulated balance, for the period/at year-end;
  • Summarised financial information about the subsidiary (assets, liabilities, revenue, profit/loss, cash flows);
  • The nature and extent of significant restrictions on the parent's ability to access or use group assets and settle group liabilities (e.g., regulatory/currency-control restrictions on transferring cash or assets between group members);
  • Nature of risks associated with interests in consolidated structured entities;
  • Consequences of changes in ownership interest (with and without loss of control) — the gain/loss and its line item, as applicable.
  • Concept: Purpose is to let a reader of the CFS understand how much of the group's assets/profits are actually attributable to outside (non-controlling) parties, and whether group cash/assets are truly fungible across entities.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: "Material NCI" is a judgement threshold set by the entity itself — Ind AS 112 does not prescribe a numeric %; disclose based on materiality to users of the CFS.

Disclosures — Joint Ventures & Associates

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): For each JV/associate that is material to the reporting entity, disclose:
  • Name, nature of relationship, principal place of business, proportion of ownership/voting rights held;
  • Whether the investment is measured using the equity method or at fair value;
  • Summarised financial information (assets, liabilities, revenue, profit/loss) sufficient to reconcile to the carrying amount of the interest;
  • Fair value of the interest, if quoted; nature of risks; unrecognised share of losses (equity method suspended once carrying amount is nil); commitments and contingent liabilities relating to the JV/associate.
  • Concept: These disclosures let users see through the single "investment in associate/JV" line in the balance sheet to the underlying scale and risk of that investee.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Individually immaterial JVs/associates can be aggregated for disclosure purposes rather than disclosed entity-by-entity.

Disclosures — Unconsolidated Structured Entities

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s): For structured entities the reporting entity has an interest in but does not consolidate (e.g., sponsored securitisation vehicles, certain funds), disclose:
  • Nature and extent of the interest, including the carrying amounts of assets and liabilities recognised relating to that interest;
  • The entity's maximum exposure to loss from the interest and how it is determined;
  • A comparison of carrying amounts vs maximum exposure to loss;
  • Any support (financial or other) provided to the structured entity without a contractual obligation to do so, and any current intention to provide such support.
  • Definitions:
TermMeaning
Structured entityAn entity designed so that voting or similar rights are not the dominant factor in deciding who controls it (e.g., control is driven by contractual arrangements)
Unconsolidated structured entityA structured entity the reporting entity has an interest in but does not control (and therefore does not consolidate)
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Even where an entity has no power/control over a structured entity, disclosure is still required if it has any interest in it (e.g., holds notes issued by a securitisation SPV it sponsors) — this is the classic "shadow banking transparency" objective behind Ind AS 112.

17. Ind AS 111 & Ind AS 28 - Joint Arrangements and Investments in Associates & Joint Ventures

Where it fits: After Ind AS 110 (control-based consolidation), this chapter covers the two intermediate levels of influence over another entity — joint control (Ind AS 111, classified further into Joint Operation/Joint Venture) and significant influence (Ind AS 28, always equity accounted) — both examinable heavily in the Consolidated Financial Statements numerical.

⚠️ Source note: The primary OCR range assigned to this chapter (lines 2431-2657 of the source concept book) actually contains Ind AS 33 (EPS) and Ind AS 34 (Interim Reporting) material — the book does not carry a dedicated Ind AS 111/28 section in the scanned file. The content below has therefore been rebuilt entirely from verified authoritative sources (see References) rather than the OCR, since none was available to correct.

Scope & Relationship Between the Standards

  • Concept: Ind AS 111 tells you what kind of joint arrangement you have (Joint Operation vs Joint Venture) and how to account for each. Ind AS 28 tells you how to apply the equity method — used both for Joint Ventures (under Ind AS 111) and for Associates (entities over which you have significant influence, but not control or joint control).
  • Key rule(s):
  • Joint Arrangement = an arrangement of which two or more parties have joint control.
  • Joint control = the contractually agreed sharing of control of an arrangement, which exists only when decisions about the relevant activities require the unanimous consent of the parties sharing control.
  • A Joint Arrangement is classified as either a Joint Operation or a Joint Venture (Ind AS 111); a Joint Venture and an Associate are both accounted for using the equity method (Ind AS 28).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Joint control ≠ 50:50 holding. Even a 60:40 arrangement can have joint control if the contract requires unanimous consent of relevant parties on relevant activities.
  • Do not confuse "joint venture" as a legal/company-law term with "Joint Venture" as an Ind AS 111 classification — classification depends on rights & obligations, not the entity's name or legal form alone.

Ind AS 28 — Significant Influence

  • Key rule(s):
  • Significant influence is presumed if the investor holds, directly or indirectly (e.g. through subsidiaries), 20% or more of the voting power of the investee — unless it can be clearly demonstrated that this is not the case.
  • Conversely, if the investor holds less than 20%, it is presumed the investor does not have significant influence, unless it can be clearly demonstrated otherwise.
  • Potential voting rights (e.g. share warrants, convertible instruments) that are currently exercisable or convertible must be considered when assessing significant influence.
  • Existence of significant influence is usually evidenced by one or more of:
  • Representation on the board of directors (or equivalent governing body);
  • Participation in policy-making processes, including participation in decisions about dividends;
  • Material transactions between the entity and its investee;
  • Interchange of managerial personnel; or
  • Provision of essential technical information.
  • Concept: Significant influence is a rebuttable-presumption test (like control's "more than 50%" heuristic in Ind AS 110), never a hard bright line — the qualitative indicators can override the 20% mechanical test in either direction.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Loss of significant influence can happen even without any change in absolute or relative ownership — e.g. if the investee becomes subject to government/court/regulatory control.
  • A holding of 20% or more, contractually surrendered along with all board/decision rights, may not amount to significant influence — always test facts, not just %.

Ind AS 28 — Equity Method: Initial & Subsequent Measurement

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Carrying Amount at year-end = Cost of Investment (incl. goodwill component) + Investor's share of post-acquisition Profit/Loss of Investee + Investor's share of post-acquisition OCI of Investee - Dividends received/receivable - Impairment losses recognised

Goodwill (included within carrying amount, not separately recognised) = Cost of Investment - Investor's share of net fair value of Investee's identifiable assets, liabilities & contingent liabilities at acquisition

  • If the above difference is negative (bargain purchase), the excess is recognised as income in profit or loss in the period the investment is acquired.
  • Goodwill embedded in the equity-accounted carrying amount is never amortised and never tested for impairment separately — the entire investment is tested as a single asset under Ind AS 36 (see below).
  • On acquisition, the investee's identifiable assets/liabilities are adjusted to fair value (as in a business combination) purely for computing the investor's share of profit/loss going forward (e.g. extra depreciation on FV step-up of investee's PPE).
  • Concept: Equity method is a "one-line consolidation" — the investment account moves each year to track the investor's proportionate share of what has happened inside the investee (profits added, losses/dividends deducted), instead of line-by-line consolidation of assets and liabilities.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Dividends received reduce the carrying amount of the investment (return of investment); they are not recognised as investment income under equity method.
  • Preference dividends of the associate (if held by outside parties) must first be deducted before computing the investor's share of ordinary profit.
  • Different reporting dates/accounting policies of the investee should be adjusted (aligned) before applying equity method, where practicable; the gap between reporting dates should not exceed 3 months.

Ind AS 28 — Losses in Excess of Interest

  • Key rule(s):
  • If the entity's share of losses of the associate/JV equals or exceeds its interest in the associate/JV (i.e. carrying amount of investment plus any long-term interests forming part of the net investment, e.g. long-term receivables), the entity discontinues recognising its share of further losses.
  • Long-term interests that are, in substance, part of the net investment (e.g. preference shares, long-term loans/receivables not expected to be settled in the foreseeable future) are reduced to zero in reverse order of seniority (i.e. priority in liquidation) before the equity investment itself is reduced.
  • After the interest is reduced to zero, additional losses are recognised as a provision/liability only to the extent the entity has incurred legal or constructive obligations, or made payments, on behalf of the associate/JV.
  • If the associate/JV subsequently reports profits, the entity resumes recognising its share of profits only after its share of profits equals the share of losses not previously recognised.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • A common trick in numericals: check whether cumulative losses allocated exceed the opening carrying amount before blindly applying the investor's % share — cap at zero (plus long-term interests) and carry forward the unabsorbed loss memorandum for future profit resumption.

Ind AS 28 — Impairment

  • Key rule(s):
  • After applying the equity method (including recognising the associate's/JV's losses), the entity applies Ind AS 109 to determine whether there is objective evidence of impairment.
  • If evidence of impairment exists, the entire carrying amount of the investment (including any embedded goodwill) is tested for impairment under Ind AS 36 as a single asset, comparing its recoverable amount (higher of value in use and fair value less costs of disposal) with its carrying amount.
  • Because goodwill within the investment is not separated out, any impairment loss recognised is not allocated to any asset (including goodwill) that forms part of the carrying amount — and impairment losses recognised in prior periods can be reversed to the extent recoverable amount subsequently increases (unlike goodwill impairment in a subsidiary, which is never reversed).
  • Concept: Because the investment is a single financial-statement-line asset, Ind AS 28's impairment approach deliberately differs from Ind AS 36's normal treatment of goodwill in a business combination (no separate goodwill impairment test, and reversal is permitted).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: A frequent conceptual MCQ trap — students assume goodwill impairment on an associate is never reversible (true for subsidiaries under Ind AS 36, false for associates/JVs under Ind AS 28).

Ind AS 28 — Upstream & Downstream Transactions

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Unrealised Profit to be Eliminated = Investor's % Holding in Associate/JV × Unrealised Profit on Intra-group Transaction (still in closing inventory/asset)

  • Downstream transaction: sale from investor to associate/JV. Upstream transaction: sale from associate/JV to investor.
  • Unrealised profits/losses on both upstream and downstream transactions are eliminated only to the extent of the investor's interest in the associate/JV (unlike full elimination in subsidiary consolidation under Ind AS 110).
  • Losses on such transactions are eliminated in the same way, unless the transaction provides evidence of an impairment of the asset transferred (in which case the full loss is recognised).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Do not eliminate 100% of the profit as done for subsidiaries — partial elimination (investor's % only) is a hallmark distinguishing associate/JV accounting from full consolidation.

Ind AS 28 — Discontinuing the Equity Method / Loss of Significant Influence or Joint Control

  • Key rule(s):
  • On the date an entity loses significant influence over an associate (or joint control over a JV), it:
  • Measures any retained investment at its fair value on that date;
  • Recognises in profit or loss the difference between (a) the fair value of any retained investment plus proceeds from disposal, and (b) the carrying amount of the investment at the date significant influence/joint control is lost;
  • Reclassifies to profit or loss (or transfers directly to retained earnings, if the standard so requires) all amounts previously recognised in OCI in relation to that associate/JV, on the same basis as would be required if the associate/JV had directly disposed of the related assets or liabilities.
  • If significant influence is reduced but retained (partial disposal), only a proportionate amount of OCI previously recognised is reclassified to profit or loss.
  • If an investment ceases to be an associate/JV but the retained interest is a financial asset, it is subsequently accounted for under Ind AS 109 from that date.
  • Equity method is not applied to a portion of an investment in an associate/JV that is classified as held for sale under Ind AS 105 — that portion is measured under Ind AS 105 instead.
  • Concept: The change is treated economically like a disposal and reacquisition (fair value "reset"), not a mere continuation of equity accounting.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: If an associate becomes a JV (or vice versa) without any loss of significant influence, the entity continues applying the equity method without any remeasurement to fair value.

Ind AS 28 — Exemptions from Equity Method

  • Key rule(s): An entity need not apply the equity method to its investment in an associate/JV if the entity is:
  • A venture capital organisation, or a mutual fund, unit trust or similar entity (including investment-linked insurance funds), that elects to measure the investment at fair value through profit or loss (FVTPL) under Ind AS 109; or
  • Itself a subsidiary (wholly or partially owned) and meets the same conditions as the exemption from preparing consolidated financial statements under Ind AS 110 (e.g. its owners, including non-controlling owners, do not object; not in the process of, or listed on, a stock exchange; ultimate/intermediate parent produces IFRS/Ind AS-compliant consolidated financial statements available for public use).
  • Concept: These are the same "no public accountability" style carve-outs seen in Ind AS 110 for CFS exemption — testable as a cross-reference point between the two standards.

Ind AS 111 — Classification of a Joint Arrangement (Structure Test)

  • Key rule(s) — decision sequence to classify as Joint Operation vs Joint Venture:
  1. Step 1 — Structured through a separate vehicle? If No (arrangement not structured through a separate legal entity/vehicle), it is automatically a Joint Operation.
  2. Step 2 — Legal form of the separate vehicle: If the legal form of the separate vehicle gives the parties rights to the assets and obligations for the liabilities of the arrangement (e.g. an unincorporated partnership/trust in some jurisdictions), it is a Joint Operation.
  3. Step 3 — Terms of the contractual arrangement: If the contract specifically states the parties have rights to the assets and obligations for the liabilities (overriding the separate legal form), it is a Joint Operation.
  4. Step 4 — Other facts and circumstances: Even where the vehicle's legal form and contract terms suggest net assets rights, if other facts show the parties have rights to substantially all the economic benefits of the vehicle's assets, and the arrangement is designed so that its liabilities will effectively be settled by cash flows received from the parties (e.g. parties obligated to purchase substantially all output at a price set to cover costs and financing, i.e. "take or pay" style arrangements), it is a Joint Operation.
  • If none of the above indicates otherwise, the arrangement is a Joint Venture.
  • Concept: Classification is driven by the parties' rights and obligations, not by the name given to the arrangement or by percentage ownership.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: A jointly controlled entity incorporated as a private limited company (separate legal personality, shareholders not personally liable for its debts) will normally default to Joint Venture classification via Step 2 — unless Step 3 (contract) or Step 4 (substance, e.g. mandatory off-take agreements) overrides it. Exam numericals often plant a "parties must buy 100% of output at cost" clause specifically to flip an apparently separate-vehicle JV into a Joint Operation.

Ind AS 111 — Accounting for Joint Operations vs Joint Ventures

  • Key rule(s):
  • A joint operator (and, per the standard, a party that participates in but does not have joint control of a joint operation, if it has rights to assets/obligations for liabilities of that joint operation) recognises, in relation to its interest in the joint operation:
  • its own assets, including its share of any assets held jointly;
  • its own liabilities, including its share of any liabilities incurred jointly;
  • its revenue from the sale of its share of the output arising from the joint operation;
  • its share of the revenue from the sale of the output by the joint operation; and
  • its own expenses, including its share of any expenses incurred jointly.
  • This is effectively a line-by-line (proportionate) recognition of assets/liabilities/income/expenses based on the operator's contractual share — applied directly in both separate and consolidated financial statements (it is not "proportionate consolidation" in the pre-2011 sense, but the numerical mechanics are similar).
  • A joint venturer accounts for its interest in the joint venture using the equity method in accordance with Ind AS 28 (both in consolidated financial statements, and — unless the cost/Ind AS 109 option under Ind AS 27 is used — potentially in separate financial statements too).
  • Concept: Joint Operation accounting mirrors direct ownership of a share of assets/activities (no separate "investment" line-item); Joint Venture accounting mirrors Associate accounting (single equity-accounted investment line).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Do not equity-account a Joint Operation — recognise its share of each asset/liability/revenue/expense directly; equity accounting applies only to Joint Ventures (and Associates).
  • A party without joint control can still apply Joint Operation-style line-by-line accounting if it has rights to assets/obligations for liabilities under the arrangement — joint control is not a precondition for this treatment for such a party.

18. Ind AS 32, 107 & 109 - Financial Instruments

Where it fits: Ind AS 32 tells you how to present a financial instrument (liability vs. equity), Ind AS 109 tells you how to classify, measure, impair and hedge it, and Ind AS 107 tells you what to disclose about the resulting risk exposure — together they are the largest, most calculation-heavy chapter in FR and a near-certain 12-16 mark question.

1. Overview & Scope

  • Key rule(s):
  • Ind AS 32 — Presentation: classification as financial liability / equity; compound instruments; treasury shares; offsetting.
  • Ind AS 109 — Recognition & Measurement: classification, initial/subsequent measurement, impairment (ECL), derecognition, hedge accounting.
  • Ind AS 107 — Disclosures: significance of financial instruments, nature/extent of risks (credit, liquidity, market) and qualitative + quantitative disclosures.
  • Concept: A financial instrument is any contract that gives rise to a financial asset of one entity and a financial liability or equity instrument of another entity.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Scope exclusions to remember: interests in subsidiaries/associates/JVs (Ind AS 110/28/111, unless designated at FVTPL), employee benefit plan rights (Ind AS 19), share-based payments (Ind AS 102), insurance contracts (Ind AS 104/117), and most leases (Ind AS 116) except specified items — a favourite "identify the standard" MCQ trap.
  • Definitions:
TermMeaning
Financial assetCash; equity instrument of another entity; contractual right to receive cash/another financial asset or to exchange financial instruments on potentially favourable terms
Financial liabilityContractual obligation to deliver cash/another financial asset, or to exchange financial instruments on potentially unfavourable terms
Equity instrumentAny contract evidencing a residual interest in the assets of an entity after deducting all its liabilities

2. Ind AS 32 – Liability vs. Equity Classification

  • Key rule(s):
  • An instrument is a financial liability if the issuer has a contractual obligation to deliver cash/another financial asset, or to exchange instruments under potentially unfavourable conditions — substance over legal form governs.
  • Fixed-for-fixed test: a contract to be settled in the entity's own equity instruments is equity only if it will be settled by delivering a fixed number of own equity shares for a fixed amount of cash ("fixed-for-fixed"). Any variability in either the number of shares or the cash amount generally makes it a liability (or derivative).
  • Preference shares that are mandatorily redeemable (or redeemable at the holder's option) are classified as a financial liability even though legally called "shares"; dividends thereon are treated as interest expense (finance cost), not a distribution.
  • Puttable instruments exception: a puttable instrument entitling the holder to a pro-rata share of net assets on liquidation is classified as equity if it meets a specified set of conditions (subordinate to all classes, entitles pro-rata share of net assets, no other liability features, etc.).
  • Concept: Classification drives P&L — a "liability" preference share's dividend hits finance cost (reducing reported profit); an "equity" instrument's dividend is a distribution of profit (below the line).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Contingent settlement provisions (e.g., "redeemable only on IPO not happening") make the instrument a liability unless the contingency is not genuine or the entity can never be required to settle in cash.
  • Classification is done at initial recognition and, other than in narrow circumstances, is not revisited for changed circumstances.

3. Compound Financial Instruments & Treasury Shares

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Equity Component = Fair Value of Instrument as a whole (proceeds) - Liability Component

  • Liability component = present value of the contractual future cash flows (interest + principal), discounted at the market interest rate for an equivalent non-convertible instrument (i.e., a similar instrument with no conversion option), at issue date.
  • The split is made once, at inception, and is never revisited thereafter, even if the option is never exercised or lapses (in which case the amount stays in equity, sometimes reclassified within equity but not to P&L).
  • Treasury shares (own equity shares reacquired): deducted from equity at cost; no gain or loss is recognised in P&L on purchase, sale, issue or cancellation of own equity instruments.
  • Concept: A compound instrument (e.g., convertible debentures) has both a debt-like element (obligation to pay interest/principal) and an equity-like element (option to convert into a fixed number of shares) — Ind AS 32 requires split accounting.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Residual method: compute the liability component first (at market rate for a plain debt instrument), then equity = balancing figure — never estimate equity first.
  • Transaction costs of issue are apportioned between the two components in proportion to the allocation of proceeds.

4. Offsetting Financial Assets and Financial Liabilities

  • Key rule(s): A financial asset and a financial liability shall be offset and the net amount presented in the balance sheet only when, and only when, the entity:
  • (a) currently has a legally enforceable right to set off the recognised amounts; AND
  • (b) intends either to settle on a net basis, or to realise the asset and settle the liability simultaneously.
  • Concept: Offsetting is the exception, not the rule — merely having a master netting agreement without the intention to settle net does not justify offset presentation.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: The right of set-off must not be contingent on a future event (e.g., default of counterparty) — a right that only crystallises on default of the other party does not meet condition (a) for offsetting in the normal course of business.

5. Ind AS 109 – Classification of Financial Assets

  • Key rule(s) — two tests decide the category:
  • Business Model Test: how the entity manages the asset to generate cash flows — (i) collect contractual cash flows only, (ii) collect and sell, or (iii) other (e.g., trading).
  • SPPI Test (Contractual Cash Flow Characteristics): whether contractual cash flows are Solely Payments of Principal and Interest on the principal amount outstanding (interest = time value of money + credit risk + other basic lending risks/costs + profit margin).
  • Classification outcomes:
Business ModelSPPI met?Category
Hold to collectYesAmortised Cost
Hold to collect and sellYesFVOCI (debt instrument) — recycled to P&L on sale
Other / trading—FVTPL
AnyNoFVTPL (mandatorily, regardless of business model)
  • Equity instruments: default classification is FVTPL; an entity may make an irrevocable election at initial recognition, instrument-by-instrument, to present fair value changes in OCI (FVOCI – equity), with no recycling to P&L even on sale (only dividend income hits P&L).
  • Concept: Classification of debt instruments = intersection of "why do I hold it" (business model) and "what do the cash flows look like" (SPPI); classification is done instrument-by-instrument at initial recognition.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • If the SPPI test fails (e.g., convertible bond, cash flows linked to an equity index), the asset goes to FVTPL irrespective of business model — check SPPI first in most exam shortcuts.
  • Reclassification of financial assets is permitted only when the entity changes its business model for managing those assets — expected to be very infrequent; reclassification is prospective from the reclassification date. Financial liabilities are never reclassified.

6. Ind AS 109 – Classification & Measurement of Financial Liabilities

  • Key rule(s):
  • Default category: Amortised Cost using the effective interest method.
  • FVTPL applies to: (a) liabilities held for trading (including derivatives), and (b) liabilities designated at FVTPL under the fair value option — an irrevocable election at initial recognition, allowed only if it eliminates or significantly reduces an accounting mismatch, or where a group is managed on a fair value basis.
  • Own credit risk: for liabilities designated at FVTPL, the change in fair value attributable to the entity's own credit risk is presented in OCI (not P&L), unless this creates/enlarges an accounting mismatch in P&L.
  • Concept: Unlike assets, liabilities are amortised-cost by default; FVTPL is the exception, chosen only to avoid mismatches.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Financial guarantee contracts and loan commitments below market rate have specific subsequent measurement rules (higher of loss allowance and amount initially recognised less cumulative income) — don't default them to plain amortised cost.

7. Initial & Subsequent Measurement — Effective Interest Rate (EIR) / Amortised Cost

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

Initial Measurement = Fair Value ± Transaction Costs (unless FVTPL, expensed)

P0 = Σt=1n CFt(1+EIR)t

where P0 = initial gross carrying amount (proceeds), CFt = estimated future contractual cash flows (interest + principal), and EIR is the rate that exactly discounts those cash flows to P0.

Amortised Cost (closing) = Amortised Cost (opening) + (EIR × Opening Amortised Cost) - Cash Received/Paid - Impairment

  • Concept: The amortised cost/EIR method spreads transaction costs, premiums/discounts, and fees over the instrument's life so that the effective yield (not the coupon rate) is recognised as interest income/expense each period.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • For variable-rate instruments, periodic re-estimation of cash flows generally adjusts the carrying amount with the adjustment recognised in P&L (catch-up approach), rather than a full re-calculation of EIR (subject to specific guidance for floating-rate instruments).
  • Transaction costs are capitalised into the initial carrying amount for amortised-cost/FVOCI instruments but expensed immediately for FVTPL instruments.

8. Impairment — Expected Credit Loss (ECL) Model

  • Key formula(s)/rule(s):

ECL = Σ ≤ft[ Probability of Default (PD) × Loss Given Default (LGD) × Exposure at Default (EAD) ] discounted to reporting date

  • General (3-stage) approach, applied to loans, debt instruments at amortised cost/FVOCI, most receivables not covered by the simplified approach:
StageTriggerLoss allowanceInterest income basis
Stage 1Initial recognition; no significant increase in credit risk (SICR)12-month ECLGross carrying amount
Stage 2SICR since initial recognition, not yet credit-impairedLifetime ECLGross carrying amount
Stage 3Credit-impaired (objective evidence of default/loss event)Lifetime ECLNet (amortised cost) carrying amount
  • Simplified approach (mandatory) for trade receivables and contract assets (Ind AS 115) with no significant financing component: always recognise lifetime ECL from initial recognition — no need to track SICR.
  • Simplified approach (optional, accounting policy choice) available for trade receivables/contract assets with a significant financing component, and for lease receivables (Ind AS 116).
  • Low credit risk exemption: if credit risk is assessed as low (broadly, "investment grade" equivalent) at the reporting date, an entity may recognise 12-month ECL without formally assessing SICR each period.
  • Concept: Unlike the old "incurred loss" model, Ind AS 109 requires a forward-looking, probability-weighted loss estimate to be recognised from day one (12-month ECL), not just when a default has already happened.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Trade receivable practical expedient: a provision matrix based on historical default rates, adjusted for forward-looking factors, is commonly used and is examinable via ageing schedules.
  • PD × LGD × EAD is the standard mnemonic; ECL must be discounted using the original EIR (or an approximation) when the time value of money is material.

9. Derecognition — Financial Assets & Financial Liabilities

  • Key rule(s)/formula(s) — Financial Asset:
  • Derecognise when: (a) contractual rights to cash flows expire, OR (b) the asset is transferred and substantially all risks and rewards of ownership are transferred; if risks/rewards are partly retained/transferred, derecognise only if control has also passed.
  • Financial Liability — 10% test for modifications: a modification/exchange of debt is treated as an extinguishment (derecognise old, recognise new liability at fair value) if the terms are substantially different, tested as:

≤ft| PV(cash flows under new terms) - PV(remaining cash flows of original liability)PV(remaining cash flows of original liability) | ≥ 10%

(both PVs discounted at the original effective interest rate). If the 10% threshold is not met, the liability continues, and a catch-up adjustment is made to the carrying amount through P&L (no new EIR is calculated, unless it's a floating-rate case), and costs/fees incurred are amortised over the remaining term.

  • Concept: A liability is only removed from the books when it is legally extinguished (discharged, cancelled, expired) or substantially modified — a simple restructuring of interest rate/tenor below the 10% threshold is not an extinguishment.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Fees paid to the same lender in a non-substantial modification are deducted from the carrying amount of the liability (amortised over remaining term); fees paid to third parties are expensed immediately.
  • For a substantial modification (extinguishment), any costs/fees are recognised as part of the gain/loss on extinguishment.

10. Embedded Derivatives

  • Key rule(s): For a hybrid contract with a host that is NOT a financial asset within the scope of Ind AS 109 (e.g., a lease, insurance contract, or an ordinary purchase contract), an embedded derivative is separated from the host and accounted for as a standalone derivative only if all three conditions are met:
  • (a) the economic characteristics/risks of the embedded derivative are not closely related to those of the host contract;
  • (b) a separate instrument with the same terms as the embedded feature would meet the definition of a derivative; and
  • (c) the hybrid (combined) instrument is not already measured at FVTPL in its entirety.
  • If the host is a financial asset within Ind AS 109's scope, no bifurcation is performed — the entire hybrid contract is classified (amortised cost/FVOCI/FVTPL) as a whole using the SPPI/business model tests.
  • Concept: Embedded derivative rules matter mainly for financial liabilities and non-financial host contracts (e.g., a foreign-currency-indexed lease payment, or a convertible bond viewed from the issuer's side).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: A common exam example is a foreign currency embedded derivative in a purchase/sale contract not denominated in the functional currency of either party — this typically requires separation.

11. Hedge Accounting

  • Key rule(s) — three types of hedging relationships:
Hedge typeHedgesEffective portion recognised in
Fair Value HedgeExposure to changes in FV of a recognised asset/liability or unrecognised firm commitmentP&L (both hedged item and hedging instrument re-measured through P&L)
Cash Flow HedgeExposure to variability in cash flows of a recognised item or highly probable forecast transactionOCI (effective portion); ineffective portion to P&L
Net Investment HedgeFX risk on a net investment in a foreign operationOCI (effective portion), recycled to P&L on disposal of the foreign operation
  • Hedge effectiveness — qualifying criteria (all three required, assessed prospectively, no more 80–125% bright-line retrospective test):
  1. There is an economic relationship between the hedged item and hedging instrument;
  2. The effect of credit risk does not dominate the value changes resulting from that economic relationship;
  3. The hedge ratio designated equals the ratio actually used for risk management purposes (no artificial "rebalancing" to create accounting results).
  • Concept: Hedge accounting is optional and matches the timing of gain/loss recognition on the hedging instrument with that of the hedged item to reduce P&L volatility from an economic hedge.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Ind AS 109 dropped the rigid 80–125% effectiveness corridor used earlier under Ind AS 39 — effectiveness is assessed qualitatively/quantitatively on an ongoing (prospective) basis, with rebalancing of the hedge ratio permitted without discontinuing the relationship.
  • On discontinuation of a cash flow hedge, the cumulative gain/loss in the cash flow hedge reserve stays in OCI until the forecast transaction occurs (or is reclassified immediately to P&L if the transaction is no longer expected to occur).

12. Ind AS 107 — Risk Disclosures & Fair Value Hierarchy

  • Key rule(s):
  • Disclosures must cover the significance of financial instruments for financial position/performance, and the nature and extent of risks arising from them, split into:
  • Credit risk — including maximum exposure, collateral held, credit quality, and ECL reconciliation;
  • Liquidity risk — including a maturity analysis of financial liabilities (undiscounted contractual cash flows) by remaining time bands;
  • Market risk — interest rate risk, currency risk, and other price risk, each with a sensitivity analysis showing the effect on P&L/equity of a reasonably possible change in the relevant risk variable.
  • Fair value hierarchy (per Ind AS 113, cross-referenced for Ind AS 107 disclosures):
LevelInput basis
Level 1Quoted prices (unadjusted) in active markets for identical assets/liabilities
Level 2Inputs other than quoted prices in Level 1 that are observable (directly or indirectly)
Level 3Unobservable inputs for the asset/liability
  • Concept: Ind AS 107 is a disclosure-only standard — it does not change recognition or measurement, but examiners frequently test the fair value hierarchy classification and the credit/liquidity/market risk categorisation of a given exposure.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: A quoted bond price that is stale/infrequently traded, or a price derived using a valuation model calibrated mostly to observable market inputs, is typically Level 2, not Level 1 — a common trick in MCQs.

1. Why Technology Matters for Accountants

  • Key rule(s):
  • Financial reporting technology spans the full cycle: data capture → processing/recording → analysis → reporting/filing → assurance, and each stage now has digital tooling (ERP, analytics, XBRL, blockchain, RPA/AI, continuous audit).
  • ICAI's stated rationale: technology increases speed, accuracy, comparability (via standardised tagging) and real-time availability of financial information to regulators, investors and management.
  • Concept: The chapter is an overview/appreciation topic — examiners test understanding of what a technology is, why it is used in accounting/reporting, and its benefits vs limitations, not computations.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Descriptive answers only — no journal entries or numericals in this chapter.
  • Keep answers to benefits / limitations / applications in bullet form for exam presentation marks.

2. XBRL (eXtensible Business Reporting Language)

  • Key rule(s) — mandatory e-filing in XBRL (Companies Act, 2013 read with the Companies (Filing of Documents and Forms in XBRL) Rules, as amended): the following classes of companies must file specified financial statements/documents (Forms AOC-4 XBRL) with the Registrar in XBRL format:
  • All companies listed on a stock exchange in India and their Indian subsidiaries; or
  • Companies having paid-up capital ≥ ₹5 crore; or
  • Companies having turnover ≥ ₹100 crore; or
  • All companies required to prepare financial statements as per the Companies (Indian Accounting Standards) Rules, 2015 (i.e., Ind AS-applicable companies).
  • Banking, insurance, power, and non-banking financial companies are generally exempt from this specific XBRL filing requirement (they file under sector-specific formats), unless separately notified.
  • Concept: XBRL is a structured, XML-based "language" for tagging each line item of a financial statement with a unique, machine-readable tag (from a taxonomy) so that data can be extracted, compared, and analysed by software without manual re-keying.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips:
  • Distinguish "taxonomy" (the standard dictionary of tags/elements, e.g., Ind AS Taxonomy notified by MCA) from an "instance document" (the actual tagged financial statement of a specific company for a specific period).
  • Applicability is tested on any one of the four criteria being met — it is not cumulative ("and"); satisfying any one limb triggers the requirement.
  • ⚠️ VERIFY — the ₹5 crore / ₹100 crore thresholds and the exempt-sector list are correct as commonly stated, but confirm against the latest Companies (Filing of Documents and Forms in XBRL) Rules notification/amendment in force for the attempt year, since MCA periodically revises these rules and the applicable taxonomy version.
  • Definitions:
TermMeaning

TaggingThe process of mapping each financial statement line item to the correct taxonomy element

3. Data Analytics & Business Intelligence (BI) in Accounting

  • Key rule(s) — the four recognised types of analytics:
  • Descriptive analytics – "what happened?" (dashboards, MIS, trend/ratio reports)
  • Diagnostic analytics – "why did it happen?" (variance analysis, drill-down)
  • Predictive analytics – "what is likely to happen?" (forecasting, credit-risk/going-concern scoring)
  • Prescriptive analytics – "what should be done?" (recommended actions/optimisation)
  • Concept: Analytics tools convert large volumes of transactional/financial data into decision-useful insight, supporting management reporting, budgeting, fraud detection and audit sampling (e.g., 100% population testing instead of sample testing).
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Learn the four-type classification verbatim — it is a favourite short-answer/MCQ distinction; do not confuse "diagnostic" (why) with "predictive" (what next).

4. Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Machine Learning in Accounting/Audit

  • Key rule(s)/applications:
  • Automated document/invoice reading and classification (OCR + NLP)
  • Anomaly and fraud detection in journal entries and expense claims
  • Risk-based audit planning — AI-assisted identification of high-risk transactions/areas
  • Chatbots/virtual assistants for routine query resolution
  • Concept: AI enables systems to "learn" from historical accounting data/patterns and improve judgment-support tasks (e.g., flagging unusual entries) without being explicitly reprogrammed for each new pattern.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Exam answers should pair each AI use-case with a limitation (e.g., black-box explainability, data-quality dependence, need for human professional judgement/override) — examiners reward balanced "benefits vs limitations" answers.

5. Robotic Process Automation (RPA)

  • Key rule(s)/characteristics: RPA "bots" mimic human, rule-based, repetitive digital actions (e.g., copying data between systems, reconciliations, invoice matching, GST/TDS return data compilation) — best suited to high-volume, rule-based, low-judgment tasks.
  • Concept: Unlike AI (which learns/adapts), RPA follows pre-defined, deterministic rules; it is process automation, not decision-making intelligence.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: A common exam trap is treating RPA and AI as synonyms — RPA = rule-following automation; AI = learning/judgement-support. State this distinction explicitly if asked to differentiate.

6. Blockchain / Distributed Ledger Technology (DLT) in Accounting

  • Key rule(s)/features:
  • Distributed, shared ledger replicated across a network of participants (no single central custodian)
  • Immutability — once a block is added (via consensus/hashing), it cannot be altered without altering all subsequent blocks
  • Enables the concept of "triple-entry accounting" — each transaction is simultaneously recorded by both counterparties and cryptographically sealed on the shared ledger, supplementing the traditional double-entry system
  • Concept: Blockchain provides a tamper-evident, shared transaction record that can reduce reconciliation effort between counterparties and strengthen audit trail integrity.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Note blockchain's limitations too — scalability/energy cost, regulatory/legal uncertainty in India, and that it changes how transactions are recorded/verified, not the underlying Ind AS recognition/measurement principles.
  • Definitions:
TermMeaning
Distributed ledgerA database of transactions replicated and synchronised across multiple participants/nodes
Consensus mechanismThe protocol by which network participants agree a new block/transaction is valid before it is added
Triple-entry accountingExtension of double-entry where a third, cryptographically-secured shared record corroborates both parties' entries

7. Cloud Computing, ERP & Continuous / Real-Time Reporting

  • Key rule(s)/concepts:
  • ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning) integrates finance, inventory, HR, and operations modules on one common database, reducing duplicate data entry and reconciliation gaps.
  • Cloud computing shifts accounting software/infrastructure to remote, on-demand hosted servers (accessed over the internet) rather than on-premise systems — enabling anywhere-access and easier scalability.
  • Continuous/real-time reporting — technology increasingly allows financial data to be reported closer to the transaction date rather than only at period-end, supporting more timely regulatory and management reporting.
  • Concept: These infrastructure-layer technologies are the "plumbing" that makes XBRL filing, analytics, AI and blockchain applications practically deployable at enterprise scale.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: If a question asks for "benefits of technology in accounting" broadly, structure the answer around this full stack (capture → ERP/cloud → analytics/AI → XBRL filing → blockchain assurance) rather than listing tools without a framework.

8. Cybersecurity, Data Privacy & Control Considerations

  • Key rule(s): Adoption of any of the above technologies must be matched with IT general controls (access control, change management, data backup) and data privacy safeguards, since financial data is now more widely networked and externally accessible.
  • Concept: Examiners expect every "benefits of technology X" answer to be balanced with the corresponding control/risk point — this is consistently how ICAI frames the chapter's evaluative questions.
  • Pitfalls / exam tips: Always close a descriptive answer on any technology topic with at least one risk/limitation/control point — pure "benefits-only" answers lose evaluative marks in this chapter.
